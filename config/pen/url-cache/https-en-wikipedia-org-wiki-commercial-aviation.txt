<figure class="mw-halign-right" typeof="mw:File/Thumb"><a href="/wiki/File:World-airline-routemap-2009.png" class="mw-file-description"><img src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/a/ac/World-airline-routemap-2009.png/286px-World-airline-routemap-2009.png" decoding="async" width="286" height="143" class="mw-file-element" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/a/ac/World-airline-routemap-2009.png/429px-World-airline-routemap-2009.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/a/ac/World-airline-routemap-2009.png/572px-World-airline-routemap-2009.png 2x" data-file-width="2048" data-file-height="1025" /></a><figcaption>Route map of the world's scheduled commercial airline traffic, 2009</figcaption></figure><p><b>Commercial aviation</b> is the part of <a href="/wiki/Civil_aviation" title="Civil aviation">civil aviation</a> that involves operating <a href="/wiki/Aircraft" title="Aircraft">aircraft</a> for remuneration or hire, as opposed to <a href="/wiki/Private_aviation" title="Private aviation">private aviation</a>.
</p><meta property="mw:PageProp/toc" />
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Definition">Definition</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Commercial_aviation&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Definition"><span>edit</span></a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Commercial aviation is not a rigorously defined category. All <a href="/wiki/Airline" title="Airline">commercial air transport</a> and aerial work operations are regarded as commercial aviation, as well as some <a href="/wiki/General_Aviation" class="mw-redirect" title="General Aviation">general aviation</a> flights.
</p><p>Commercial air transport is defined as an aircraft operation involving the transport of passengers, cargo or mail for remuneration or hire. It includes scheduled and non-scheduled air transport operations. Aerial work is defined as an aircraft operation in which an aircraft is used for specialized services such as agriculture, construction, photography, surveying, observation and patrol, search and rescue, advertisement, etc.<sup id="cite_ref-ICAO1_1-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-ICAO1-1">&#91;1&#93;</a></sup> General aviation includes commercial activities such as <a href="/wiki/Flight_training" title="Flight training">flight instruction</a>, aerial work, and corporate and business aviation, as well as non-commercial activities such as recreational flying.
</p><p>Most commercial aviation activities require at minimum a <a href="/wiki/Commercial_pilot_licence" title="Commercial pilot licence">commercial pilot licence</a>, and some require an <a href="/wiki/Airline_transport_pilot_licence" title="Airline transport pilot licence">airline transport pilot licence</a> (ATPL). In the US, the <a href="/wiki/Pilot_in_command" title="Pilot in command">pilot in command</a> of a scheduled air carriers' aircraft must hold an ATPL.<sup id="cite_ref-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-2">&#91;2&#93;</a></sup> In the UK, pilots must hold an ATPL before they be pilot in command of an aircraft with 9 or more passenger seats.<sup id="cite_ref-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-3">&#91;3&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>Not all activities involving pilot remuneration require a commercial pilot licence. For example, in <a href="/wiki/European_Union_Aviation_Safety_Agency" title="European Union Aviation Safety Agency">European Union Aviation Safety Agency</a> states<sup id="cite_ref-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-4">&#91;4&#93;</a></sup> and the UK<sup id="cite_ref-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-5">&#91;5&#93;</a></sup> it is possible to become a paid flight instructor with only a <a href="/wiki/Private_pilot_licence" title="Private pilot licence">private pilot licence</a>. Nonetheless, in the UK, flight instruction is considered a commercial operation.<sup id="cite_ref-6" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-6">&#91;6&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>It is the purpose of the flight, not the aircraft or pilot, that determines whether the flight is commercial or private.<sup id="cite_ref-7" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-7">&#91;7&#93;</a></sup> For example, if a commercially licensed pilot flies a plane to visit a friend or attend a business meeting, this would be a private flight. Conversely, a private pilot could legally fly a multi-engine complex aircraft carrying passengers for non-commercial purposes (no compensation paid to the pilot, and a <i><a href="/wiki/Pro_rata" title="Pro rata">pro rata</a></i> or larger portion of the aircraft operating expenses paid by the pilot).
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Potential_radiation_exposure">Potential radiation exposure</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Commercial_aviation&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Potential radiation exposure"><span>edit</span></a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The <a href="/wiki/Magnetosphere" title="Magnetosphere">magnetosphere</a> guides <a href="/wiki/Cosmic_ray" title="Cosmic ray">cosmic ray</a> and <a href="/wiki/Solar_energetic_particles" title="Solar energetic particles">solar energetic particles</a> to polar latitudes, while high-energy charged particles enter the <a href="/wiki/Mesosphere" title="Mesosphere">mesosphere</a>, <a href="/wiki/Stratosphere" title="Stratosphere">stratosphere</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Troposphere" title="Troposphere">troposphere</a>. These energetic particles at the top of the atmosphere shatter atmospheric atoms and molecules, creating harmful lower-energy particles that penetrate deep into the atmosphere and create measurable radiation. All aircraft flying above 8 km (26,200 feet) altitude are exposed to these particles. The dose exposure is greater in polar regions than in mid-latitude and equatorial regions. When a <a href="/wiki/Space_weather" title="Space weather">space weather</a> event causes radiation exposure to exceed the safe level set by aviation authorities, the aircraft's flight path is diverted.<sup id="cite_ref-8" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-8">&#91;8&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>While the most significant — but highly unlikely — health consequences of atmospheric radiation exposure include death from <a href="/wiki/Radiation-induced_cancer" title="Radiation-induced cancer">radiation-induced cancer</a> due to long-term exposure, many lifestyle-degrading and career-impacting cancer forms can also occur.<sup id="cite_ref-9" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-9">&#91;9&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-10" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-10">&#91;10&#93;</a></sup> A cancer diagnosis can have significant career impact for a commercial pilot. A cancer diagnosis can ground a pilot temporarily or permanently. International guidelines from the <a href="/wiki/International_Commission_on_Radiological_Protection" title="International Commission on Radiological Protection">International Commission on Radiological Protection</a> (ICRP) have been developed to mitigate this statistical risk.<sup id="cite_ref-11" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-11">&#91;11&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-12" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-12">&#91;12&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-13" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-13">&#91;13&#93;</a></sup> The ICRP recommends effective dose limits of a 5-year average of 20 <a href="/wiki/Sievert" title="Sievert">mSv</a> per year with no more than 50 mSv in a single year for nonpregnant, occupationally exposed persons, and 1 mSv per year for the general public. Radiation dose limits are not engineering limits. In the U.S., they are treated as an upper limit of acceptability and not a regulatory limit.<sup id="cite_ref-14" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-14">&#91;14&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>Measurement of radiation at altitudes above 8 km (26,000 ft) has historically been done by instruments that record the data on board where the data are then processed later on the ground. However, a system of real-time radiation measurement has been developed through the NASA Automated Radiation Measurements for Aerospace Safety (ARMAS) program.<sup id="cite_ref-15" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-15">&#91;15&#93;</a></sup> <a rel="nofollow" class="external text" href="http://sol.spacenvironment.net/~ARMAS/">ARMAS</a> has flown hundreds of flights since 2013, mostly on research aircraft, and sent the data to the ground through <a href="/wiki/Iridium_satellite_constellation" title="Iridium satellite constellation">Iridium satellite</a> links. The goal is to assimilate this data into physics-based global radiation models, e.g., NASA's Nowcast of Atmospheric Ionizing Radiation System (<a rel="nofollow" class="external text" href="http://sol.spacenvironment.net/~nairas/index.html">NAIRAS</a>), so as to provide the weather of the radiation environment rather than the <a href="/wiki/Climatology" title="Climatology">climatology</a>.
</p>

<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Origins">Origins</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Commercial_aviation&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Origins"><span>edit</span></a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>

<p><a href="/wiki/Harry_Bruno" title="Harry Bruno">Harry Bruno</a> and <a href="/wiki/Juan_Trippe" title="Juan Trippe">Juan Trippe</a> were early promoters of commercial aviation.
</p><p>The <a href="/wiki/Air_Commerce_Act_of_1926" class="mw-redirect" title="Air Commerce Act of 1926">Air Commerce Act of 1926</a> began to regularize commercial aviation by establishing standards, facilitation, and promotion. An Aeronautical Branch was established in the <a href="/wiki/United_States_Department_of_Commerce" title="United States Department of Commerce">Department of Commerce</a> with <a href="/wiki/William_P._MacCracken_Jr." title="William P. MacCracken Jr.">William P. MacCracken Jr.</a> as director. To promote commercial aviation, he told <a href="/wiki/Town_fathers" class="mw-redirect" title="Town fathers">town fathers</a> that "Communities without airports would be communities without airmail."
</p><p>Writing for <i><a href="/wiki/Collier%27s" title="Collier&#39;s">Collier's</a></i> in 1929, he noted "Commercial aviation is the first industry inspired by hero-worship and built upon heros". He cited the promotion in South America by <a href="/wiki/Herbert_Dargue" title="Herbert Dargue">Herbert Dargue</a> in early 1927. After his 1927 trans-Atlantic flight, <a href="/wiki/Charles_Lindbergh" title="Charles Lindbergh">Charles Lindbergh</a> made a tour of the <a href="/wiki/Contiguous_United_States" title="Contiguous United States">contiguous United States</a>, paid for by the <a href="/wiki/Daniel_Guggenheim_Fund_for_the_Promotion_of_Aeronautics" title="Daniel Guggenheim Fund for the Promotion of Aeronautics">Daniel Guggenheim Foundation for the Promotion of Aeronautics</a>. From that point, commercial aviation took off:
</p>
<blockquote><p>Roads were choked on Sundays, for weeks afterward, by motorists trying to get to <a href="/wiki/Lambert_Field" class="mw-redirect" title="Lambert Field">Lambert Field</a>, Lindbergh's home port in Saint Louis, to buy their first air hop. Hundreds of thousands of you went aloft for the first time that summer.<sup id="cite_ref-16" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-16">&#91;16&#93;</a></sup></p></blockquote><figure class="mw-default-size mw-halign-right" typeof="mw:File/Thumb"><a href="/wiki/File:011_Aircraft_of_various_airlines_together_at_Narita_Airport,_Japan._Swiss_Air_Lines,_United_Airlines,_Thai_Airways.JPG" class="mw-file-description"><img src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/8a/011_Aircraft_of_various_airlines_together_at_Narita_Airport%2C_Japan._Swiss_Air_Lines%2C_United_Airlines%2C_Thai_Airways.JPG/220px-011_Aircraft_of_various_airlines_together_at_Narita_Airport%2C_Japan._Swiss_Air_Lines%2C_United_Airlines%2C_Thai_Airways.JPG" decoding="async" width="220" height="147" class="mw-file-element" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/8a/011_Aircraft_of_various_airlines_together_at_Narita_Airport%2C_Japan._Swiss_Air_Lines%2C_United_Airlines%2C_Thai_Airways.JPG/330px-011_Aircraft_of_various_airlines_together_at_Narita_Airport%2C_Japan._Swiss_Air_Lines%2C_United_Airlines%2C_Thai_Airways.JPG 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/8a/011_Aircraft_of_various_airlines_together_at_Narita_Airport%2C_Japan._Swiss_Air_Lines%2C_United_Airlines%2C_Thai_Airways.JPG/440px-011_Aircraft_of_various_airlines_together_at_Narita_Airport%2C_Japan._Swiss_Air_Lines%2C_United_Airlines%2C_Thai_Airways.JPG 2x" data-file-width="1083" data-file-height="722" /></a><figcaption>Variety of commercial airliners at Tokyo Narita Airport</figcaption></figure>
<p>The Aeronautical Branch was charged with issuing <a href="/wiki/Commercial_pilot_license" class="mw-redirect" title="Commercial pilot license">commercial pilot licenses</a>, <a href="/wiki/Airworthiness_certificate" title="Airworthiness certificate">airworthiness certificates</a>, and with investigating <a href="/wiki/Air_accident" class="mw-redirect" title="Air accident">air accidents</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-17" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-17">&#91;17&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="After_1945">After 1945</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Commercial_aviation&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: After 1945"><span>edit</span></a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>

<p>After World War II, commercial aviation grew rapidly, using mostly ex-military aircraft to transport people and cargo. The experience used in designing <a href="/wiki/Heavy_bomber" title="Heavy bomber">heavy bombers</a> such as the <a href="/wiki/Boeing_B-29_Superfortress" title="Boeing B-29 Superfortress">Boeing B-29 Superfortress</a> and <a href="/wiki/Avro_Lancaster" title="Avro Lancaster">Avro Lancaster</a> could be used for designing heavy commercial aircraft. The <a href="/wiki/Douglas_DC-3" title="Douglas DC-3">Douglas DC-3</a> also made for easier and longer commercial flights. The first commercial jet airliner to fly was the British <a href="/wiki/De_Havilland_Comet" title="De Havilland Comet">de Havilland DH.106 Comet</a>. By 1952, the British state airline <a href="/wiki/British_Overseas_Airways_Corporation" title="British Overseas Airways Corporation">British Overseas Airways Corporation</a> had introduced the Comet into scheduled service. While a technical achievement, the plane suffered a series of highly public failures, as the shape of the windows<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">&#91;<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (March 2019)">citation needed</span></a></i>&#93;</sup> led to cracks due to <a href="/wiki/Fatigue_(material)" title="Fatigue (material)">metal fatigue</a>. The fatigue was caused by cycles of pressurization and depressurization of the cabin, and eventually led to catastrophic failure of the plane's fuselage.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">&#91;<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (March 2019)">citation needed</span></a></i>&#93;</sup> By the time the problems were overcome, other jet airliner designs had already taken to the skies.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Latin_America">Latin America</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Commercial_aviation&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Latin America"><span>edit</span></a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Pre-war">Pre-war</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Commercial_aviation&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Pre-war"><span>edit</span></a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<p>Inspired by the major players such as the <a href="/wiki/United_States" title="United States">United States</a>, the <a href="/wiki/Soviet_Union" title="Soviet Union">Soviet Union</a>, <a href="/wiki/Russia" title="Russia">Russia</a>, <a href="/wiki/France" title="France">France</a> and Britain in the aviation industry<sup class="noprint Inline-Template" style="margin-left:0.1em; white-space:nowrap;">&#91;<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Please_clarify" title="Wikipedia:Please clarify"><span title="What was inspired by these? (September 2022)">clarification needed</span></a></i>&#93;</sup>. In the 1910s, <a href="/wiki/Brazil" title="Brazil">Brazil</a> and <a href="/wiki/Argentina" title="Argentina">Argentina</a> were among the first Latin American countries to possess the instruments of aircraft that were not all locally made, yet the aircraft was locally congregated.<sup id="cite_ref-:0_18-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-:0-18">&#91;18&#93;</a></sup> At that time, many individuals were interested to be pilots in Latin American countries, yet there were not sufficient resources and funding to support and promote the best interests of the aviation industry.<sup id="cite_ref-:0_18-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-:0-18">&#91;18&#93;</a></sup> Amidst these obstacles, Argentina and the <a href="/wiki/Dominican_Republic" title="Dominican Republic">Dominican Republic</a> made efforts in creating jet aviation rather than creating and using propeller planes.<sup id="cite_ref-19" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-19">&#91;19&#93;</a></sup> In 1944, the <a href="/wiki/Chicago_Convention_on_International_Civil_Aviation" class="mw-redirect" title="Chicago Convention on International Civil Aviation">Chicago Convention on International Civil Aviation</a> attended by all Latin American countries except Argentina drafted the clauses of aviation law.<sup id="cite_ref-:1_20-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-:1-20">&#91;20&#93;</a></sup> The introduction of the <a href="/wiki/Fighter_aircraft" title="Fighter aircraft">jet fighter</a> <a href="/wiki/Lockheed_P-80_Shooting_Star" title="Lockheed P-80 Shooting Star">F-80</a> by the US in 1945 pushed the Latin American countries even further away from development of aviation industry because it was simply expensive to recreate the sophisticated technology of F-80.<sup id="cite_ref-:0_18-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-:0-18">&#91;18&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Post-war">Post-war</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Commercial_aviation&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Post-war"><span>edit</span></a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<p>The Latin American Civil Aviation Commission (LACAC) was formed in December 1973 "intended to provide civil aviation authorities in the region with an adequate framework for cooperation and coordination of activities related to civil aviation".<sup id="cite_ref-21" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-21">&#91;21&#93;</a></sup> In 1976, about seven percent of the world logged in the Latin American and Caribbean region.<sup id="cite_ref-:1_20-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-:1-20">&#91;20&#93;</a></sup> This contributed to the increase of average annual rate of air traffic.<sup id="cite_ref-:1_20-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-:1-20">&#91;20&#93;</a></sup> Subsequently, higher passenger load factor decided the profitability of these airlines.
</p><p>According to C. Bogolasky, airline pooling agreements between Latin American airlines contributed to better financial performance of the airlines. The economic problems related to the "airline capacity regulation, regulation of non-scheduled operations, tariff enforcement, high operating costs, passenger and cargo rates."<sup id="cite_ref-:1_20-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-:1-20">&#91;20&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Corporate_social_responsibility">Corporate social responsibility</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Commercial_aviation&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: Corporate social responsibility"><span>edit</span></a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p><a href="/wiki/Corporate_social_responsibility" title="Corporate social responsibility">Corporate social responsibility</a> comprises an umbrella of responsibilities of an organization towards its community, stakeholders and shareholders.<sup id="cite_ref-:2_22-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-:2-22">&#91;22&#93;</a></sup> Organizations who are socially responsible fulfill their <a href="/wiki/Triple_bottom_line" title="Triple bottom line">triple bottom line</a> obligations and dedicate efforts to minimize negative impact on stakeholders and shareholders.<sup id="cite_ref-:2_22-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-:2-22">&#91;22&#93;</a></sup> According to "The Pyramid of Corporate Social Responsibility" by Archie B. Carroll, there are four steps of social responsibility. First, economic responsibility of an organization is to produce profit and maximize the growth of an organization. Second, legal responsibility of an organization is to be compliant with all the laws and regulations. Third, ethical responsibility of an organization to create and follow standards of right decision-making considering how it affects all the stakeholders. Fourth, philanthropic responsibility of an organization to help the community and stakeholders by "giving back".<sup id="cite_ref-:2_22-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-:2-22">&#91;22&#93;</a></sup> The extent of fulfilling the four responsibilities defines the <a href="/wiki/Corporate_citizenship" class="mw-redirect" title="Corporate citizenship">corporate citizenship</a> of an organization.<sup id="cite_ref-:2_22-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-:2-22">&#91;22&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p><a href="/wiki/Delta_Air_Lines" title="Delta Air Lines">Delta</a> and <a href="/wiki/LATAM_Airlines_Group" title="LATAM Airlines Group">LATAM</a> Airlines were the only two airlines listed on the <a href="/wiki/Dow_Jones_Sustainability_Indices" title="Dow Jones Sustainability Indices">Dow Jones Sustainability Index</a>,<sup id="cite_ref-:3_23-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-:3-23">&#91;23&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-24" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-24">&#91;24&#93;</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/LATAM_Airlines_Group" title="LATAM Airlines Group">LATAM</a> being the only airline company in the world to achieve 100% scores for efficiency, reliability and climate strategy in their corporate sustainability assessment.<sup id="cite_ref-:3_23-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-:3-23">&#91;23&#93;</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/LATAM_Airlines_Group" title="LATAM Airlines Group">LATAM</a> promotes their corporate citizenship in their 2016 Sustainability report.<sup id="cite_ref-:4_25-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-:4-25">&#91;25&#93;</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/LATAM_Airlines_Group" title="LATAM Airlines Group">LATAM</a> is affiliated with 6 countries which are Argentina, Colombia, Brazil, Ecuador, Chile, and Peru.<sup id="cite_ref-:4_25-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-:4-25">&#91;25&#93;</a></sup> LATAM accounts for 95% of South America's air traffic.
</p>
