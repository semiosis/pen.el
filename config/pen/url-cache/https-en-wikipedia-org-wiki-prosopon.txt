<p><b>Prosopon</b><sup id="cite_ref-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-4"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>a<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup> originally meant 'face' but is used as a <a href="/wiki/Theological" class="mw-redirect" title="Theological">theological</a> term<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEGonzález2005142_5-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEGonzález2005142-5"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>4<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup> in <a href="/wiki/Christian_theology" title="Christian theology">Christian theology</a> as designation for the concept of a <a href="/wiki/God_in_Christianity" title="God in Christianity">divine</a> <a href="/wiki/Personhood#Christianity" title="Personhood">person</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEDaley2009342–345_6-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEDaley2009342–345-6"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>5<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup> The term has a particular significance in <a href="/wiki/Christian_triadology" class="mw-redirect" title="Christian triadology">Christian triadology</a> (study of the <a href="/wiki/Trinity" title="Trinity">Trinity</a>), and also in <a href="/wiki/Christology" title="Christology">Christology</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEGrillmeier1975501-519_7-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEGrillmeier1975501-519-7"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>6<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEMeyendorff1989173,_190-192,_198,_287,_338_8-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEMeyendorff1989173,_190-192,_198,_287,_338-8"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>7<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup>
</p><p>In the Bible, prosopon is mostly translated as 'face'. In <a href="/wiki/English_language" title="English language">English language</a>, <i>prosopon</i> is used mainly in <a href="/wiki/Scholarly_writing" class="mw-redirect" title="Scholarly writing">scholarly works</a>, related to theology, philosophy or history of religion. While it is commonly translated as <i><a href="/wiki/Person" title="Person">person</a></i>, it is also translated as "role,"<sup id="cite_ref-9" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-9"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>8<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup> or "character," like a character in a play.<sup id="cite_ref-10" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-10"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>9<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup> The term <i>prosopon</i> should not be confused with the term <a href="/wiki/Hypostasis_(religion)" class="mw-redirect" title="Hypostasis (religion)">hypostasis</a>. Both terms are used to describe the Father, Son, and Spirit but <i>hypostasis</i> indicates a reality of existence that <i>prosopon</i> may not have.<sup id="cite_ref-11" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-11"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>10<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup> Whether the Trinity should be described as three hypostases or three prosopa was a core issue in the <a href="/wiki/Arian_Controversy" class="mw-redirect" title="Arian Controversy">Arian Controversy</a>.
</p><p><i>Prosopon</i> is a <a href="/wiki/Greek_language" title="Greek language">Greek</a> term. The Latin equivalent, traditionally used in <a href="/wiki/Western_Christianity" title="Western Christianity">Western Christianity</a>, and from which the English term <i>person</i> is derived, is <i><a href="/wiki/Persona" title="Persona">persona</a>.</i>
</p>
<meta property="mw:PageProp/toc" />
<div class="mw-heading mw-heading2"><h2 id="Overview">Overview</h2><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Prosopon&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Overview"><span>edit</span></a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></div>
<p>The term <i>prosopon</i> originally designated one's "face" or "mask". Most of instances in the New Testament are translated as 'face' or as figurative application of 'face', such as appearance or presence. <a href="/wiki/Paul_the_Apostle" title="Paul the Apostle">Paul the Apostle</a> uses the term when speaking of his direct apprehension in the heart of the face (prosopon) of Christ. In that sense, it was used in Greek theatre, since actors wore specific masks on stage, in order to reveal their character and emotional state to the audience.<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEMeyendorff1989191_12-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEMeyendorff1989191-12"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>11<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup>
</p><p>
The term prosopon was important in the development of the <a href="/wiki/Trinity" title="Trinity">Doctrine of the Trinity</a>. It was the subject of many theological debates and disputes, particularly through early centuries of <a href="/wiki/Christian_history" class="mw-redirect" title="Christian history">Christian history</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEMeyendorff1989_13-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEMeyendorff1989-13"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>12<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup> It was the core issue in the Meletian Schism; a dispute between two pro-Nicene groups. </p><blockquote><p>
“The doctrinal difference between the Meletians and the old Nicenes consisted chiefly in this: that the latter acknowledged three hypostases in the divine trinity, the former only three prosopa; the one laying the stress on the triplicity of the divine essence, the other on its unity.” Philip Schaf</p></blockquote><p>
While <i>prosopon</i> can mean <i>hypostasis</i>, it can also indicate a role played in a theatre. Applied to the Trinity, it can indicate different roles played by a single Person. For that reason, the Sabellians accepted the term<sup id="cite_ref-14" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-14"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>13<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup> but Basil of Caesarea rejected it. Basil, insisted that each Person (Father, Son, and Spirit) must be described as a hypostasis. He wrote:</p><blockquote><p>"It is not enough to count differences in the Persons (prosōpa). It is necessary also to confess that each Person (prosōpon) exists in a true hypostasis. The mirage of persons (prosōpa) without hypostaseis is not denied even by Sabellius, who said that the same God, though he is one subject, is transformed according to the need of each occasion and is thus spoken of now as Father, now as Son, and now as Holy Spirit.”<sup id="cite_ref-15" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-15"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>14<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-16" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-16"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>15<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup></p></blockquote><p>As another example of the distinction, Jerome, writing in Latin, strongly opposed Basil's three hypostaseis and explained "the tri-unity as ‘one substance, three persons' (una substantia, tres personae)."<sup id="cite_ref-17" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-17"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>16<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup> In other words, for Jerome, there was a real and important distinction between hypostasis and prosopon (Greek)/persona (Latin).
</p><div class="mw-heading mw-heading2"><h2 id="Prosopon_in_Christian_triadology">Prosopon in Christian triadology</h2><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Prosopon&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Prosopon in Christian triadology"><span>edit</span></a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></div>
<figure class="mw-default-size mw-halign-right" typeof="mw:File/Thumb"><a href="/wiki/File:%D0%A1%D0%BE%D0%BF%D1%80%D0%B5%D1%81%D1%82%D0%BE%D0%BB%D1%8C%D0%B5_%D0%92%D0%B5%D0%BD%D0%B5%D1%86%D0%B8%D1%8F.jpeg" class="mw-file-description"><img src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/6/6e/%D0%A1%D0%BE%D0%BF%D1%80%D0%B5%D1%81%D1%82%D0%BE%D0%BB%D1%8C%D0%B5_%D0%92%D0%B5%D0%BD%D0%B5%D1%86%D0%B8%D1%8F.jpeg/220px-%D0%A1%D0%BE%D0%BF%D1%80%D0%B5%D1%81%D1%82%D0%BE%D0%BB%D1%8C%D0%B5_%D0%92%D0%B5%D0%BD%D0%B5%D1%86%D0%B8%D1%8F.jpeg" decoding="async" width="220" height="296" class="mw-file-element" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/6/6e/%D0%A1%D0%BE%D0%BF%D1%80%D0%B5%D1%81%D1%82%D0%BE%D0%BB%D1%8C%D0%B5_%D0%92%D0%B5%D0%BD%D0%B5%D1%86%D0%B8%D1%8F.jpeg/330px-%D0%A1%D0%BE%D0%BF%D1%80%D0%B5%D1%81%D1%82%D0%BE%D0%BB%D1%8C%D0%B5_%D0%92%D0%B5%D0%BD%D0%B5%D1%86%D0%B8%D1%8F.jpeg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/6/6e/%D0%A1%D0%BE%D0%BF%D1%80%D0%B5%D1%81%D1%82%D0%BE%D0%BB%D1%8C%D0%B5_%D0%92%D0%B5%D0%BD%D0%B5%D1%86%D0%B8%D1%8F.jpeg 2x" data-file-width="386" data-file-height="520" /></a><figcaption><a href="/wiki/Cretan_School" class="mw-redirect" title="Cretan School">Cretan School</a> icon representing three persons of the Trinity, Venice (16th century)</figcaption></figure>
<p>In Christian triadology, the study of the Trinity, three specific theological concepts have emerged throughout history, in reference to number and mutual relations of divine persons:
</p>
<ul><li>monoprosopic concept advocates that God has only one person;<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTERamelli2011474_18-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTERamelli2011474-18"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>17<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup></li>
<li>dyoprosopic concept advocates that God has two persons (Father and Son);</li>
<li>triprosopic concept advocates that God has three persons (Father, Son and the Holy Spirit).</li></ul>
<p>The most notable example of monoprosopic views is represented in ancient <a href="/wiki/Sabellianism" title="Sabellianism">Sabellianism</a> and its later variants, including teachings of some modern Christian denominations, like those of <a href="/wiki/Oneness_Pentecostalism" title="Oneness Pentecostalism">Oneness Pentecostalism</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEReed201452–70_19-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEReed201452–70-19"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>18<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup>
</p>
<div class="mw-heading mw-heading2"><h2 id="Prosopon_in_Christology">Prosopon in Christology</h2><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Prosopon&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Prosopon in Christology"><span>edit</span></a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></div>
<p>Within <a href="/wiki/Christology" title="Christology">Christology</a>, two specific theological concepts have emerged throughout history, in reference to the <a href="/wiki/Person_of_Christ" class="mw-redirect" title="Person of Christ">Person of Christ</a>: 
</p>
<ul><li>monoprosopic concept (in Christology) advocates that Christ has only one person;</li>
<li>dyoprosopic concept (in Christology) advocates that Christ has two persons (divine and human).<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTESpoerl1994545-568_20-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTESpoerl1994545-568-20"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>19<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup></li></ul>
<p>During the first half of the 5th century, some <a href="/wiki/Catechetical_School_of_Antioch" class="mw-redirect" title="Catechetical School of Antioch">Antiochene theologians</a>, including <a href="/wiki/Theodore_of_Mopsuestia" title="Theodore of Mopsuestia">Theodore of Mopsuestia</a>, and his disciple <a href="/wiki/Nestorius" title="Nestorius">Nestorius</a>, questioned the concept of <a href="/wiki/Hypostatic_union" title="Hypostatic union">hypostatic union</a> of the two <a href="/wiki/Physis" title="Physis">natures</a> (divine and human) of Jesus, but accepted a more loosely defined concept of the <i>prosopic union</i>. Since their views on hypostatic union were seen as controversial, additional questions arose regarding their teachings on the prosopic union.<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEGrillmeier1975432,_463_21-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEGrillmeier1975432,_463-21"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>20<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup>
</p><p>Theodore believed that <a href="/wiki/Incarnation_(Christianity)" title="Incarnation (Christianity)">incarnation of Jesus</a> represents an indwelling of God different from the indwelling experienced by the <a href="/wiki/Old_Testament" title="Old Testament">Old Testament</a> prophets or <a href="/wiki/New_Testament" title="New Testament">New Testament</a> apostles. Jesus was viewed as a human being who shared the divine sonship of the Logos; the Logos united itself to Jesus from the moment of Jesus' conception. After the <a href="/wiki/Resurrection_of_Jesus" title="Resurrection of Jesus">resurrection</a>, the human Jesus and the Logos reveal that they have always been one prosopon.<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTENorris198025_22-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTENorris198025-22"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>21<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup>
</p><p>Theodore addresses the prosopic union in applying prosopon to Christ the <a href="/wiki/Logos_(Christianity)" title="Logos (Christianity)">Logos</a>. He accounts for two expressions of Christ – human and divine. Yet, he does not mean Christ achieved a unity of the two expressions through the formation of a third prosopon, but that one prosopon is produced by the Logos giving his own countenance to the assured man.<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEGrillmeier1975432_23-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEGrillmeier1975432-23"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>22<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup> He interprets the unity of God and man in Christ along the lines of the body-<a href="/wiki/Soul" title="Soul">soul</a> unity. Prosopon plays a special part in his interpretation of Christ. He rejected the Hypostasis concept – believing it to be a contradiction of Christ's true nature. He espoused that, in Christ, both body and soul had to be assumed. Christ assumed a soul and by the grace of God brought it to <a href="/wiki/Immutability_(theology)" title="Immutability (theology)">immutability</a> and to a full dominion over the sufferings of the body.<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEGrillmeier1975424-427_24-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEGrillmeier1975424-427-24"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>23<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup>
</p><p>Nestorius furthered Theodore's views on the prosopic union, claiming that prosopon is the "appearance" of the <a href="/wiki/Ousia" title="Ousia">ousia</a> (essence), and stating: "the prosopon makes known the ousia".<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEGrillmeier1975510_25-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEGrillmeier1975510-25"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>24<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup> On several instances, he emphasized the relation of each of the two natures (divine and human) with their respective appearances, using the term prosopon both in plural forms, and also as a singular designation for the prosopic union.<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEGrillmeier1975463_26-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEGrillmeier1975463-26"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>25<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup> Such terminological complexities and inconsistencies proved to be challenging not only for his contemporary critiques or followers, but also for later commentators and scholars.<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEChesnut1978392–409_27-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEChesnut1978392–409-27"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>26<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup>
</p><p>The very suggestion of prosopic duality was challenging enough to cause heated debates among Christian theologians in the first half of the 5th century, resulting in official condemnation of such views. The <a href="/wiki/Council_of_Ephesus" title="Council of Ephesus">Council of Ephesus</a> of 431 affirmed the teaching of "One Person" of Jesus Christ, condemning all other teachings. The <a href="/wiki/Council_of_Chalcedon" title="Council of Chalcedon">Council of Chalcedon</a> in 451 reaffirmed the notion of One Person of Jesus Christ, formulating the famous <a href="/wiki/Chalcedonian_Definition" title="Chalcedonian Definition">Chalcedonian Definition</a> with its "<a href="/wiki/Chalcedonian_monoprosopic_Christology" class="mw-redirect" title="Chalcedonian monoprosopic Christology">monoprosopic</a>" (having one person) clauses, and in the same time explicitly denying the validity of "dyoprosopic" (having two persons) views.<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEMeyendorff1989177-178_28-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEMeyendorff1989177-178-28"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>27<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup>
</p>
<div class="mw-heading mw-heading2"><h2 id="In_Mandaeism">In Mandaeism</h2><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Prosopon&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: In Mandaeism"><span>edit</span></a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></div>
<p>In the <a href="/wiki/Mandaean_texts" class="mw-redirect" title="Mandaean texts">Mandaean scripture</a> of the <a href="/wiki/Ginza_Rabba" title="Ginza Rabba">Ginza Rabba</a> (in <a href="/wiki/Right_Ginza" title="Right Ginza">Right Ginza</a> books 1 and 2.1), the face or countenance of <a href="/wiki/Hayyi_Rabbi" title="Hayyi Rabbi">Hayyi Rabbi</a> is referred to as the "Great Countenance of Glory" (<a href="/wiki/Classical_Mandaic_language" class="mw-redirect" title="Classical Mandaic language">Classical Mandaic</a>: <span lang="myz" dir="rtl">ࡐࡀࡓࡑࡅࡐࡀ ࡓࡁࡀ ࡖࡏࡒࡀࡓࡀ</span>, <small>romanized:&#160;</small><span title="Classical Mandaic-language romanization"><i lang="myz-Latn">parṣupa rba ḏ-ʿqara</i></span>; pronounced <i>parṣufa</i> in <a href="/wiki/Modern_Mandaic" class="mw-redirect" title="Modern Mandaic">Modern Mandaic</a>; also cognate with <a href="/wiki/Classical_Syriac_language" class="mw-redirect" title="Classical Syriac language">Classical Syriac</a>: <span lang="syc" dir="rtl">ܦܪܨܘܦܐ</span>, <small>romanized:&#160;</small><span title="Classical Syriac-language romanization"><i lang="syc-Latn">prṣupa</i></span>, <small><a href="/wiki/Literal_translation" title="Literal translation">lit.</a>&#8201;</small>&#39;countenance&#39;, attested in the <a href="/wiki/Peshitta" title="Peshitta">Peshitta</a> including in <a href="/wiki/Matthew_17" title="Matthew 17">Matthew 17</a>:2<sup id="cite_ref-29" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-29"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>28<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup>).<sup id="cite_ref-GR_Gelbert_30-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-GR_Gelbert-30"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>29<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup> This <a href="/wiki/Aramaic" title="Aramaic">Aramaic</a> term is a borrowing from the Greek word <i>prosopon</i>.<sup id="cite_ref-31" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-31"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>30<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup>
</p>
