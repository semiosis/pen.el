<div class="field field-text-body"><h2>Introduction</h2>
<p>The Heidelberg Catechism (1563) was composed in the city of Heidelberg, Germany, at the request of Elector Frederick III, who ruled the province of the Palatinate from 1559 to 1576. The new catechism was intended as a tool for teaching young people, a guide for preaching in the provincial churches, and a form of confessional unity among the several Protestant factions in the Palatinate. An old tradition credits Zacharias Ursinus and Caspar Olevianus with being the coauthors of the catechism, but the project was actually the work of a team of ministers and university theologians under the watchful eye of Frederick himself. Ursinus probably served as the primary writer on the team, and Olevianus had a lesser role. The catechism was approved by a synod in Heidelberg in January 1563. A second and third German edition, each with small additions, as well as a Latin translation were published the same year in Heidelberg. The third edition was included in the Palatinate Church Order of November 15, 1563, at which time the catechism was divided into fifty-two sections or Lord's Days, so that one Lord's Day could be explained in an afternoon worship service each Sunday of the year.</p>
<p>The Synod of Dort approved the Heidelberg Catechism in 1619, and it soon became the most ecumenical of the Reformed catechisms and confessions. It has been translated into many European, Asian, and African languages and is still the most widely used and warmly praised catechism of the Reformation period.</p>
<p>Most of the footnoted biblical references in this translation of the catechism were included in the early German and Latin editions, but the precise selection was approved by Synod 1975 of the Christian Reformed Church.</p>
<h4>Lord’s Day 1</h4>
Q &amp; A 1
<p>Q.&nbsp;What is your only comfort<br>
in life and in death?</p>
<p>A.&nbsp;That I am not my own,1<br>
but belong—</p>
<p>body and soul,<br>
in life and in death—2</p>
<p>to my faithful Savior, Jesus Christ.3</p>
<p>He has fully paid for all my sins with his precious blood,4<br>
and has set me free from the tyranny of the devil.5<br>
He also watches over me in such a way6<br>
that not a hair can fall from my head<br>
without the will of my Father in heaven;7<br>
in fact, all things must work together for my salvation.8</p>
<p>Because I belong to him,<br>
Christ, by his Holy Spirit,<br>
assures me of eternal life9<br>
and makes me wholeheartedly willing and ready<br>
from now on to live for him.10</p>
<p>1&nbsp;<a href="https://www.biblegateway.com/passage/?search=1%20Cor.%206:19-20&amp;version=nrsv">1 Cor. 6:19-20</a><br>
2&nbsp;<a href="https://www.biblegateway.com/passage/?search=Rom.%2014:7-9&amp;version=nrsv">Rom. 14:7-9</a><br>
3&nbsp;<a href="https://www.biblegateway.com/passage/?search=1%20Cor.%203:23&amp;version=nrsv">1 Cor. 3:23</a>;&nbsp;<a href="https://www.biblegateway.com/passage/?search=Titus%202:14&amp;version=nrsv">Titus 2:14</a><br>

<p>Q.&nbsp;How does the Lord’s Supper<br>

<p>A.&nbsp;The Lord’s Supper declares to us</p>
<p>that all our sins are completely forgiven<br>
through the one sacrifice of Jesus Christ,<br>
which he himself accomplished on the cross once for all.1</p>
<p>It also declares to us</p>
<p>that the Holy Spirit grafts us into Christ,2<br>

<p>that the living and the dead<br>
do not have their sins forgiven<br>

<p>It also teaches</p>
<p>that Christ is bodily present<br>
under the form of bread and wine<br>
where Christ is therefore to be worshiped.</p>

<p>nothing but a denial<br>
of the one sacrifice and suffering of Jesus Christ<br>

Q &amp; A 126
<p>Q.&nbsp;What does the fifth petition mean?</p>
<p>A.&nbsp;“Forgive us our debts,<br>
as we also have forgiven our debtors” means:</p>
<p>Because of Christ’s blood,<br>
do not hold against us, poor sinners that we are,</p>
<p>any of the sins we do<br>
or the evil that constantly clings to us.1</p>
<p>Forgive us just as we are fully determined,</p>
<p>as evidence of your grace in us,</p>
<p>to forgive our neighbors.2</p>
<p>1&nbsp;<a href="https://www.biblegateway.com/passage/?search=Ps.%2051:1-7&amp;version=nrsv">Ps. 51:1-7</a>;&nbsp;<a href="https://www.biblegateway.com/passage/?search=Ps.%20143:2&amp;version=nrsv">143:2</a>;&nbsp;<a href="https://www.biblegateway.com/passage/?search=Rom.%208:1&amp;version=nrsv">Rom. 8:1</a>;&nbsp;<a href="https://www.biblegateway.com/passage/?search=1%20John%202:1-2&amp;version=nrsv">1 John 2:1-2</a><br>
2&nbsp;<a href="https://www.biblegateway.com/passage/?search=Matt.%206:14-15&amp;version=nrsv">Matt. 6:14-15</a>;&nbsp;<a href="https://www.biblegateway.com/passage/?search=Matt.%2018:21-35&amp;version=nrsv">18:21-35</a></p>
<h4>Lord’s Day 52</h4>
Q &amp; A 127
<p>Q.&nbsp;What does the sixth petition mean?</p>
<p>A.&nbsp;“And do not bring us to the time of trial,<br>
but rescue us from the evil one” means:</p>
<p>By ourselves we are too weak<br>
to hold our own even for a moment.1<br>
And our sworn enemies—</p>
<p>the devil,2&nbsp;the world,3&nbsp;and our own flesh—4</p>
<p>never stop attacking us.</p>
<p>And so, Lord,<br>
uphold us and make us strong</p>
<p>with the strength of your Holy Spirit,</p>
<p>so that we may not go down to defeat</p>
<p>in this spiritual struggle,5</p>
<p>but may firmly resist our enemies</p>
<p>until we finally win the complete victory.6</p>
<p>1&nbsp;<a href="https://www.biblegateway.com/passage/?search=Ps.%20103:14-16&amp;version=nrsv">Ps. 103:14-16</a>;&nbsp;<a href="https://www.biblegateway.com/passage/?search=John%2015:1-5&amp;version=nrsv">John 15:1-5</a><br>
2&nbsp;<a href="https://www.biblegateway.com/passage/?search=2%20Cor.%2011:14&amp;version=nrsv">2 Cor. 11:14</a>;&nbsp;<a href="https://www.biblegateway.com/passage/?search=Eph.%206:10-13&amp;version=nrsv">Eph. 6:10-13</a>;&nbsp;<a href="https://www.biblegateway.com/passage/?search=1%20Pet.%205:8&amp;version=nrsv">1 Pet. 5:8</a><br>
3&nbsp;<a href="https://www.biblegateway.com/passage/?search=John%2015:18-21&amp;version=nrsv">John 15:18-21</a><br>
4&nbsp;<a href="https://www.biblegateway.com/passage/?search=Rom.%207:23&amp;version=nrsv">Rom. 7:23</a>;&nbsp;<a href="https://www.biblegateway.com/passage/?search=Gal.%205:17&amp;version=nrsv">Gal. 5:17</a><br>
5&nbsp;<a href="https://www.biblegateway.com/passage/?search=Matt.%2010:19-20&amp;version=nrsv">Matt. 10:19-20</a>;&nbsp;<a href="https://www.biblegateway.com/passage/?search=Matt.%2026:41&amp;version=nrsv">26:41</a>;&nbsp;<a href="https://www.biblegateway.com/passage/?search=Mark%2013:33&amp;version=nrsv">Mark 13:33</a>;&nbsp;<a href="https://www.biblegateway.com/passage/?search=Rom.%205:3-5&amp;version=nrsv">Rom. 5:3-5</a><br>
6&nbsp;<a href="https://www.biblegateway.com/passage/?search=1%20Cor.%2010:13&amp;version=nrsv">1 Cor. 10:13</a>;&nbsp;<a href="https://www.biblegateway.com/passage/?search=1%20Thess.%203:13&amp;version=nrsv">1 Thess. 3:13</a>;&nbsp;<a href="https://www.biblegateway.com/passage/?search=1%20Thess.%205:23&amp;version=nrsv">5:23</a></p>
Q &amp; A 128
<p>Q.&nbsp;What does your conclusion to this prayer mean?</p>
<p>A.&nbsp;For the kingdom<br>
and the power<br>
and the glory are yours forever” means:</p>
<p>We have made all these petitions of you<br>
because, as our all-powerful king,</p>
<p>you are both willing and able<br>
to give us all that is good;1</p>
<p>and because your holy name,</p>
<p>and not we ourselves,</p>
<p>should receive all the praise, forever.2</p>
<p>1&nbsp;<a href="https://www.biblegateway.com/passage/?search=Rom.%2010:11-13&amp;version=nrsv">Rom. 10:11-13</a>;&nbsp;<a href="https://www.biblegateway.com/passage/?search=2%20Pet.%202:9&amp;version=nrsv">2 Pet. 2:9</a><br>
2&nbsp;<a href="https://www.biblegateway.com/passage/?search=Ps.%20115:1&amp;version=nrsv">Ps. 115:1</a>;&nbsp;<a href="https://www.biblegateway.com/passage/?search=John%2014:13&amp;version=nrsv">John 14:13</a></p>
Q &amp; A 129
<p>Q.&nbsp;What does that little word “Amen” express?</p>
<p>A.&nbsp;“Amen” means:</p>
<p>This shall truly and surely be!</p>
<p>It is even more sure</p>
<p>that God listens to my prayer</p>
<p>than that I really desire</p>
<p>what I pray for.1</p>
<p>1&nbsp;<a href="https://www.biblegateway.com/passage/?search=Isa.%2065:24&amp;version=nrsv">Isa. 65:24</a>;&nbsp;<a href="https://www.biblegateway.com/passage/?search=2%20Cor.%201:20&amp;version=nrsv">2 Cor. 1:20</a>;&nbsp;<a href="https://www.biblegateway.com/passage/?search=2%20Tim.%202:13&amp;version=nrsv">2 Tim. 2:13</a></p></div>

<div class="field field-text-body"><h3>Background</h3>
<p>The Heidelberg Catechism, written in 1563, originated in one of the few pockets of Calvinistic faith in the Lutheran and Catholic territories of Germany. Conceived originally as a teaching instrument to promote religious unity in the Palatinate, the catechism soon became a guide for preaching as well. It is a remarkably warm-hearted and personalized confession of faith, eminently deserving of its popularity among Reformed churches to the present day.</p>
<p>This page contains the English version&nbsp;<a data-entity-substitution="canonical" data-entity-type="node" data-entity-uuid="5b55222f-26e2-4beb-bc68-e9bca2fcc2ec" href="/welcome/beliefs/introduction-reformed-confessions-translation-2011">approved by Synod 2011</a>&nbsp;of the Christian Reformed Church. You may also&nbsp;<a data-entity-substitution="media" data-entity-type="media" data-entity-uuid="13956e4d-c772-4523-a1df-b43c7612808d" href="/sites/default/files/HeidelbergCatechism.pdf">view it as a PDF</a>. An earlier version (approved by Synod 1988)&nbsp;is also available as a&nbsp;<a data-entity-substitution="media" data-entity-type="media" data-entity-uuid="e4038d89-a9c0-4799-a232-e7a4fdc9587f" href="/sites/default/files/Heidelberg%20Catechism_old.pdf">PDF</a>.</p></div>
