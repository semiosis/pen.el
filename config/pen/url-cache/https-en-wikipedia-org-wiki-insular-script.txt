<p><b>Insular script</b> is a <a href="/wiki/Middle_Ages" title="Middle Ages">medieval</a> <a href="/wiki/Script_(styles_of_handwriting)" class="mw-redirect" title="Script (styles of handwriting)">script</a> system originating from Ireland that spread to <a href="/wiki/England" title="England">England</a> and continental Europe under the influence of <a href="/wiki/Hiberno-Scottish_mission" title="Hiberno-Scottish mission">Irish Christianity</a>. Irish missionaries took the script to continental Europe, where they founded monasteries such as <a href="/wiki/Bobbio_Abbey" title="Bobbio Abbey">Bobbio</a>. The scripts were also used in monasteries like <a href="/wiki/Fulda_monastery" class="mw-redirect" title="Fulda monastery">Fulda</a>, which were influenced by English missionaries. They are associated with <a href="/wiki/Insular_art" title="Insular art">Insular art</a>, of which most surviving examples are <a href="/wiki/Illuminated_manuscript" title="Illuminated manuscript">illuminated manuscripts</a>. It greatly influenced modern <a href="/wiki/Gaelic_type" title="Gaelic type">Gaelic type</a> and handwriting.
</p><p>The term "Insular script" is used to refer to a diverse family of scripts used for different functions. At the top of the hierarchy was the Insular <a href="/wiki/Half-uncial" class="mw-redirect" title="Half-uncial">half-uncial</a> (or "Insular <a href="/wiki/Majuscule" class="mw-redirect" title="Majuscule">majuscule</a>"), used for important documents and sacred text. The full <a href="/wiki/Uncial" class="mw-redirect" title="Uncial">uncial</a>, in a version called "English uncial", was used in some English centres. Then "in descending order of formality and increased speed of writing" came "set <a href="/wiki/Lowercase" class="mw-redirect" title="Lowercase">minuscule</a>", "cursive minuscule" and "current minuscule". These were used for non-scriptural texts, letters, accounting records, notes, and all the other types of written documents.<sup id="cite_ref-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-1">&#91;1&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<meta property="mw:PageProp/toc" />
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Origin">Origin</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Insular_script&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Origin"><span>edit</span></a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The scripts developed in Ireland in the 7th century and were used as late as the 19th century, though its most flourishing period fell between 600 and 850. They were closely related to the <a href="/wiki/Uncial" class="mw-redirect" title="Uncial">uncial</a> and <a href="/wiki/Half-uncial" class="mw-redirect" title="Half-uncial">half-uncial</a> scripts, their immediate influences; the highest grade of Insular script is the majuscule Insular half-uncial, which is closely derived from Continental half-uncial script.
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Appearance">Appearance</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Insular_script&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Appearance"><span>edit</span></a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<figure class="mw-default-size" typeof="mw:File/Thumb"><a href="/wiki/File:Script,_Chad-Gospels.jpg" class="mw-file-description"><img src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/a/ae/Script%2C_Chad-Gospels.jpg/220px-Script%2C_Chad-Gospels.jpg" decoding="async" width="220" height="77" class="mw-file-element" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/a/ae/Script%2C_Chad-Gospels.jpg/330px-Script%2C_Chad-Gospels.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/a/ae/Script%2C_Chad-Gospels.jpg/440px-Script%2C_Chad-Gospels.jpg 2x" data-file-width="1024" data-file-height="360" /></a><figcaption><a href="/wiki/St_Chad_Gospels" class="mw-redirect" title="St Chad Gospels">St Chad Gospels</a>: <span title="Medieval Latin-language text"><i lang="la">Et factum est iter[um cum sabbatis ambula]ret Iesus per sata</i></span> (Mark 2:23, p.&#160;151)</figcaption></figure>
<figure typeof="mw:File/Thumb"><a href="/wiki/File:Evolution_of_minuscule.svg" class="mw-file-description"><img src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/c/c0/Evolution_of_minuscule.svg/400px-Evolution_of_minuscule.svg.png" decoding="async" width="400" height="288" class="mw-file-element" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/c/c0/Evolution_of_minuscule.svg/600px-Evolution_of_minuscule.svg.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/c/c0/Evolution_of_minuscule.svg/800px-Evolution_of_minuscule.svg.png 2x" data-file-width="1824" data-file-height="1312" /></a><figcaption>Simplified relationship between various scripts, showing the development of Insular uncial from Roman and the Greek uncial</figcaption></figure>
<p>Works written in Insular scripts commonly use large initial letters surrounded by red ink dots (although this is also true of other <a href="/wiki/Script_(styles_of_handwriting)" class="mw-redirect" title="Script (styles of handwriting)">scripts</a> written in Ireland and England). Letters following a large initial at the start of a paragraph or section often gradually diminish in size as they are written across a line or a page, until the normal size is reached, which is called a "diminuendo" effect, and is a distinctive Insular innovation, which later influenced Continental illumination style. Letters with <a href="/wiki/Ascender_(typography)" title="Ascender (typography)">ascenders</a> (<i>b</i>, <i>d</i>, <i>h</i>, <i>l</i>, etc.) are written with triangular or wedge-shaped tops. The bows of letters such as <i>b</i>, <i>d</i>, <i>p</i>, and <i>q</i> are very wide. The script uses many <a href="/wiki/Ligature_(typography)" class="mw-redirect" title="Ligature (typography)">ligatures</a> and has many unique <a href="/wiki/Scribal_abbreviation" title="Scribal abbreviation">scribal abbreviations</a>, along with many borrowings from <a href="/wiki/Tironian_notes" title="Tironian notes">Tironian notes</a>.
</p><p>Insular script was spread to England by the <a href="/wiki/Hiberno-Scottish_mission" title="Hiberno-Scottish mission">Hiberno-Scottish mission</a>; previously, uncial script had been brought to England by <a href="/wiki/Augustine_of_Canterbury" title="Augustine of Canterbury">Augustine of Canterbury</a>. The influences of both scripts produced the Insular script system.
Within this system, the palaeographer Julian Brown identified five grades, with decreasing formality:
</p>
<ul><li>Insular half-uncial, or "Irish majuscule": the most formal; became reserved for <a href="/wiki/Rubric" title="Rubric">rubrics</a> (highlighted directions) and other displays after the 9th century.<sup id="cite_ref-formal_2-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-formal-2">&#91;2&#93;</a></sup></li>
<li>Insular hybrid minuscule: the most formal of the minuscules, came to be used for formal church books when use of the "Irish majuscule" diminished.<sup id="cite_ref-formal_2-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-formal-2">&#91;2&#93;</a></sup></li>
<li>Insular set minuscule</li>
<li>Insular cursive minuscule</li>
<li>Insular current minuscule: the least formal;<sup id="cite_ref-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-3">&#91;3&#93;</a></sup> <i>current</i> here means ‘running’ (rapid).<sup id="cite_ref-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-4">&#91;4&#93;</a></sup></li></ul>
<p>Brown has also postulated two phases of development for this script, Phase II being mainly influenced by Roman uncial examples, developed at <a href="/wiki/Wearmouth-Jarrow" class="mw-redirect" title="Wearmouth-Jarrow">Wearmouth-Jarrow</a> and typified by the <a href="/wiki/Lindisfarne_Gospels" title="Lindisfarne Gospels">Lindisfarne Gospels</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-5">&#91;5&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Usage">Usage</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Insular_script&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Usage"><span>edit</span></a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Insular script was used not only for <a href="/wiki/Latin" title="Latin">Latin</a> religious books, but also for every other kind of book, including vernacular works. Examples include the <a href="/wiki/Book_of_Kells" title="Book of Kells">Book of Kells</a>, the <a href="/wiki/Cathach_of_St._Columba" title="Cathach of St. Columba">Cathach of St. Columba</a>, the <a href="/wiki/Ambrosiana_Orosius" class="mw-redirect" title="Ambrosiana Orosius">Ambrosiana Orosius</a>, the <a href="/wiki/Durham_Cathedral_Library_A._II._10._Gospel_Book_Fragment" class="mw-redirect" title="Durham Cathedral Library A. II. 10. Gospel Book Fragment">Durham Gospel Fragment</a>, the <a href="/wiki/Book_of_Durrow" title="Book of Durrow">Book of Durrow</a>, the <a href="/wiki/Durham_Gospels" title="Durham Gospels">Durham Gospels</a>, the <a href="/wiki/Echternach_Gospels" title="Echternach Gospels">Echternach Gospels</a>, the <a href="/wiki/Lindisfarne_Gospels" title="Lindisfarne Gospels">Lindisfarne Gospels</a>, the <a href="/wiki/Lichfield_Gospels" title="Lichfield Gospels">Lichfield Gospels</a>, the <a href="/wiki/St._Gall_Gospel_Book" class="mw-redirect" title="St. Gall Gospel Book">St. Gall Gospel Book</a>, and the <a href="/wiki/Book_of_Armagh" title="Book of Armagh">Book of Armagh</a>.
</p><p>Insular script was influential in the development of <a href="/wiki/Carolingian_minuscule" title="Carolingian minuscule">Carolingian minuscule</a> in the <a href="/wiki/Scriptoria" class="mw-redirect" title="Scriptoria">scriptoria</a> of the Carolingian empire.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">&#91;<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (March 2024)">citation needed</span></a></i>&#93;</sup>
</p><p>In Ireland, Insular script was superseded in <abbr title="circa">c.</abbr><span style="white-space:nowrap;">&#8201;850</span> by Late Insular script; in England, it was followed by a form of <a href="/wiki/Caroline_minuscule" class="mw-redirect" title="Caroline minuscule">Caroline minuscule</a>.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">&#91;<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (March 2024)">citation needed</span></a></i>&#93;</sup>
</p><p>The <a href="/wiki/Tironian_notes" title="Tironian notes">Tironian</a> <span title="Latin-language text"><i lang="la">et</i></span>, <span class="nowrap">&#x27e8;⁊&#x27e9;</span>&#160;&#8211;&#32; equivalent of <a href="/wiki/Ampersand" title="Ampersand">ampersand</a> <span class="nowrap">&#x27e8;&amp;&#x27e9;</span>&#160;&#8211;&#32; was in widespread use in the script (meaning <span title="Old Irish (to 900)-language text"><i lang="sga">agus</i></span> 'and' in Irish, and <span title="Old English (ca. 450-1100)-language text"><i lang="ang">ond</i></span> 'and' in <a href="/wiki/Old_English" title="Old English">Old English</a>) and is occasionally continued in modern <a href="/wiki/Gaelic_type" title="Gaelic type">Gaelic typefaces</a> derived from Insular script.
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Unicode">Unicode</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Insular_script&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Unicode"><span>edit</span></a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>

<div class="side-box-text plainlist"><b>This article contains uncommon <a href="/wiki/Unicode" title="Unicode">Unicode</a> characters.</b> Without proper <a href="/wiki/Help:Multilingual_support" title="Help:Multilingual support">rendering support</a>, you may see <a href="/wiki/Specials_(Unicode_block)#Replacement_character" title="Specials (Unicode block)">question marks, boxes, or other symbols</a>&#32;instead of the intended characters.</div></div>

<p>Unicode treats representation of letters of the <a href="/wiki/Latin_alphabet" title="Latin alphabet">Latin alphabet</a> written in insular script as a typeface choice that needs no separate coding.<sup id="cite_ref-Everson_6-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Everson-6">&#91;6&#93;</a></sup> Only a few Insular letters have specific code-points because they are used by phonetic specialists. To render the full alphabet correctly, a suitable <a href="/wiki/Display_font" class="mw-redirect" title="Display font">display font</a> should be chosen. To display the specialist characters, there are several fonts that may be used; three free ones that support these characters are <a href="/wiki/Junicode" title="Junicode">Junicode</a>, Montagel, and Quivira. <a href="/wiki/Gentium" title="Gentium">Gentium</a> and <a href="/wiki/Charis_SIL" title="Charis SIL">Charis SIL</a> support the alphabetic letters (U+A77x and U+A78x).
</p><p>
According to <a href="/wiki/Michael_Everson" title="Michael Everson">Michael Everson</a>, in the 2006 Unicode proposal for these characters:<sup id="cite_ref-Everson_6-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Everson-6">&#91;6&#93;</a></sup> <style data-mw-deduplicate="TemplateStyles:r1211633275">.mw-parser-output .templatequote{overflow:hidden;margin:1em 0;padding:0 32px}.mw-parser-output .templatequote .templatequotecite{line-height:1.5em;text-align:left;padding-left:1.6em;margin-top:0}</style></p><blockquote class="templatequote"><p>To write text in an ordinary <a href="/wiki/Gaelic_type" title="Gaelic type">Gaelic font</a>, only <a href="/wiki/ASCII" title="ASCII">ASCII</a> letters should be used, the font making all the relevant substitutions; the insular letters [proposed here] are for use only by specialists who require them for particular purposes.</p></blockquote>
