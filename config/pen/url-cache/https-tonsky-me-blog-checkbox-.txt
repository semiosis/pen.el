        <h1 class="title">In Loving Memory of Square Checkbox</h1>
        <p>This is a checkbox:</p>
        <figure>
<img src="checkbox@2x.png?t=1706441704" style="aspect-ratio: 600/150; " width="600" height="150">        </figure>
        <p>It’s square, it has a checkmark inside, and its distinguishing feature is that you can select any number of them at the same time:</p>
        <figure>
<img src="checkboxes@2x.png?t=1706441704" style="aspect-ratio: 600/400; " width="600" height="400">        </figure>
        <p>Different operating systems rendered them differently during their evolution:</p>
        <figure>
<img src="checkbox_macos_sonoma@2x.png?t=1706441704" style="aspect-ratio: 500/100; " width="500" height="100"><figcaption>macOS Sonoma (2023)</figcaption>        </figure>
        <figure>
<img src="checkbox_os_x_maverics@2x.png?t=1706441704" style="aspect-ratio: 500/225; " width="500" height="225"><figcaption>OS X Mavericks (2013)</figcaption>        </figure>
        <figure>
<img src="checkbox_os_x_leopard@2x.png?t=1706441704" style="aspect-ratio: 500/125; " width="500" height="125"><figcaption>OS X Leopard (2007)</figcaption>        </figure>
        <figure>
<img src="checkbox_mac_os_9@2x.png?t=1706441704" style="aspect-ratio: 500/232; " width="500" height="232"><figcaption>Mac OS 9 (1999)</figcaption>        </figure>
        <figure>
<img src="checkbox_mac_os_7.5@2x.png?t=1706441704" style="aspect-ratio: 500/125; " width="500" height="125"><figcaption>Mac OS 7.5 (1994)</figcaption>        </figure>
        <figure>
<img src="checkbox_windows_11@2x.png?t=1706441704" style="aspect-ratio: 500/200; " width="500" height="200"><figcaption>Windows 11 (2021)</figcaption>        </figure>
        <figure>
<img src="checkbox_windows_8@2x.png?t=1706441704" style="aspect-ratio: 500/280; " width="500" height="280"><figcaption>Windows 8 (2012)</figcaption>        </figure>
        <figure>
<img src="checkbox_windows_7@2x.png?t=1706441704" style="aspect-ratio: 500/150; " width="500" height="150"><figcaption>Windows 7 (2009)</figcaption>        </figure>
        <figure>
<img src="checkbox_windows_xp@2x.png?t=1706441704" style="aspect-ratio: 500/200; " width="500" height="200"><figcaption>Windows XP (2001)</figcaption>        </figure>
        <figure>
<img src="checkbox_windows_2000@2x.png?t=1706441704" style="aspect-ratio: 500/220; " width="500" height="220"><figcaption>Windows 2000 (2000)</figcaption>        </figure>
        <figure>
<img src="checkbox_windows_95@2x.png?t=1706441704" style="aspect-ratio: 500/124; " width="500" height="124"><figcaption>Windows 95 (1995)</figcaption>        </figure>
        <figure>
<img src="checkbox_windows_3.11@2x.png?t=1706441704" style="aspect-ratio: 500/200; " width="500" height="200"><figcaption>Windows for Workgroups 3.11 (1993)</figcaption>        </figure>
        <p>As you can see, even the checkmark wasn’t always there. But one thing remained constant: checkboxes were square.</p>
        <p>Why square? Because that’s how you can tell them from radio buttons:</p>
        <figure>
<img src="radiobuttons@2x.png?t=1706441704" style="aspect-ratio: 600/620; " width="600" height="620">        </figure>
        <p>Their distinguishing feature is a single choice. If you select one, everything else is de-selected.</p>
        <p>I’m not sure when the distinction between square/round was introduced, but it seems to already exist in the 90-s:</p>
        <figure>
<img src="turbo_pascal@2x.png?t=1706441704" style="aspect-ratio: 720/540; " width="720" height="540"><figcaption>Turbo Pascal 7.0 (1992)</figcaption>        </figure>
        <figure>
<img src="norton_commander_5@2x.png?t=1706441704" style="aspect-ratio: 720/540; " width="720" height="540"><figcaption>Norton Commander 5 (1993)</figcaption>        </figure>
        <p>(Guess where <code>[x]</code> is used now? In Markdown! What a comeback, huh?)</p>
        <p>And since then, every major operating system followed this tradition. From Windows 3.11:</p>
        <figure>
<img src="radio_windows_3.11@2x.png?t=1706441704" style="aspect-ratio: 500/100; " width="500" height="100"><figcaption>Windows for Workgroups 3.11 (1993)</figcaption>        </figure>
        <p>through Windows 95:</p>
        <figure>
<img src="radio_windows_95@2x.png?t=1706441704" style="aspect-ratio: 500/340; " width="500" height="340">        </figure>
        <p>to Windows 11:</p>
        <figure>
<img src="radio_windows_11@2x.png?t=1706441704" style="aspect-ratio: 500/400; " width="500" height="400"><figcaption>Windows 11 (2021)</figcaption>        </figure>
        <p>from Mac OS 4:</p>
        <figure>
<img src="radio_mac_os_7.5@2x.png?t=1706441704" style="aspect-ratio: 500/120; " width="500" height="120"><figcaption>Mac OS 4 (1987)</figcaption>        </figure>
        <p>till macOS Sonoma:</p>
        <figure>
<img src="radio_macos_sonoma@2x.png?t=1706441704" style="aspect-ratio: 500/300; " width="500" height="300"><figcaption>macOS Sonoma (2023)</figcaption>        </figure>
        <p>There was a brief confusion up until 1986 when Apple used rounded rectangles instead of circles:</p>
        <figure>
<img src="radio_mac_os_3@2x.png?t=1706441704" style="aspect-ratio: 500/200; " width="500" height="200"><figcaption>Mac OS 3 (1986)</figcaption>        </figure>
        <p>but it was quickly resolved.</p>
        <p>The point is, every major OS vendor has been adhering to the convention that checkboxes are square and radio buttons are round.</p>
        <p>Then the Web came. And when I say Web, I mean CSS. And when I say CSS, I mean Flash and then JavaScript.</p>
        <p>You see, people on the Web think conventions are boring. That regular controls need to be reinvented and redesigned. They don’t believe there are any norms.</p>
        <p>That’s why it’s common to see radio buttons containing checkmarks:</p>
        <figure>
<img src="twitter@2x.png?t=1706441704" style="aspect-ratio: 500/400; " width="500" height="400"><figcaption>Twitter (2024)</figcaption>        </figure>
        <p>Or square radio buttons:</p>
        <figure>
<img src="radio_yandex_maps@2x.png?t=1706441704" style="aspect-ratio: 700/300; " width="700" height="300"><figcaption>Yandex Maps (2024)</figcaption>        </figure>
        <p>Following the Web’s example, native apps introduced us to round checkboxes:</p>
        <figure>
<img src="telegram@2x.png?t=1706441704" style="aspect-ratio: 720/400; " width="720" height="400"><figcaption>Telegram for macOS (2024)</figcaption>        </figure>
        <p>Sometimes people just don’t make distinctions anymore. For example, here the first group is single-choice, while the second one is multiple-choice:</p>
        <figure>
<img src="yandex_maps@2x.png?t=1706441704" style="aspect-ratio: 700/600; " width="700" height="600"><figcaption>Yandex Maps (2024)</figcaption>        </figure>
        <p>Or here, one of those polls is single-answer, another is multiple-answer:</p>
        <figure>
<img src="telegram_polls@2x.png?t=1706441704" style="aspect-ratio: 400/580; " width="400" height="580"><figcaption>Telegram Web (2024)</figcaption>        </figure>
        <p>How are people supposed to know?</p>
        <figure>
<img src="scooby@2x.jpg?t=1706441704" style="aspect-ratio: 500/682; " width="500" height="682">        </figure>
        <p>But despite all this chaos and temptation, operating system vendors knew better. To this day, they follow THE convention: checkboxes are square, radio buttons are round.</p>
        <p>Maybe it was part of their internal training. Maybe they had experienced art directors. Maybe it was just luck. I don’t know — it doesn’t really matter — but — somehow — they managed to stick to the convention.</p>
        <p>Until this day.</p>
        <p>Apple is the first major operating system vendor who had abandoned a four-decades-long tradition. Their new visionOS — for the first time in the history of Apple — will have round checkboxes.</p>
        <figure>
<img src="checkbox_vision@2x.png?t=1706441704" style="aspect-ratio: 650/672; " width="650" height="672"><figcaption><a href="https://developer.apple.com/design/resources/#visionos-apps" target="_blank">Apple Design Resouces — visionOS — Library</a></figcaption>        </figure>
        <p>How should we even call these? Radio checks? Check buttons?</p>
        <p>Anyway, with Apple’s betrayal, I think it’s fair to say there’s no hope for this tradition to continue.</p>
        <p>I therefore officially announce 2024 to be the year when the square checkbox has finally died.</p>
        <figure>
<img src="rip@2x.jpg?t=1706441704" style="aspect-ratio: 960/600; " width="960" height="600">        </figure>
        <p>Kids these days will use a toggle anyway.</p>
