<p>The <b>Nazarenes</b> (or <b>Nazoreans</b>; <a href="/wiki/Greek_language" title="Greek language">Greek</a>: <span lang="el">Ναζωραῖοι</span>, <small><a href="/wiki/Romanization_of_Greek" title="Romanization of Greek">romanized</a>:&#160;</small><span title="Greek-language romanization"><i lang="el-Latn">Nazōraioi</i></span>)<sup id="cite_ref-strongs_1-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-strongs-1">&#91;1&#93;</a></sup> were an early <a href="/wiki/Jewish_Christian" class="mw-redirect" title="Jewish Christian">Jewish Christian</a> <a href="/wiki/Sect" title="Sect">sect</a> in first-century Judaism. The first use of the term is found in the <a href="/wiki/Acts_of_the_Apostles" title="Acts of the Apostles">Acts of the Apostles</a> (<a rel="nofollow" class="external text" href="https://bible.oremus.org/?passage=Acts%2024:5&amp;version=nrsv">Acts 24:5</a>) of the <a href="/wiki/New_Testament" title="New Testament">New Testament</a>, where <a href="/wiki/Paul_the_Apostle" title="Paul the Apostle">Paul the Apostle</a> is accused of being a ringleader of the sect of the Nazarenes ("<span title="Ancient Greek (to 1453)-language text"><span lang="grc">πρωτοστάτην  τε  τῆς  τῶν  Ναζωραίων αἱρέσεως</span></span>") before the Roman <a href="/wiki/Procurator_(Ancient_Rome)" class="mw-redirect" title="Procurator (Ancient Rome)">procurator</a> <a href="/wiki/Antonius_Felix" title="Antonius Felix">Antonius Felix</a> at <a href="/wiki/Caesarea_Maritima" title="Caesarea Maritima">Caesarea Maritima</a> by <a href="/wiki/Tertullus" title="Tertullus">Tertullus</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-2">&#91;2&#93;</a></sup> At that time, the term simply designated followers of <a href="/wiki/Jesus" title="Jesus">Jesus of Nazareth</a>, as the <a href="/wiki/Hebrew_language" title="Hebrew language">Hebrew</a> term <style data-mw-deduplicate="TemplateStyles:r1020748213">.mw-parser-output .script-hebrew,.mw-parser-output .script-Hebr{font-family:"SBL Hebrew","SBL BibLit","Taamey Ashkenaz","Taamey Frank CLM","Frank Ruehl CLM","Ezra SIL","Ezra SIL SR","Keter Aram Tsova","Taamey David CLM","Keter YG","Shofar","David CLM","Hadasim CLM","Simple CLM","Nachlieli",Cardo,Alef,"Noto Serif Hebrew","Noto Sans Hebrew","David Libre",David,"Times New Roman",Gisha,Arial,FreeSerif,FreeSans}</style><span class="script-hebrew" style="font-size: 115%;" dir="rtl">נוֹצְרִי</span>&#8206; (<i><span title="Hebrew-language romanization"><i lang="he-Latn">nôṣrî</i></span></i>),<sup id="cite_ref-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-3">&#91;3&#93;</a></sup> and the Arabic term نَصْرَانِي (<i><span title="Arabic-language romanization"><i lang="ar-Latn">naṣrānī</i></span></i>),<sup id="cite_ref-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-4">&#91;4&#93;</a></sup> still do. 
</p><p>As time passed, the term came to refer to a sect of Jewish Christians who continued to observe the <a href="/wiki/Torah" title="Torah">Torah</a>, in contrast to gentiles who eschewed Torah observance.<sup id="cite_ref-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-5">&#91;5&#93;</a></sup> They are described by <a href="/wiki/Epiphanius_of_Salamis" title="Epiphanius of Salamis">Epiphanius of Salamis</a> and are mentioned later by <a href="/wiki/Jerome" title="Jerome">Jerome</a> and <a href="/wiki/Augustine_of_Hippo" title="Augustine of Hippo">Augustine of Hippo</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-6" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-6">&#91;6&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-7" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-7">&#91;7&#93;</a></sup> The writers made a distinction between the Nazarenes of their time and the "Nazarenes" mentioned in Acts 24:5.<sup id="cite_ref-8" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-8">&#91;8&#93;</a></sup>
</p>

<h2><span id="Nazarene_.28title.29"></span><span class="mw-headline" id="Nazarene_(title)">Nazarene (title)</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Nazarene_(sect)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Nazarene (title)"><span>edit</span></a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<link rel="mw-deduplicated-inline-style" href="mw-data:TemplateStyles:r1033289096"><div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Nazarene_(title)" title="Nazarene (title)">Nazarene (title)</a></div>
<p>The English term <i>Nazarene</i> is commonly used to translate two related Greek words that appear in the New Testament: <i>Nazōraios</i> (<span title="Ancient Greek (to 1453)-language text"><span lang="grc">Ναζωραῖος, Ναζαραῖος</span></span>) ("Nazorean") and <i>Nazarēnos</i> ("Nazarene"). The term <i>Nazōraios</i> may have a religious significance instead of denoting a place of origin, while <i>Nazarēnos</i> (<span title="Ancient Greek (to 1453)-language text"><span lang="grc">Ναζαρηνός</span></span>) is an adjectival form of the phrase <i>apo Nazaret</i> "from Nazareth."<sup id="cite_ref-9" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-9">&#91;9&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>Because of this, the phrases traditionally rendered as "Jesus of Nazareth" can also be translated as "Jesus the Nazarene" or "Jesus the Nazorean." In the New Testament, the form <i>Nazōraios</i> or <i>Nazaraios</i> is more common than <i>Nazarēnos</i> (meaning "from Nazareth").<sup id="cite_ref-strongs_1-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-strongs-1">&#91;1&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span id="The_Sect_of_the_Nazarenes_.281st_century.29"></span><span class="mw-headline" id="The_Sect_of_the_Nazarenes_(1st_century)">The Sect of the Nazarenes (1st century)</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Nazarene_(sect)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: The Sect of the Nazarenes (1st century)"><span>edit</span></a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<link rel="mw-deduplicated-inline-style" href="mw-data:TemplateStyles:r1033289096"><div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">See also: <a href="/wiki/Nazarene_(title)#&quot;Nazarenes&quot;_-_a_term_for_the_Early_Christians" title="Nazarene (title)">Nazarene</a>, <a href="/wiki/Nazirite#In_the_New_Testament" title="Nazirite">Nazirite</a>, <a href="/wiki/Book_of_Acts" class="mw-redirect" title="Book of Acts">Book of Acts</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Early_Christianity" title="Early Christianity">Early Christianity</a></div>
<p>The Greek epithet <i>Nazōraios</i> is applied to Jesus 14 times in the New Testament, and is used once in <a href="/wiki/Acts_of_the_Apostles" title="Acts of the Apostles">Acts</a> to refer to the sect of Christians of which Paul was a leader.<sup id="cite_ref-strongs_1-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-strongs-1">&#91;1&#93;</a></sup> It is traditionally translated as "a man from <a href="/wiki/Nazareth" title="Nazareth">Nazareth</a>"; the plural <i>Nazōraioi</i> would mean "men from Nazareth". The title is first applied to the Christians by <a href="/wiki/Tertullus" title="Tertullus">Tertullus</a> (<a rel="nofollow" class="external text" href="https://bible.oremus.org/?passage=Acts%2024:5&amp;version=nrsv">Acts 24:5</a>), though <a href="/wiki/Herod_Agrippa_II" title="Herod Agrippa II">Herod Agrippa II</a> (<a rel="nofollow" class="external text" href="https://bible.oremus.org/?passage=Acts%2026:28&amp;version=nrsv">Acts 26:28</a>) uses the term "Christians" which had first been used at <a href="/wiki/Antioch" title="Antioch">Antioch</a> (<a rel="nofollow" class="external text" href="https://bible.oremus.org/?passage=Acts%2011:26&amp;version=nrsv">Acts 11:26</a>). The name used by Tertullus survives into <a href="/wiki/Mishnaic_Hebrew" title="Mishnaic Hebrew">Mishnaic</a> and <a href="/wiki/Modern_Hebrew" title="Modern Hebrew">modern Hebrew</a> as <i>notzrim</i> (<link rel="mw-deduplicated-inline-style" href="mw-data:TemplateStyles:r1020748213"><span class="script-hebrew" style="font-size: 115%;" dir="rtl">נוצרים</span>&#8206;) a standard Hebrew term for "Christian", the name also exist in the <a href="/wiki/Quran" title="Quran">Quran</a> and modern <a href="/wiki/Arabic" title="Arabic">Arabic</a> as <span title="Arabic-language text"><span lang="ar" dir="rtl">نَصَارَىٰ</span></span> <i>naṣārā</i> (plural of <span title="Arabic-language text"><span lang="ar" dir="rtl">نَصْرَانِيّ</span></span> <i><a href="/wiki/Christians#Arabic_terms" title="Christians">naṣrānī</a></i> "Christian").

</p>
<h2><span id="The_Nazarenes_.284th_century.29"></span><span class="mw-headline" id="The_Nazarenes_(4th_century)">The Nazarenes (4th century)</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Nazarene_(sect)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: The Nazarenes (4th century)"><span>edit</span></a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<link rel="mw-deduplicated-inline-style" href="mw-data:TemplateStyles:r1033289096"><div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">See also: <a href="/wiki/Nazarene_(title)#Nazarenes,_and_Ephanius&#39;_Nasaraioi_(4th_century_CE)" title="Nazarene (title)">Nazarene (title) §&#160;Nazarenes, and Ephanius' Nasaraioi (4th century CE)</a></div>
<p>According to <a href="/wiki/Epiphanius_of_Salamis" title="Epiphanius of Salamis">Epiphanius</a> in his <i><a href="/wiki/Panarion" title="Panarion">Panarion</a></i>, the 4th-century Nazarenes (Ναζωραῖοι) were originally Jewish converts of the <a href="/wiki/Apostles_in_the_New_Testament" title="Apostles in the New Testament">Apostles</a><sup id="cite_ref-19" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-19">&#91;19&#93;</a></sup> who fled <a href="/wiki/Jerusalem" title="Jerusalem">Jerusalem</a> because of Jesus' prophecy of its <a href="/wiki/Siege_of_Jerusalem_(70_CE)" title="Siege of Jerusalem (70 CE)">coming siege</a> (during the <a href="/wiki/First_Jewish%E2%80%93Roman_War" title="First Jewish–Roman War">First Jewish–Roman War</a> in 70 CE). They fled to <a href="/wiki/Pella,_Jordan" title="Pella, Jordan">Pella</a>, <a href="/wiki/Perea" title="Perea">Peraea</a> (northeast of Jerusalem), and eventually spread outwards to <a href="/wiki/Halab" class="mw-redirect" title="Halab">Beroea</a> (Aleppo) and <a href="/wiki/Bashan" title="Bashan">Basanitis</a>, where they permanently settled (Panarion 29.3.3).<sup id="cite_ref-20" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-20">&#91;20&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>The Nazarenes were similar to the <a href="/wiki/Ebionites" title="Ebionites">Ebionites</a>, in that they considered themselves <a href="/wiki/Jews" title="Jews">Jews</a>, maintained an adherence to the <a href="/wiki/Law_of_Moses" title="Law of Moses">Law of Moses</a>. Unlike the Ebionites, they accepted the <a href="/wiki/Virgin_birth_of_Jesus" title="Virgin birth of Jesus">Virgin Birth</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-Krauss_21-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Krauss-21">&#91;21&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-Hegg_2007_22-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Hegg_2007-22">&#91;22&#93;</a></sup> They seemed to consider Jesus as a prophet, but other attestations from the church fathers might suggest that they also hold on the divinity of Jesus.<sup id="cite_ref-23" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-23">&#91;23&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>As late as the eleventh century, <a href="/wiki/Humbert_of_Silva_Candida" title="Humbert of Silva Candida">Cardinal Humbert of Mourmoutiers</a> still referred to the Nazarene sect as a <a href="/wiki/Seventh-day_Sabbath" class="mw-redirect" title="Seventh-day Sabbath">Sabbath</a>-keeping Christian body existing at that time.<sup id="cite_ref-24" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-24">&#91;24&#93;</a></sup> Modern scholars believe it is the Pasagini or <a href="/wiki/Pasagians" title="Pasagians">Pasagians</a> who are referenced by Cardinal Humbert, suggesting the Nazarene sect existed well into the eleventh century and beyond (the <a href="/wiki/Catholic" class="mw-redirect" title="Catholic">Catholic</a> writings of Bonacursus entitled <i>Against the Heretics</i>). It is believed that Gregorius of Bergamo, about 1250 CE, also wrote concerning the Nazarenes as the <a href="/wiki/Pasagians" title="Pasagians">Pasagians</a>.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Gospel_of_the_Nazarenes">Gospel of the Nazarenes</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Nazarene_(sect)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Gospel of the Nazarenes"><span>edit</span></a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<link rel="mw-deduplicated-inline-style" href="mw-data:TemplateStyles:r1033289096"><div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Gospel_of_the_Nazarenes" title="Gospel of the Nazarenes">Gospel of the Nazarenes</a></div>
<p>The Gospel of the Nazarenes is the title given to fragments of one of the lost <a href="/wiki/Jewish-Christian_Gospels" class="mw-redirect" title="Jewish-Christian Gospels">Jewish-Christian Gospels</a> of Matthew partially reconstructed from the writings of <a href="/wiki/Jerome" title="Jerome">Jerome</a>.
</p>
<h3><span id="Patristic_references_to_.22Nazarenes.22"></span><span class="mw-headline" id="Patristic_references_to_&quot;Nazarenes&quot;">Patristic references to "Nazarenes"</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Nazarene_(sect)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Patristic references to &quot;Nazarenes&quot;"><span>edit</span></a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>In the 4th century, <a href="/wiki/Jerome" title="Jerome">Jerome</a> also refers to Nazarenes as those "who accept Messiah in such a way that they do not cease to observe the old Law." In his <i>Epistle</i> 75, to Augustine, he said:
</p>
<style data-mw-deduplicate="TemplateStyles:r1211633275">.mw-parser-output .templatequote{overflow:hidden;margin:1em 0;padding:0 32px}.mw-parser-output .templatequote .templatequotecite{line-height:1.5em;text-align:left;padding-left:1.6em;margin-top:0}</style><blockquote class="templatequote"><p>What shall I say of the Ebionites who pretend to be Christians? To-day there still exists among the Jews in all the synagogues of the East a heresy which is called that of the Minæans, and which is still condemned by the <a href="/wiki/Pharisees" title="Pharisees">Pharisees</a>; [its followers] are ordinarily called 'Nasarenes'; they believe that Christ, the son of God, was born of the <a href="/wiki/Virgin_Mary" class="mw-redirect" title="Virgin Mary">Virgin Mary</a>, and they hold him to be the one who suffered under Pontius Pilate and ascended to heaven, and in whom we also believe. But while they pretend to be both Jews and Christians, they are neither.<sup id="cite_ref-25" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-25">&#91;25&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-26" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-26">&#91;26&#93;</a></sup></p></blockquote>
<p><a href="/wiki/Jerome" title="Jerome">Jerome</a> saw a distinction between Nazarenes and Ebionites, a different Jewish sect, but does not comment on whether Nazarene Jews considered themselves to be "Christian" or not or how they viewed themselves as fitting into the descriptions he uses. He clearly equates them with <a href="/wiki/Filaster" class="mw-redirect" title="Filaster">Filaster</a>'s Nazarei.<sup id="cite_ref-27" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-27">&#91;27&#93;</a></sup> His criticism of the Nazarenes is noticeably more direct and critical than that of Epiphanius.
</p><p>The following creed is from a church at Constantinople at the same period, and condemns practices of the Nazarenes:
</p>
<link rel="mw-deduplicated-inline-style" href="mw-data:TemplateStyles:r1211633275"><blockquote class="templatequote"><p>I renounce all customs, rites, legalisms, unleavened breads &amp; sacrifices of lambs of the Hebrews, and all other feasts of the Hebrews, sacrifices, prayers, aspersions, purifications, sanctifications and propitiations and fasts, and new moons, and Sabbaths, and superstitions, and hymns and chants and observances and Synagogues, and the food and drink of the Hebrews; in one word, I renounce everything Jewish, every law, rite and custom and if afterwards I shall wish to deny and return to <a href="/wiki/Judaizers" title="Judaizers">Jewish superstition</a>, or shall be found eating with the Jews, or feasting with them, or secretly conversing and condemning the Christian religion instead of openly confuting them and condemning their vain faith, then let the trembling of <a href="/wiki/Gehazi" title="Gehazi">Gehazi</a> cleave to me, as well as the legal punishments to which I acknowledge myself liable. And may I be anathema in the world to come, and may my soul be set down with Satan and the devils."<sup id="cite_ref-28" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-28">&#91;28&#93;</a></sup></p></blockquote>
<p>"Nazarenes" are referenced past the fourth century CE as well. Jacobus de Voragine (1230–98) described James as a "Nazarene" in The Golden Legend, vol 7. Thomas Aquinas (1225–74) quotes Augustine of Hippo, who was given an apocryphal book called Hieremias (<a href="/wiki/Jeremiah" title="Jeremiah">Jeremiah</a> in Latin) by a "Hebrew of the Nazarene Sect", in <a href="/wiki/Catena_(Biblical_commentary)" class="mw-redirect" title="Catena (Biblical commentary)">Catena</a> Aurea — Gospel of Matthew, chapter 27. So this terminology seems to have remained at least through the 13th century in European discussions.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Nazarene_beliefs">Nazarene beliefs</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Nazarene_(sect)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Nazarene beliefs"><span>edit</span></a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The beliefs of the Nazarene sect or sects are described through various church fathers and heresiologists.
</p>
<ul><li>in Jesus as Messiah:</li></ul>
<link rel="mw-deduplicated-inline-style" href="mw-data:TemplateStyles:r1211633275"><blockquote class="templatequote"><p>The Nazarenes... accept Messiah in such a way that they do not cease to observe the old Law.</p><div class="templatequotecite">—&#8202;<cite>Jerome, On. Is. 8:14</cite></div></blockquote>
<ul><li>in the Virgin Birth:</li></ul>
<link rel="mw-deduplicated-inline-style" href="mw-data:TemplateStyles:r1211633275"><blockquote class="templatequote"><p>They believe that the <a href="/wiki/Messiah" title="Messiah">Messiah</a> was born of the Virgin Mary.</p><div class="templatequotecite">—&#8202;<cite>Jerome, Letter 75 Jerome to Augustine</cite></div></blockquote>
<ul><li>Adhering to circumcision and the Law of Moses:</li></ul>
<link rel="mw-deduplicated-inline-style" href="mw-data:TemplateStyles:r1211633275"><blockquote class="templatequote"><p>They disagree with Jews because they have come to faith in Christ; but since they are still fettered by the Law – circumcision, the Sabbath, and the rest – they are not in accord with the Christians.</p><div class="templatequotecite">—&#8202;<cite><a href="/wiki/Epiphanius_of_Salamis" title="Epiphanius of Salamis">Epiphanius of Salamis</a>, <i><a href="/wiki/Panarion" title="Panarion">Panarion</a></i> 29.7.4</cite></div></blockquote>
<ul><li>Use of Old Testament and New Testament:</li></ul>
<link rel="mw-deduplicated-inline-style" href="mw-data:TemplateStyles:r1211633275"><blockquote class="templatequote"><p>They use not only the New Testament but the Old Testament as well, as the Jews do.</p><div class="templatequotecite">—&#8202;<cite>Epiphanius of Salamis, <i>Panarion</i> 29.7.2</cite></div></blockquote>
<ul><li>Use of Hebrew and Aramaic New Testament source texts:</li></ul>
<link rel="mw-deduplicated-inline-style" href="mw-data:TemplateStyles:r1211633275"><blockquote class="templatequote"><p>They have the Gospel according to Matthew in its entirety in Hebrew. For it is clear that they still preserve this, in the Hebrew alphabet, as it was originally written.</p><div class="templatequotecite">—&#8202;<cite>Epiphanius of Salamis, <i>Panarion</i> 29.9.4</cite></div></blockquote>
<link rel="mw-deduplicated-inline-style" href="mw-data:TemplateStyles:r1211633275"><blockquote class="templatequote"><p>And he <a href="/wiki/Hegesippus_(chronicler)" title="Hegesippus (chronicler)">Hegesippus</a> the Nazarene quotes some passages from the <a href="/wiki/Gospel_according_to_the_Hebrews" class="mw-redirect" title="Gospel according to the Hebrews">Gospel according to the Hebrews</a> and from the Syriac [the Aramaic], and some particulars from the Hebrew tongue, showing that he was a convert from the Hebrews, and he mentions other matters as taken from the oral tradition of the Jews.</p><div class="templatequotecite">—&#8202;<cite><a href="/wiki/Eusebius" title="Eusebius">Eusebius</a>, <i><a href="/wiki/Church_History_(Eusebius)" title="Church History (Eusebius)">Church History</a></i> 4.22</cite></div></blockquote>
<h2><span id="The_Judeo-Nazarenes_.287th_century.29"></span><span class="mw-headline" id="The_Judeo-Nazarenes_(7th_century)">The Judeo-Nazarenes (7th century)</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Nazarene_(sect)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: The Judeo-Nazarenes (7th century)"><span>edit</span></a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p><a href="/w/index.php?title=Odon_Lafontaine&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="Odon Lafontaine (page does not exist)">Odon Lafontaine</a>, from the <a href="/wiki/Revisionist_School_of_Islamic_Studies" class="mw-redirect" title="Revisionist School of Islamic Studies">Revisionist School of Islamic Studies</a>, hypothesized that this sect survived into the seventh century, when they attempted to precipitate the return of <a href="/wiki/Jesus" title="Jesus">Jesus</a>, as the <a href="/wiki/Messiah" title="Messiah">Messiah</a> of the <a href="/wiki/Apocalypse" title="Apocalypse">Apocalypse</a>, by translating their scripture and lectionaries into Arabic, recruiting the local Arab Christians, capturing Jerusalem, rebuilding the Temple, and re-initiating its sacrifices. When Jesus did not return, he concluded that the situation eventually span out of control, resulting in the development of the <a href="/wiki/Quran" title="Quran">Quran</a> and <a href="/wiki/Islam" title="Islam">Islam</a>. <sup id="cite_ref-29" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-29">&#91;29&#93;</a></sup>

<p>Those few who are initiated into the secrets of the <a href="/wiki/Mandaeism" title="Mandaeism">Mandaean</a> religion are called <i>Naṣuraiia</i> or Nasoraeans/Nasaraeans meaning guardians or possessors of secret rites and knowledge.<sup id="cite_ref-RudolphEI_30-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-RudolphEI-30">&#91;30&#93;</a></sup> According to the <a href="/wiki/Haran_Gawaita" title="Haran Gawaita">Haran Gawaita</a>, Nasoraean Mandaeans fled <a href="/wiki/Jerusalem" title="Jerusalem">Jerusalem</a> before its <a href="/wiki/Siege_of_Jerusalem_(70_CE)" title="Siege of Jerusalem (70 CE)">fall in 70 CE</a> due to persecution by a faction of <a href="/wiki/Jews" title="Jews">Jews</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-31" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-31">&#91;31&#93;</a></sup> The word <i>Naṣuraiia</i> may come from the root n-ṣ-r meaning "to keep", since although they reject the <a href="/wiki/Law_of_Moses" title="Law of Moses">Mosaic Law</a>, they consider themselves to be keepers of <a href="/wiki/Gnosis" title="Gnosis">Gnosis</a>. <a href="/wiki/Epiphanius_of_Salamis" title="Epiphanius of Salamis">Epiphanius</a> mentions a group called Nasaraeans (Νασαραίοι, <a rel="nofollow" class="external text" href="https://web.archive.org/web/20150906041916/http://www.masseiana.org/panarion_bk1.htm#18">Part 18</a> of the Panarion), distinguished from the "Nazoraioi" (<a rel="nofollow" class="external text" href="https://web.archive.org/web/20150906041916/http://www.masseiana.org/panarion_bk1.htm#29">Part 29</a>). According to <a href="/wiki/Joseph_Lightfoot" class="mw-redirect" title="Joseph Lightfoot">Joseph Lightfoot</a>, <a href="/wiki/Epiphanius_of_Salamis" title="Epiphanius of Salamis">Epiphanius</a> also makes a distinction between the Ossaeans and the Nasaraeans,<sup id="cite_ref-panarion1-19_32-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-panarion1-19-32">&#91;32&#93;</a></sup> the two main groups within the <a href="/wiki/Essenes" title="Essenes">Essenes</a>:<sup id="cite_ref-lightfoot1875_33-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-lightfoot1875-33">&#91;33&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<link rel="mw-deduplicated-inline-style" href="mw-data:TemplateStyles:r1211633275"><blockquote class="templatequote"><p>The Nasaraeans ‐ they were Jews by nationality ‐ originally from Gileaditis, Bashanitis and the Transjordan ... They acknowledged <a href="/wiki/Moses" title="Moses">Moses</a> and believed that he had received laws ‐ not this law, however, but some other. And so, they were Jews who kept all the Jewish observances, but they would not offer sacrifice or eat meat. They considered it unlawful to eat meat or make sacrifices with it. They claim that these <a href="/wiki/Torah" title="Torah">Books</a> are fictions, and that none of these customs were instituted by the fathers. This was the difference between the Nasaraeans and the others.</p><div class="templatequotecite">—&#8202;<cite>Epiphanius' Panarion 1:18</cite></div></blockquote>
<p>The Nasaraeans may be the same as the <a href="/wiki/Mandaeans" title="Mandaeans">Mandaeans</a> of today. <a href="/wiki/Epiphanius_of_Salamis" title="Epiphanius of Salamis">Epiphanius</a>  says (29:6) that they existed before Christ. That is questioned by some, but others accept the pre-Christian origin of this group.<sup id="cite_ref-Drower1960_34-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Drower1960-34">&#91;34&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>In the <i><a href="/wiki/Ginza_Rabba" title="Ginza Rabba">Ginza Rabba</a></i>, the term <i>Nasoraean</i> is used to refer to righteous Mandaeans, i.e., <a href="/wiki/Mandaean_priest" title="Mandaean priest">Mandaean priests</a> (comparable to the concept of <a href="/wiki/Pneumatic_(Gnosticism)" title="Pneumatic (Gnosticism)"><i>pneumatikoi</i></a> in Gnosticism).<sup id="cite_ref-Buckley_2002_35-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Buckley_2002-35">&#91;35&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-Buckley2010_36-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Buckley2010-36">&#91;36&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-GR_Gelbert_37-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-GR_Gelbert-37">&#91;37&#93;</a></sup> As Nasoraeans, Mandaeans believe that they constitute the true congregation of <i>bnai nhura</i> meaning 'Sons of Light'.<sup id="cite_ref-BSN_38-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-BSN-38">&#91;38&#93;</a></sup><sup class="reference nowrap"><span title="Page / location: 50">&#58;&#8202;50&#8202;</span></sup>
</p>
<h2><span id="Modern_.22Nazarene.22_churches"></span><span class="mw-headline" id="Modern_&quot;Nazarene&quot;_churches">Modern "Nazarene" churches</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Nazarene_(sect)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Modern &quot;Nazarene&quot; churches"><span>edit</span></a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>A number of modern churches use the word "Nazarene" or variants in their name or beliefs:
</p>
<ul><li>The <a href="/wiki/Apostolic_Christian_Church_(Nazarene)" title="Apostolic Christian Church (Nazarene)">Apostolic Christian Church (Nazarene)</a>, originating in the Swiss Nazarene Baptist movement;</li>
<li>The <a href="/wiki/Church_of_the_Nazarene" title="Church of the Nazarene">Church of the Nazarene</a>, a <a href="/wiki/Protestant" class="mw-redirect" title="Protestant">Protestant</a> Christian denomination that was born out of the <a href="/wiki/Holiness_Movement" class="mw-redirect" title="Holiness Movement">Holiness Movement</a> of the late 19th and early 20th centuries;</li></ul>
