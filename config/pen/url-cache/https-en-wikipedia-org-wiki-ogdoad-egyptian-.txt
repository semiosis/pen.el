<style data-mw-deduplicate="TemplateStyles:r1033289096">.mw-parser-output .hatnote{font-style:italic}.mw-parser-output div.hatnote{padding-left:1.6em;margin-bottom:0.5em}.mw-parser-output .hatnote i{font-style:normal}.mw-parser-output .hatnote+link+.hatnote{margin-top:-0.5em}</style><div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">This article is about the deities in Egyptian mythology. <p>In <a href="/wiki/Egyptian_mythology" title="Egyptian mythology">Egyptian mythology</a>, the <b>Ogdoad</b> (<a href="/wiki/Ancient_Greek_language" class="mw-redirect" title="Ancient Greek language">Ancient Greek</a>: <span lang="grc">ὀγδοάς</span> "the Eightfold"; <a href="/wiki/Ancient_Egyptian_language" class="mw-redirect" title="Ancient Egyptian language">Ancient Egyptian</a>: <i lang="egy">ḫmnyw</i>, a plural <a href="/wiki/Afroasiatic_languages#Nisba" title="Afroasiatic languages">nisba</a> of <span title="Ancient Egyptian-language text"><i lang="egy">ḫmnw</i></span> "eight") were eight <a href="/wiki/Primordial_deities" class="mw-redirect" title="Primordial deities">primordial deities</a> worshiped in <a href="/wiki/Hermopolis" title="Hermopolis">Hermopolis</a>.
</p><p>References to the Ogdoad date to the <a href="/wiki/Old_Kingdom_of_Egypt" title="Old Kingdom of Egypt">Old Kingdom of Egypt</a>, and even at the time of composition of the <a href="/wiki/Pyramid_Texts" title="Pyramid Texts">Pyramid Texts</a> toward the end of the Old Kingdom, they appear to have been antiquated and mostly forgotten by everyone except their theologians. They are frequently mentioned in the <a href="/wiki/Coffin_Texts" title="Coffin Texts">Coffin Texts</a> of the <a href="/wiki/Middle_Kingdom_of_Egypt" title="Middle Kingdom of Egypt">Middle Kingdom</a>. The oldest known pictorial representations of the group do not pre-date the time of <a href="/wiki/Seti_I" title="Seti I">Seti I</a> (<a href="/wiki/New_Kingdom_of_Egypt" title="New Kingdom of Egypt">New Kingdom</a>, thirteenth century BC), when the group appears to be rediscovered by the theologians of Hermopolis for the purposes of creating a more elaborate creation-account.<sup id="cite_ref-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-2">&#91;2&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>Texts of the <a href="/wiki/Late_Period_of_ancient_Egypt" title="Late Period of ancient Egypt">Late Period</a> describe them as having the heads of frogs (male) and serpents (female), and they are often depicted in this way in reliefs of the last dynasty, the <a href="/wiki/Ptolemaic_Kingdom" title="Ptolemaic Kingdom">Ptolemaic Kingdom</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-3">&#91;3&#93;</a></sup>
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Attributes">Attributes</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Ogdoad_(Egyptian)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Attributes">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The names of <a href="/wiki/Nu_(mythology)" title="Nu (mythology)">Nu and Naunet</a> are written with the determiners for <a href="/wiki/Sky_(hieroglyph)" title="Sky (hieroglyph)">sky</a> and <a href="/wiki/N-water_ripple_(n_hieroglyph)" class="mw-redirect" title="N-water ripple (n hieroglyph)">water</a>, and it seems clear that they represent the primordial waters.
</p><p><a href="/wiki/Huh_(god)" class="mw-redirect" title="Huh (god)">Ḥeḥu and Ḥeḥut</a> have no readily identifiable determiners; according to a suggestion due to <a href="/wiki/Heinrich_Karl_Brugsch" title="Heinrich Karl Brugsch">Brugsch</a> (1885), the names are associated with a term for an undefined or <a href="/wiki/Infinity_(philosophy)" title="Infinity (philosophy)">unlimited number</a>, <i>ḥeḥ</i>, suggesting a concept similar to the Greek <i><a href="/wiki/Aeon" title="Aeon">aion</a></i>. From the context of a number of passages in which Ḥeḥu is mentioned, however, Brugsch also suggested that the names may be a personification of the atmosphere between heaven and earth (c.f. <i><a href="/wiki/Shu_(Egyptian_god)" title="Shu (Egyptian god)">Shu</a></i>).
</p><p>The names of <a href="/wiki/Kek_(mythology)" title="Kek (mythology)">Kekui and Kekuit</a> are written with a determiner combining the <a href="/wiki/Sky_(hieroglyph)" title="Sky (hieroglyph)">sky hieroglyph</a> with a staff or scepter used for words related to darkness and obscurity, and <i>kkw</i> as a regular word means "darkness", suggesting that these gods represent <a href="/wiki/Darkness" title="Darkness">primordial darkness</a>, comparable to the Greek <a href="/wiki/Erebus" title="Erebus">Erebus</a>, but in some aspects they appear to represent day as well as night, or the change from night to day and from day to night.
</p><p>The fourth pair has no consistent attributes as it appears with varying names; sometimes the name <i>Qerḥ</i> is replaced by <i>Ni, Nenu, Nu, or <a href="/wiki/Amun" title="Amun">Amun</a></i>, and the name <i>Qerḥet</i> by <i>Ennit, Nenuit, Nunu, Nit, or <a href="/wiki/Amunet" title="Amunet">Amunet</a></i>. The common meaning of <i>qerḥ</i> is "night", but the determinative (D41 for "to halt, stop, deny") also suggests the principle of inactivity or repose.<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEBudge1904283–286_5-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEBudge1904283–286-5">&#91;5&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>There is no obvious way to allot or attribute four functions to the four pairs of deities, and it seems clear that "the ancient Egyptians themselves had no very clear idea" regarding such functions.<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEBudge1904287–288_6-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEBudge1904287–288-6">&#91;6&#93;</a></sup> Nevertheless, there have been attempts to assign "four ontological concepts"<sup id="cite_ref-7" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-7">&#91;7&#93;</a></sup>
to the four pairs.
</p><p>For example, in the context of the New Kingdom, <a href="/wiki/Maulana_Karenga" title="Maulana Karenga">Karenga</a> (2004) uses "fluidity" (for "flood, waters"),   "darkness", "unboundedness", and "invisibility" (or "repose, inactivity").<sup id="cite_ref-8" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-8">&#91;8&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
