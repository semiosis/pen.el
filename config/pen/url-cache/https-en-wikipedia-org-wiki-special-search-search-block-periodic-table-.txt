<p>A <b>block</b> of the <a href="/wiki/Periodic_table" title="Periodic table">periodic table</a> is a set of elements unified by the <a href="/wiki/Atomic_orbitals" class="mw-redirect" title="Atomic orbitals">atomic orbitals</a> their <a href="/wiki/Valence_electrons" class="mw-redirect" title="Valence electrons">valence electrons</a> or vacancies lie in.<sup id="cite_ref-Jensen2015_1-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Jensen2015-1">&#91;1&#93;</a></sup> The term seems to have been first used by <a href="/wiki/Charles_Janet" title="Charles Janet">Charles Janet</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-2">&#91;2&#93;</a></sup> Each block is named after its characteristic orbital: <a href="#s-block">s-block</a>, <a href="#p-block">p-block</a>, <a href="#d-block">d-block</a>, <a href="#f-block">f-block</a> and <a href="#g-block">g-block</a>.
</p><p>The block names (s, p, d, and f) are derived from the <a href="/wiki/Spectroscopic_notation" title="Spectroscopic notation">spectroscopic notation</a> for the value of an electron's <a href="/wiki/Azimuthal_quantum_number" title="Azimuthal quantum number">azimuthal quantum number</a>: sharp&#160;(0), principal&#160;(1), diffuse&#160;(2), and fundamental&#160;(3). Succeeding notations proceed in alphabetical order, as g, h, etc., though elements that would belong in such blocks have not yet been found.
</p>
<meta property="mw:PageProp/toc" />
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Characteristics">Characteristics</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Block_(periodic_table)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Characteristics"><span>edit</span></a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<style data-mw-deduplicate="TemplateStyles:r1210818076">.mw-parser-output .quotebox{background-color:#F9F9F9;border:1px solid #aaa;box-sizing:border-box;padding:10px;font-size:88%;max-width:100%}.mw-parser-output .quotebox.floatleft{margin:.5em 1.4em .8em 0}.mw-parser-output .quotebox.floatright{margin:.5em 0 .8em 1.4em}.mw-parser-output .quotebox.centered{overflow:hidden;position:relative;margin:.5em auto .8em auto}.mw-parser-output .quotebox.floatleft span,.mw-parser-output .quotebox.floatright span{font-style:inherit}.mw-parser-output .quotebox>blockquote{margin:0;padding:0;border-left:0;font-family:inherit;font-size:inherit}.mw-parser-output .quotebox-title{background-color:#F9F9F9;text-align:center;font-size:110%;font-weight:bold}.mw-parser-output .quotebox-quote>:first-child{margin-top:0}.mw-parser-output .quotebox-quote:last-child>:last-child{margin-bottom:0}.mw-parser-output .quotebox-quote.quoted:before{font-family:"Times New Roman",serif;font-weight:bold;font-size:large;color:gray;content:" “ ";vertical-align:-45%;line-height:0}.mw-parser-output .quotebox-quote.quoted:after{font-family:"Times New Roman",serif;font-weight:bold;font-size:large;color:gray;content:" ” ";line-height:0}.mw-parser-output .quotebox .left-aligned{text-align:left}.mw-parser-output .quotebox .right-aligned{text-align:right}.mw-parser-output .quotebox .center-aligned{text-align:center}.mw-parser-output .quotebox .quote-title,.mw-parser-output .quotebox .quotebox-quote{display:block}.mw-parser-output .quotebox cite{display:block;font-style:normal}@media screen and (max-width:640px){.mw-parser-output .quotebox{width:100%!important;margin:0 0 .8em!important;float:none!important}}</style><div class="quotebox pullquote floatleft" style="width:25em; ; background-color: Cornsilk;">
<blockquote class="quotebox-quote left-aligned" style="">
<p>The division into blocks is justified by their distinctive nature: s is characterized, except in H and He, by highly electropositive metals; p by a range of very distinctive metals and non-metals, many of them essential to life; d by metals with multiple oxidation states; f by metals so similar that their separation is problematic. Useful statements about the elements can be made on the basis of the block they belong to and their position in it, for example highest oxidation state, density, melting point&#160;... Electronegativity is rather systematically distributed across and between blocks.
</p>
</blockquote>
<p style="padding-bottom: 0em;"><cite class="right-aligned" style="">P. J. Stewart<br />In <i>Foundations of Chemistry,</i> 2017<sup id="cite_ref-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-3">&#91;3&#93;</a></sup></cite></p>
</div>
<p>There is an <i>approximate</i> correspondence between this nomenclature of blocks, based on <a href="/wiki/Electronic_configuration" class="mw-redirect" title="Electronic configuration">electronic configuration</a>, and sets of elements based on chemical properties. The s-block and p-block together are usually considered <a href="/wiki/Main-group_element" title="Main-group element">main-group elements</a>, the d-block corresponds to the <a href="/wiki/Transition_metal" title="Transition metal">transition metals</a>, and the f-block corresponds to the inner transition metals and encompasses nearly all of the <a href="/wiki/Lanthanide" title="Lanthanide">lanthanides</a> (like <a href="/wiki/Lanthanum" title="Lanthanum">lanthanum</a>, <a href="/wiki/Praseodymium" title="Praseodymium">praseodymium</a> and <a href="/wiki/Dysprosium" title="Dysprosium">dysprosium</a>) and the <a href="/wiki/Actinide" title="Actinide">actinides</a> (like <a href="/wiki/Actinium" title="Actinium">actinium</a>, <a href="/wiki/Uranium" title="Uranium">uranium</a> and <a href="/wiki/Einsteinium" title="Einsteinium">einsteinium</a>).
</p><p>The <a href="/wiki/Group_12_element" title="Group 12 element">group 12</a> elements <a href="/wiki/Zinc" title="Zinc">zinc</a>, <a href="/wiki/Cadmium" title="Cadmium">cadmium</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Mercury_(element)" title="Mercury (element)">mercury</a> are sometimes regarded as main group, rather than transition group, because they are chemically and physically more similar to the p-block elements than the other d-block elements. The <a href="/wiki/Group_3_element" title="Group 3 element">group 3</a> elements are occasionally considered main group elements due to their similarities to the s-block elements. However, they remain d-block elements even when considered to be main group.
</p><p>Groups (columns) in the f-block (between groups 2 and 3) are not numbered.
</p><p><a href="/wiki/Helium" title="Helium">Helium</a> is an <a href="/wiki/S-block" class="mw-redirect" title="S-block">s-block</a> element, with its outer (and only) electrons in the 1s <a href="/wiki/Atomic_orbital" title="Atomic orbital">atomic orbital</a>, although its chemical properties are more similar to the p-block <a href="/wiki/Noble_gas" title="Noble gas">noble gases</a> in group 18 due to its full shell.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="s-block">s-block</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Block_(periodic_table)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: s-block"><span>edit</span></a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<link rel="mw-deduplicated-inline-style" href="mw-data:TemplateStyles:r1210818076"><div class="quotebox pullquote floatright" style="width:25em; ; background-color: Cornsilk;">
<blockquote class="quotebox-quote left-aligned" style="">
<p>Na, K, Mg and Ca are essential in biological systems. Some&#160;... other s-block elements are used in medicine (e.g. Li and Ba) and/or occur as minor but useful contaminants in Ca bio-minerals e.g. Sr…These metals display only one stable oxidation state [+1 or +2]. This enables [their]&#160;... ions to move around the cell without…danger of being oxidised or reduced.
</p>
</blockquote>
<p style="padding-bottom: 0em;"><cite class="right-aligned" style="">Wilkins, R. G. and Wilkins, P. C. (2003) <br />
<i>The role of calcium and comparable cations in animal behaviour,</i> <a href="/wiki/Royal_Society_of_Chemistry" title="Royal Society of Chemistry">RSC</a>, Cambridge, p. 1</cite></p>
</div>
<p>The s-block, with the s standing for "sharp" and <a href="/wiki/Azimuthal_quantum_number" title="Azimuthal quantum number">azimuthal quantum number</a> 0, is on the left side of the conventional periodic table and is composed of elements from the first two columns plus one element in the rightmost column, the nonmetals <a href="/wiki/Hydrogen" title="Hydrogen">hydrogen</a> and <a href="/wiki/Helium" title="Helium">helium</a> and the <a href="/wiki/Alkali_metal" title="Alkali metal">alkali metals</a> (in group 1) and <a href="/wiki/Alkaline_earth_metal" title="Alkaline earth metal">alkaline earth metals</a> (group 2). Their general valence configuration is <i>n</i>s<sup>1–2</sup>. <a href="/wiki/Helium" title="Helium">Helium</a> is an s-element, but nearly always finds its place to the far right in <a href="/wiki/Noble_gases" class="mw-redirect" title="Noble gases">group&#160;18</a>, above the p-element <a href="/wiki/Neon" title="Neon">neon</a>. Each <a href="/wiki/Period_(periodic_table)" title="Period (periodic table)">row</a> of the table has two s-elements.
</p><p>The metals of the s-block (from <a href="/wiki/Period_2_element" title="Period 2 element">the second period</a> onwards) are mostly soft and have generally low melting and boiling points. Most impart colour to a flame.
</p><p>Chemically, all s-elements except <a href="/wiki/Helium" title="Helium">helium</a> are highly reactive. Metals of the s-block are highly electropositive and often form essentially ionic compounds with nonmetals, especially with the highly electronegative halogen nonmetals.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="p-block">p-block</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Block_(periodic_table)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: p-block"><span>edit</span></a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The p-block, with the p standing for "principal" and <a href="/wiki/Azimuthal_quantum_number" title="Azimuthal quantum number">azimuthal quantum number</a> 1, is on the right side of the standard periodic table and encompasses elements in groups 13 to 18. Their general electronic configuration is <i>n</i>s<sup>2</sup> <i>n</i>p<sup>1–6</sup>. <a href="/wiki/Helium" title="Helium">Helium</a>, though being the first element in group 18, is not included in the p-block. Each row of the table has a place for six p-elements except for <a href="/wiki/Period_1_element" title="Period 1 element">the first row</a> (which has none).
</p>

<p>This block is the only one having all three types of elements: <a href="/wiki/Metal" title="Metal">metals</a>, <a href="/wiki/Nonmetal_(chemistry)" class="mw-redirect" title="Nonmetal (chemistry)">nonmetals</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Metalloid" title="Metalloid">metalloids</a>. The p-block elements can be described on a group-by-group basis as: group 13, the <i><a href="/wiki/Icosagen" class="mw-redirect" title="Icosagen">icosagens</a></i>; 14, the <i><a href="/wiki/Crystallogen" class="mw-redirect" title="Crystallogen">crystallogens</a></i>; 15, the <i><a href="/wiki/Pnictogens" class="mw-redirect" title="Pnictogens">pnictogens</a></i>; 16, the <i><a href="/wiki/Chalcogens" class="mw-redirect" title="Chalcogens">chalcogens</a></i>; 17, the <i><a href="/wiki/Halogens" class="mw-redirect" title="Halogens">halogens</a></i>; and 18, the <i><a href="/wiki/Group_18" class="mw-redirect" title="Group 18">helium group</a></i>, composed of the <i><a href="/wiki/Noble_gases" class="mw-redirect" title="Noble gases">noble gases</a></i> (excluding helium) and <a href="/wiki/Oganesson" title="Oganesson">oganesson</a>. Alternatively, the p-block can be described as containing <i><a href="/wiki/Post-transition_metals" class="mw-redirect" title="Post-transition metals">post-transition metals</a></i>; <i>metalloids</i>; <i><a href="/wiki/Reactive_nonmetals" class="mw-redirect" title="Reactive nonmetals">reactive nonmetals</a></i> including the <i>halogens</i>; and <i>noble gases</i> (excluding helium).
</p><p>The p-block elements are unified by the fact that their valence (outermost) electrons are in the p orbital. The p orbital consists of six lobed shapes coming from a central point at evenly spaced angles. The p orbital can hold a maximum of six electrons, hence there are six columns in the p-block. Elements in column 13, the first column of the p-block, have one p-orbital electron. Elements in column 14, the second column of the p-block, have two p-orbital electrons. The trend continues this way until column 18, which has six p-orbital electrons.
</p><p>The block is a stronghold of the <a href="/wiki/Octet_rule" title="Octet rule">octet rule</a> in its first row, but elements in subsequent rows often display <a href="/wiki/Hypervalence" class="mw-redirect" title="Hypervalence">hypervalence</a>. The p-block elements show variable oxidation states usually differing by multiples of two. The reactivity of elements in a group generally decreases downwards. (Helium breaks this trend in group 18 by being more reactive than neon, but since helium is actually an s-block element, the p-block portion of the trend remains intact.)
</p><p>The bonding between metals and nonmetals depends on the electronegativity difference. Ionicity is possible when the electronegativity difference is high enough (e.g. <a href="/wiki/Lithium_nitride" title="Lithium nitride">Li<sub>3</sub>N</a>, <a href="/wiki/Sodium_chloride" title="Sodium chloride">NaCl</a>, <a href="/wiki/Lead(II)_oxide" title="Lead(II) oxide">PbO</a>). Metals in relatively high oxidation states tend to form covalent structures (e.g. <a href="/wiki/Tungsten_hexafluoride" title="Tungsten hexafluoride">WF<sub>6</sub></a>, <a href="/wiki/Osmium_tetroxide" title="Osmium tetroxide">OsO<sub>4</sub></a>, <a href="/wiki/Titanium_tetrachloride" title="Titanium tetrachloride">TiCl<sub>4</sub></a>, <a href="/wiki/Aluminium_chloride" title="Aluminium chloride">AlCl<sub>3</sub></a>), as do the more noble metals even in low oxidation states (e.g. <a href="/wiki/Gold(I)_chloride" title="Gold(I) chloride">AuCl</a>, <a href="/wiki/Mercury(II)_chloride" title="Mercury(II) chloride">HgCl<sub>2</sub></a>). There are also some metal oxides displaying <a href="/wiki/Electrical_conductivity" class="mw-redirect" title="Electrical conductivity">electrical (metallic) conductivity</a>, like <a href="/wiki/Ruthenium(IV)_oxide" title="Ruthenium(IV) oxide">RuO<sub>2</sub></a>, <a href="/wiki/Rhenium_trioxide" title="Rhenium trioxide">ReO<sub>3</sub></a>, and <a href="/wiki/Iridium(IV)_oxide" title="Iridium(IV) oxide">IrO<sub>2</sub></a>.<sup id="cite_ref-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-4">&#91;4&#93;</a></sup> The metalloids tend to form either covalent compounds or alloys with metals, though even then ionicity is possible with the most electropositive metals (e.g. <a href="/wiki/Magnesium_silicide" title="Magnesium silicide">Mg<sub>2</sub>Si</a>).
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="d-block">d-block</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Block_(periodic_table)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: d-block"><span>edit</span></a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<link rel="mw-deduplicated-inline-style" href="mw-data:TemplateStyles:r1210818076"><div class="quotebox pullquote floatright" style="width:25em; ; background-color: Cornsilk;">
<blockquote class="quotebox-quote left-aligned" style="">
<p>The<span class="nowrap">&#160;</span>... elements show a horizontal similarity in their physical and chemical properties as well as the usual vertical relationship. This horizontal similarity is so marked that the chemistry of the first<span class="nowrap">&#160;</span>... series<span class="nowrap">&#160;</span>... is often discussed separately from that of the second and third series, which are more similar to one another than to the first series.
</p>
</blockquote>
<p style="padding-bottom: 0em;"><cite class="right-aligned" style="">Kneen, W. R., Rogers, M. J. W., and Simpson, P. (1972)<br />
<i>Chemistry: Facts, patterns, and principles,</i> Addison-Wesley, London, pp. 487−489&#160;</cite></p>
</div>
<p>The d-block, with the d standing for "diffuse" and azimuthal quantum number 2, is in the middle of the periodic table and encompasses elements from groups 3 to 12; it starts in the <a href="/wiki/Period_4_element" title="Period 4 element">4th period</a>. Periods from the fourth onwards have a space for ten d-block elements. Most or all of these elements are also known as <a href="/wiki/Transition_metal" title="Transition metal">transition metals</a> because they occupy a transitional zone in properties, between the strongly electropositive metals of groups 1 and 2, and the weakly electropositive metals of groups 13 to 16. Group 3 or group 12, while still counted as d-block metals, are sometimes not counted as transition metals because they do not show the chemical properties characteristic of transition metals as much, for example, multiple <a href="/wiki/Oxidation_state" title="Oxidation state">oxidation states</a> and coloured compounds. 
</p><p>The d-block elements are all metals and most have one or more chemically active d-orbital electrons. Because there is a relatively small difference in the energy of the different d-orbital electrons, the number of electrons participating in chemical bonding can vary. The d-block elements have a tendency to exhibit two or more oxidation states, differing by multiples of one. The most <a href="/wiki/Template:List_of_oxidation_states_of_the_elements" title="Template:List of oxidation states of the elements">common oxidation states</a> are +2 and +3. <a href="/wiki/Chromium" title="Chromium">Chromium</a>, <a href="/wiki/Iron" title="Iron">iron</a>, <a href="/wiki/Molybdenum" title="Molybdenum">molybdenum</a>, <a href="/wiki/Ruthenium" title="Ruthenium">ruthenium</a>, <a href="/wiki/Tungsten" title="Tungsten">tungsten</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Osmium" title="Osmium">osmium</a> can have formal oxidation numbers as low as −4; <a href="/wiki/Iridium" title="Iridium">iridium</a> holds the singular distinction of being capable of achieving an oxidation state of +9.
</p><p>The d-orbitals (four shaped as <a href="/wiki/Four-leaf_clover" title="Four-leaf clover">four-leaf clovers</a>, and the fifth as a <a href="/wiki/Dumbbell" title="Dumbbell">dumbbell</a> with a ring around it) can contain up to five pairs of electrons.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="f-block">f-block</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Block_(periodic_table)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: f-block"><span>edit</span></a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<link rel="mw-deduplicated-inline-style" href="mw-data:TemplateStyles:r1210818076"><div class="quotebox pullquote floatright" style="width:25em; ; background-color: Cornsilk;">
<blockquote class="quotebox-quote left-aligned" style="">
<p>Because of their complex electronic structure, the significant electron correlation effects, and the large relativistic contributions,&#160;the f-block&#160;elements are probably the most challenging group of elements for electronic structure theory.&#160;
</p>
</blockquote>
<p style="padding-bottom: 0em;"><cite class="right-aligned" style="">Dolg, M., ed. (2015)<br />
<i>Computational method in lanthanide and actinide chemistry,</i> John Wiley &amp; Sons, Chichester, p. xvii</cite></p>
</div>
<p>The f-block, with the f standing for "fundamental" and azimuthal quantum number 3, appears as a footnote in a standard 18-column table but is located at the center-left of a 32-column full-width table, between groups 2 and 3. Periods from the sixth onwards have a place for fourteen f-block elements. These elements are generally not considered part of any <a href="/wiki/Group_(periodic_table)" title="Group (periodic table)">group</a>. They are sometimes called <a href="/wiki/Inner_transition_metal" class="mw-redirect" title="Inner transition metal">inner transition metals</a> because they provide a transition between the s-block and d-block in the <a href="/wiki/Period_6_element" title="Period 6 element">6th</a> and <a href="/wiki/Period_7_element" title="Period 7 element">7th</a> row (period), in the same way that the d-block <a href="/wiki/Transition_metal" title="Transition metal">transition metals</a> provide a transitional bridge between the s-block and p-block in the 4th and 5th rows.
</p><p>The f-block elements come in two series: <a href="/wiki/Lanthanum" title="Lanthanum">lanthanum</a> through <a href="/wiki/Ytterbium" title="Ytterbium">ytterbium</a> in period 6, and <a href="/wiki/Actinium" title="Actinium">actinium</a> through <a href="/wiki/Nobelium" title="Nobelium">nobelium</a> in period 7. All are metals. The f-orbital electrons are less active in the chemistry of the period 6 f-block elements, although they do make some contribution:<sup id="cite_ref-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-5">&#91;5&#93;</a></sup> these are rather similar to each other. They are more active in the early period 7 f-block elements, where the energies of the 5f, 7s, and 6d shells are quite similar; consequently these elements tend to show as much chemical variability as their transition metals analogues. The later period 7 f-block elements from about curium onwards behave more like their period 6 counterparts.
</p><p>The f-block elements are unified by mostly having one or more electrons in an inner f-orbital. Of the f-orbitals, six have six lobes each, and the seventh looks like a dumbbell with a donut with two rings. They can contain up to seven pairs of electrons; hence, the block occupies fourteen columns in the periodic table. They are not assigned group numbers, since vertical periodic trends cannot be discerned in a "group" of two elements. 
</p><p>The two 14-member rows of the f-block elements are sometimes confused with the <i><a href="/wiki/Lanthanides" class="mw-redirect" title="Lanthanides">lanthanides</a></i> and the <i><a href="/wiki/Actinides" class="mw-redirect" title="Actinides">actinides</a></i>, which are names for sets of elements based on chemical properties more so than electron configurations. Those sets have 15 elements rather than 14, extending into the first members of the d-block in their periods, <a href="/wiki/Lutetium" title="Lutetium">lutetium</a> and <a href="/wiki/Lawrencium" title="Lawrencium">lawrencium</a> respectively.
</p><p>In many periodic tables, the f-block is shifted one element to the right, so that lanthanum and actinium become d-block elements, and Ce–Lu and Th–Lr form the f-block tearing the d-block into two very uneven portions. This is a holdover from early erroneous measurements of electron configurations, in which the 4f shell was thought to complete its filling only at lutetium.<sup id="cite_ref-Jensen1982_6-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Jensen1982-6">&#91;6&#93;</a></sup> In fact ytterbium completes the 4f shell, and on this basis <a href="/wiki/Lev_Landau" title="Lev Landau">Lev Landau</a> and <a href="/wiki/Evgeny_Lifshitz" title="Evgeny Lifshitz">Evgeny Lifshitz</a> considered in 1948 that lutetium cannot correctly be considered an f-block element.<sup id="cite_ref-Landau_7-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Landau-7">&#91;7&#93;</a></sup> Since then, physical, chemical, and electronic evidence has overwhelmingly supported that the f-block contains the elements La–Yb and Ac–No,<sup id="cite_ref-Jensen1982_6-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Jensen1982-6">&#91;6&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-Fluck_8-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Fluck-8">&#91;8&#93;</a></sup> as shown here and as supported by <a href="/wiki/International_Union_of_Pure_and_Applied_Chemistry" title="International Union of Pure and Applied Chemistry">International Union of Pure and Applied Chemistry</a> reports dating from 1988<sup id="cite_ref-Fluck_8-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Fluck-8">&#91;8&#93;</a></sup> and 2021.<sup id="cite_ref-2021IUPAC_9-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-2021IUPAC-9">&#91;9&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="g-block">g-block</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Block_(periodic_table)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: g-block"><span>edit</span></a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>A g-block, with azimuthal quantum number 4, is predicted to begin in the vicinity of <a href="/wiki/Unbiunium" title="Unbiunium">element 121</a>. Though g-orbitals are not expected to start filling in the ground state until around element <a href="/wiki/Unbiquadium" title="Unbiquadium">124</a>–<a href="/wiki/Unbihexium" title="Unbihexium">126</a> (see <a href="/wiki/Extended_periodic_table" title="Extended periodic table">extended periodic table</a>), they are likely already low enough in energy to start participating chemically in element 121,<sup id="cite_ref-10" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-10">&#91;10&#93;</a></sup> similar to the situation of the 4f and 5f orbitals.
</p><p>If the trend of the previous rows continued, then the g-block would have eighteen elements. However, calculations predict a very strong blurring of periodicity in the eighth period, to the point that individual blocks become hard to delineate. It is likely that the eighth period will not quite follow the trend of previous rows.<sup id="cite_ref-recentattempts_11-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-recentattempts-11">&#91;11&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
