<dt class="term-meaning">Meaning</dt>
<dd class="def-meaning">Face Of God</dd>
<dt class="term-etymology">Etymology</dt>
<dd class="def-etymology">From (1) the verb <span class="HebF">&#x05E4;&#x05E0;&#x05D4;</span> (<i>pana</i>), to turn, and (2) the word <span class="HebF">&#x05D0;&#x05DC;</span> (<i>'el</i>), God.</dd>

<dd class="def-related">&bull; Via <span class="HebF">&#x05D0;&#x05DC;</span> (<i>'el</i>): See the 'browse by form' menu for a long list of <i>'el</i>-names.</dd>

</div><h2 id="anc-2" onClick="fold(this.id)"><span id="foldic-2" class="foldIcon">&#128316;</span>The name Peniel in the Bible</h2><div class="h2rule"></div><div id="par-2" class="unfold"><p>
There are one place and one man named Peniel in the Bible, although the man's name is usually turned into Penuel (<span class="HebF">&#x05E4;&#x05E0;&#x05D5;&#x05D0;&#x05DC;</span>):</p><ul>
<li>The place called Peniel is located near the brook <a class="reg" href="Jabbok.html">Jabbok</a>, where <a class="reg" href="Jacob.html">Jacob</a> wrestled the angel of <a class="reg" href="YHWH.html">YHWH</a> and prevailed (Genesis 32:30). The angel called Jacob <a class="reg" href="Israel.html">Israel</a>, and Israel called the place Peniel, and the author of the story in turn called Peniel Penuel (Genesis 32:31). The story of <a class="reg" href="Gideon.html">Gideon</a> features a town called Penuel, and it's generally assumed that it's the same one. After having been insulted, Gideon kills the men of Penuel and destroys their tower (Judges 9:17). But because of its strategic position, king <a class="reg" href="Jeroboam.html">Jeroboam</a> of <a class="reg" href="Judah.html">Judah</a> fortified it centuries later (1 Kings 12:25).</li>
<li>The man called Peniel is a son of Shashak, son of <a class="reg" href="Elpaal.html">Elpaal</a>, son of <a class="reg" href="Shaharaim.html">Shaharaim</a> of <a class="reg" href="Benjamin.html">Benjamin</a> and <a class="reg" href="Hushim.html">Hushim</a> (1 Chronicles 8:25). Ancient <a class="reg" href="Jew.html">Jewish</a> commentators believed that Peniel was a mistake and noted that it should be read Penuel (see for a possible reason why our article on the name Penuel). All English translations followed this old Jewish advice and tell of Penuel, the son of Shashak.</li></ul>
</div><h2 id="anc-3" onClick="fold(this.id)"><span id="foldic-3" class="foldIcon">&#128316;</span>Etymology of the name Peniel</h2><div class="h2rule"></div><div id="par-3" class="unfold"><p>
The name Penuel consists of two (or rather three) elements, the final one being <span class="HebF">&#x05D0;&#x05DC;</span> (<a class="reg" href="El.html">El</a>), the prominent <a class="reg" href="Canaan.html">Canaanite</a> deity, whose name became applied to the God of Israel, or the common abbreviation of <a class="reg" href="Elohim.html">Elohim</a>, the genus God:</p>
<div class="Dictionary"><div class="dictHead">Excerpted from: Abarim Publications' Biblical Dictionary</div><div class="h2rule"></div>
<h6>&#x05D0;&#x05DC;&nbsp;&nbsp;&#x05D0;&#x05DC;&#x05D4;</h6><p>In names <span class="HebF">&#x05D0;&#x05DC;</span> (<i>'el</i>) usually refers to <span class="HebF">&#x05D0;&#x05DC;&#x05D4;&#x05D9;&#x05DD;</span> (<i>'elohim</i>), that is Elohim, or God, also known as <span class="HebF">&#x05D0;&#x05DC;&#x05D4;</span> (<a href="../Dictionary/a/a-l-he.html" class="reg"><i>'eloah</i></a>). In English, the words 'God' and 'god' exclusively refer to the deity but in <a href="Hebrew.html" class="reg">Hebrew</a> the words <span class="HebF">&#x05D0;&#x05DC;</span> (<i>'l</i>) and <span class="HebF">&#x05D0;&#x05DC;&#x05D4;</span> (<i>'lh</i>) are far more common and may express approach and negation, acts of wailing and pointing, and may even mean oak or terebinth.</p>
<div class="dictHead1"><a href="../Dictionary/a/a-l.html" class="button2"> &mdash; See the full Dictionary article &mdash; </a></div></div>
<p>The first part of our name comes from the verb <span class="HebF">&#x05E4;&#x05E0;&#x05D4;</span> (<i>pana</i>), meaning to turn:</p>
<div class="Dictionary"><div class="dictHead">Excerpted from: Abarim Publications' Biblical Dictionary</div><div class="h2rule"></div>
<h6>&#x05E4;&#x05E0;&#x05D4;</h6><p>The verb <span class="HebF">&#x05E4;&#x05E0;&#x05D4;</span> (<i>pana</i>) means to turn toward. Adverb <span class="HebF">&#x05E4;&#x05E0;&#x05D9;&#x05DE;&#x05D4;</span> (<i>penima</i>) means toward the inside. Adjective <span class="HebF">&#x05E4;&#x05E0;&#x05D9;&#x05DE;&#x05D9;</span> (<i>penimi</i>) means inner.</p><p>
This verb's primary derivation is the plural noun <span class="HebF">&#x05E4;&#x05E0;&#x05D9;&#x05DD;</span> (<i>panim</i>), literally turnings or inclinations. It's the common Biblical word for face and may also be used to mean scope, sight, surface, and so on.</p><p>
The noun <span class="HebF">&#x05E4;&#x05E0;&#x05D4;</span> (<i>pinna</i>) means corner, and is commonly used to describe where a wall makes a turn. This noun is thought to have derived from a by-form or parental form of the verb <span class="HebF">&#x05E4;&#x05E0;&#x05D4;</span> (<i>pana</i>), namely <span class="HebF">&#x05E4;&#x05E0;&#x05DF;</span> (<i>panan</i>), of similar meaning.</p><p>A second derivation of this form is the plural noun <span class="HebF">&#x05E4;&#x05E0;&#x05D9;&#x05E0;&#x05D9;&#x05DD;</span> (<i>peninim</i>), which describes a red coral, probably with swirling branches or otherwise corrugated structure.</p>
<div class="dictHead1"><a href="../Dictionary/p/p-n-he.html" class="button2"> &mdash; See the full Dictionary article &mdash; </a></div></div>
<p>These two elements are tied together by the letter <span class="HebF">&#x05D9;</span> (<a class="reg" href="../Dictionary/y/y.html"><i>yod</i></a>), which in this case creates a possessive form out of the first part: <i>pana</i>-of.
</p></div><h2 id="anc-4" onClick="fold(this.id)"><span id="foldic-4" class="foldIcon">&#128316;</span>Peniel meaning</h2><div class="h2rule"></div><div id="par-4" class="unfold"><p>
For a meaning of the name Peniel, NOBSE Study Bible Name List reads <b>The Face Of God</b>, and Jones' Dictionary of Old Testament Proper Names and BDB Theological Dictionary both read <b>Face Of God</b>.
</p>
