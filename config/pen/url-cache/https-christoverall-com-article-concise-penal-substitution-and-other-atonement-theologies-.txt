			<p>Trying to state all that our Lord Jesus achieved in his glorious work is difficult given its multi-faceted aspects. John Calvin sought to grasp the comprehensive nature of Christâ€™s work by the <em>munus triplexâ€”</em>Christâ€™s threefold office as our new covenant head and mediatorâ€”prophet, priest, and king. What Calvin sought to avoid was reductionism, the â€œcardinalâ€ sin of theology. Yet, although there is a danger in prioritizing one aspect of our Lordâ€™s work, Scripture does stress the centrality of Christâ€™s priestly office and his sacrificial death for our sins (Matt. 1:21; 1 Cor. 15:3â€“4). And given the centrality of Christâ€™s cross, it is crucial that we explain it correctly.</p>
<p>However, one problem we face is that, throughout church history, there have been a number of atonement theologies. Unlike the ecumenical confessions of Nicaea and Chalcedon that established orthodox Trinitarian and Christological doctrine, there is no catholic confession regarding the cross. From this fact, some have concluded that no one view best explains what is central to the crossâ€”a conclusion I reject. The truth is that despite an ecumenical confession, all Christians have agreed that Christâ€™s death â€œis for our sinsâ€ (1 Cor. 15:3) resulting in our reconciliation with God.</p>
<p>While conceptual clarity of the doctrine occurred over time, similar to other doctrines, clarity and precision was achieved, as various atonement debates occurred. Specifically, it was during the Reformation and post-Reformation eras, building on the work of people like Anselm, that conceptual clarity occurred in the articulation of penal substitution as the best <em>theological</em> explanation for <em>why </em>the cross was necessary and <em>what</em> it achieved.</p>
<p>Recently, however, some have challenged the claim that penal substitution best explains what is central to Christâ€™s cross. We are told repeatedly that penal substitution does not account for the richness of the cross. What is needed is not <em>one </em>view but multiple views. Is this correct? My thesis is that it is <em>not</em>, and for at least two reasons. First, views other than penal substitution fail to grasp the central problem that the cross remedies, namely our sin <em>before God</em>. Second, from another angle, other views stress various legitimate <em>results</em> of the cross, but without penal substitution as the foundation, the results alone cannot explain the central problem of our sin before God. Before developing these two points, let me first describe the basic atonement views set over against penal substitution.</p>
<h2>Atonement Views in Historical Theology</h2>
<p>Over the centuries, five main atonement theologies have been given:</p>
<h3><em>Recapitulation </em></h3>
<p>First, there is the recapitulation view, often associated with Irenaeus and Athanasius. This view interprets Christâ€™s work primarily in terms of his identification with us through the incarnation. By becoming human, the divine Son reversed what Adam did by living our life and dying our death. Adamâ€™s disobedience resulted in the corruption of our nature and the deprivation of Godlikeness. Christ reverses both of these results in his incarnation and entire cross-work. Especially in Christâ€™s resurrection, immortality and reconciliation with God is restored to us. This view captures much biblical truth. Christâ€™s work is presented in representational and substitutionary terms. But its <em>central</em> focus is on sinâ€™s effects on us and Christâ€™s restorative work, <em>not</em> on our sin before God <em>and</em> the need for Christ to satisfy Godâ€™s own righteous demand against us by paying for our sin.</p>
<h3><em>Christus Victor (Or Ransom)</em></h3>
<p>Second, <em>Christus Victor </em>is another view of the cross, often associated with the ransom theory to Satan. The primary object(s) of Christâ€™s death is (are) the powers which he liberates us from, namely, sin, death, and Satan. Like recapitulation, this view captures a lot of biblical truth, especially Christâ€™s defeat of the powers (Gen. 3:15; John 12:31â€“33; Col. 2:13â€“15; Heb. 2:14â€“16; Rev. 12:1â€“12), but unlike penal substitution, God is <em>not</em> viewed as the primary object of the cross.</p>
<h3><em>Moral Influence (or Example)</em></h3>
<p>Third, the moral influence view was promoted within non-orthodox theology. It had its roots in the theology of Peter Abelard (1079â€“1142), but came into its own with the rise of classic liberal theology (eighteenth to nineteenth centuries). It taught that Godâ€™s love is more basic than his justice and that God can forgive our sins without Christ satisfying divine justice. God is <em>not</em> the primary object of the cross. Instead, Christâ€™s death reveals Godâ€™s love and sets an example for us.</p>
<h3><em>The Governmental View</em></h3>
<p>Fourth, the governmental view arose in the post-Reformation era and it is identified with Hugo Grotius, John Miley, and the Arminian tradition. Against penal substitution, this view denies that Godâ€™s justice <em>necessitates</em> the full payment of our sin since Godâ€™s justice is not viewed as <em>essential </em>to him. Instead, as the moral Governor, God can choose to relax the demand of the law (since it is external to him) similar to a human judge, thus forgiving us by his mercy. Yet, God cannot completely overlook our sin; he governs the world justly and sin is a serious miscarriage of justice. So, Christâ€™s cross upholds the moral governance of the world allowing God to forgive us without a full payment of our sin, while it also simultaneously reveals Godâ€™s hatred of sin thus motivating us to repent and believe in Christ.</p>
<h3><em>Penal Substitution</em></h3>
<p>Fifth, there is penal substitution. It had precursors in the early church and Anselm, but came to full-bloom in the Reformation. Penal substitution does <em>not </em>minimize the multi-faceted aspects of Christâ€™s death. Instead, it contends that <em>central </em>to the cross is God the Son incarnate acting as our new covenant representative and substitute to fully satisfy Godâ€™s righteous demand against us due to our sin. And apart from this <em>central</em> achievement of the cross, there is no restoration of humanity, defeat of the powers, or revealed love.</p>
<p>Why? For this important reason: central to penal substitution is a specific understanding of the God-law-sin relationship, or better, a specific <em>doctrine of God</em>. Penal substitution takes seriously the fact that the triune God is alone independent and self-sufficient. Specifically, in relation to the moral law, this entails that God does not adjudicate a law external to him; instead, he <em>is the law</em>. This is why, in relation to sin, God <em>cannot</em> tolerate sin (Hab. 1:12â€“13; Isa. 1:4â€“20; 35:8); he <em>must </em>act in holy justice against it (Gen. 18:25) because God cannot deny himself.</p>
<p>Yet, how does God demonstrate his holy justice <em>and</em> covenant love, given his free decision to redeem us (Gen. 3:15; Hos. 11:9)? In this regard, in the Bibleâ€™s unfolding story a tension results that is rooted in <em>who </em>God is in relation to our sinâ€”and which is <em>central</em> to the <em>why</em> of the cross. Since God <em>is</em> the Law, he <em>cannot</em> forgive us without the full satisfaction of his holy demand (Rom. 3:21â€“26; Heb. 9:15â€“22). To justify the ungodly (Rom. 4:5), the triune God must take the initiative to provide a Redeemer who can act in perfect covenantal obedience for us <em>and </em>pay for our sin. Christ must not only be our victor and substitute, but our <em>penal</em> substitute. Ultimately, this is what is <em>central</em> to the cross, and other views of the atonement fail to stress this vital point.</p>
<h2>Evaluating Other Views of the Cross in Relation to Penal Substitution</h2>
<p>Let us now return to my thesis. Various atonement theologies emphasize many biblical truths and entailments of the cross, but unlike penal substitution, they fail to grasp the <em>central</em> problem that the cross remedies, namely our sin <em>before God</em>.</p>
<p>Every view of the atonement stresses something true about the cross. Recapitulation rightly grasps the Adam-Christ relationship across redemptive history (Rom. 5:12â€“21). In the first Adam, God demanded covenantal obedience, yet sadly, Adam disobeyed and brought sin and death into the world. What is needed is the incarnation of Godâ€™s Son, the last Adam, to live and die for us, and thus restore us to the purpose of our creation (Heb. 2:5â€“18). Yet, this view does not emphasize enough that God requires perfect covenantal obedience from us, and the need for Christ to be our representative in life <em>and</em> penal substitute in death. It rightly stresses many biblical truths but fails to grasp adequately the God-law-sin relationship.</p>
<p>Similarly, <em>Christus Victor</em> emphasizes many glorious truths. It underscores the need for the Son to become human to crush Satanâ€™s head (Gen. 3:15) and to defeat sin (Heb. 9:26) and death (Rom. 6:9). Yet, this view does not make central the God-law-sin relationship. In Scripture, the powers of sin, death, and Satan are only over us because of our <em>sin before God</em> (Gen. 2:17; Rom. 6:23; Heb. 2:14â€“15). After all, <em>why</em> did the divine Son <em>have to</em> <em>die </em>to defeat the powers? Why not simply exert his divine power to defeat them without the cross? Scriptureâ€™s answer is clear: itâ€™s only when our sin is paid for that the powers are destroyed (Col. 2:13â€“15; 1 Cor. 15:55â€“57). This is why our greatest problem is not the powers against us, but how we can stand justified before God. <em>Christus Victor</em> without penal substitution hangs in mid-air.</p>
<p>Scripture also presents Christ and his cross as the supreme moral example of love, obedience, and suffering (John 13:12â€“17; Eph. 5:1â€“2, 25â€“27; Phil. 2:5â€“11; 1 Pet. 2:18â€“25). But the cross only functions this way because of <em>who </em>dies and <em>what </em>he achieves, namely the full satisfaction of Godâ€™s holy demand against our sin, which is the very demonstration of divine love (1 John 4:7â€“10). Also, itâ€™s never enough for Christ merely to identify with us in his incarnation and show us how to live. Solidarity is not atonement; it is only the prerequisite. We need more than an example to redeem us. What we need is for the divine Son to live and die <em>for</em> us. Our problem is not that we need a great teacher to show us how to live. Our problem is sin before the triune holy God, and <em>this </em>problem requires the enfleshment of Godâ€™s own Son to live for us and to die for us as our penal substitute. It is only as our propitiatory sacrifice that <em>Godâ€™s</em> own righteous demand is fully met, and only in Christ do we receive all the glorious benefits of his new covenant work.</p>
<p>Of all the atonement views, only penal substitution best captures the God-centered nature of the cross. The alternatives either minimize or deny 1) that Godâ€™s holy justice is essential to him, 2) why our sin is first against <em>God</em> (Ps. 51:4), and 3) why Christ as our penal substitute is <em>central</em> to the cross. Before we can speak of the horizontal results of the cross (e.g., moral example, inter-personal reconciliation, etc.), we must first speak of the vertical: namely the triune God, in his Son, taking his own demand on himself so that we, in Christ, may be justified before him (Rom. 5:1â€“2). The other views miss this point. For them, the object of the cross is either our sin (forms of recapitulation), or Satan (ransom theory), or the powers (forms of <em>Christus Victor</em>). But what they fail to see is that the primary person we have sinned against is God, and as such, the ultimate object of the cross is God himself.</p>
<p>The Bibleâ€™s presentation of the cross is rich and multifaceted. It is like a beautiful gem that can be looked at from many angles. Yet, the explanation of the crossâ€™s <em>central</em> means is that Christ Jesus has come as our mediator and new covenant head to offer himself before God on behalf of sin. Penal substitution best accounts for <em>why </em>the divine Son <em>had </em>to die, and why <em>he </em>alone saves. With Paul, may we alone glory in and preach Christ crucified (1 Cor. 1:23).</p>
