<p><b>Imparted righteousness</b>, in <a href="/wiki/Methodism" title="Methodism">Methodist</a> <a href="/wiki/Theology" title="Theology">theology</a>,  is that <a href="/wiki/Divine_grace" title="Divine grace">gracious</a> gift of God given at the moment of the <a href="/wiki/Born_again_(Christianity)" class="mw-redirect" title="Born again (Christianity)">new birth</a> which enables a <a href="/wiki/Disciple_(Christianity)" title="Disciple (Christianity)">Christian disciple</a> to strive for <a href="/wiki/Sacred" class="mw-redirect" title="Sacred">holiness</a> and <a href="/wiki/Sanctification" class="mw-redirect" title="Sanctification">sanctification</a>. <a href="/wiki/John_Wesley" title="John Wesley">John Wesley</a> believed that imparted <a href="/wiki/Righteousness" title="Righteousness">righteousness</a> worked in tandem with <a href="/wiki/Imputed_righteousness" title="Imputed righteousness">imputed righteousness</a>. <i>Imputed righteousness</i> is the righteousness of <a href="/wiki/Jesus" title="Jesus">Jesus</a> credited to the Christian, enabling the Christian to be <a href="/wiki/Justification_(theology)" title="Justification (theology)">justified</a>; imparted righteousness is what God does in Christ by the power of the <a href="/wiki/Holy_Spirit" title="Holy Spirit">Holy Spirit</a> <i>after</i> justification, working in the Christian to enable and empower the process of sanctification (and, in Wesleyan thought, <a href="/wiki/Christian_perfection" title="Christian perfection">Christian perfection</a>).
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Hymnody_and_other_sources">Hymnody and other sources</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Imparted_righteousness&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Hymnody and other sources"><span>edit</span></a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Charles Wesley believed in imparted righteousness. This comes through in the Wesleyan hymnody such as his famous hymn "<a href="/wiki/And_can_it_be" class="mw-redirect" title="And can it be">And can it be</a>". The last verse reads:
</p><p>No condemnation now I dread;
Jesus, and all in Him, is mine&#160;!
Alive in Him, my living Head,
And clothed in righteousness divine,
Bold I approach the eternal throne,
And claim the crown, through Christ my own.
</p><p>Clothed in righteousness divine. Ephesians 6:14 [TNIV] says "Stand firm then with the belt of truth buckled around your waist, with the <i>breastplate of righteousness</i> in place...."
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Protestant_distinctive_in_imparted_righteousness">Protestant distinctive in imparted righteousness</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Imparted_righteousness&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Protestant distinctive in imparted righteousness"><span>edit</span></a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Preachers and theologians from various Protestant traditions (not only Wesleyan) use the term "imparted righteousness" to identify the righteous principle imparted by God to believers when He <a href="/wiki/Regeneration_(theology)" title="Regeneration (theology)">regenerates</a> them. Believers thereby become "partakers of the divine nature" (cf. 2 Peter 1:4). It is this principle of righteousness imparted to men in regeneration which is ever in conflict with the <a href="/wiki/Fall_of_Man" class="mw-redirect" title="Fall of Man">old Adamic nature</a>. Protestants, however, maintain the distinction between the "imputed righteousness" of Christ which is the basis for  <a href="/wiki/Justification_(theology)" title="Justification (theology)">justification</a> and the "imparted righteousness" which is the basis for subsequent <a href="/wiki/Sanctification" class="mw-redirect" title="Sanctification">sanctification</a>.
</p><p>It is somewhat problematic for some Christians (notably Calvinists) to call the doctrine "imparted righteousness," for that which is imparted is a righteous <i>principle</i> into man's nature, not <i>righteousness</i> per se.  Care must be taken in using the term <i>imparted</i> righteousness because it is sometimes confused with and sometimes intentionally used to refer to the Roman Catholic doctrine of <i><a href="/wiki/Infused_righteousness" title="Infused righteousness">infused righteousness</a>,</i> which in Catholicism is the basis for justification.
</p>
<h2><span id="The_.22NPP.22_case_against_both_Imputed_and_Infused_Righteousness"></span><span class="mw-headline" id="The_&quot;NPP&quot;_case_against_both_Imputed_and_Infused_Righteousness">The <a href="/wiki/New_Perspective_on_Paul" title="New Perspective on Paul">"NPP"</a> case against both Imputed and Infused Righteousness</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Imparted_righteousness&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: The &quot;NPP&quot; case against both Imputed and Infused Righteousness"><span>edit</span></a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>This section is a precis of <a href="/wiki/N._T._Wright" title="N. T. Wright">N. T. Wright</a>'s work in "What Saint Paul Really Said".<sup id="cite_ref-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-1">&#91;1&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p><a href="/wiki/N._T._Wright" title="N. T. Wright">N. T. Wright</a>, who is one of the best-known advocates of the <a href="/wiki/New_Perspective_on_Paul" title="New Perspective on Paul">New Perspective on Paul</a>, explains that although the "righteousness of God" and "righteousness from God" have been confused and conflated in the past, they are distinct concepts. He relates the court-room metaphor, pointing out that there are three parties in the Hebrew court - two parties in disagreement and one judge (there is no "Prosecuting Attorney"). The judge decides the outcome of the dispute between the parties, declaring one to be correct and the other incorrect. The one who is declared "correct" in court is called "righteous" in the matter that was judged.
</p><p>The "righteousness of God", referring to God's (the judge's) faithfulness to the covenant relationship, <i>can be neither imputed nor imparted to anybody</i> but refers only to His role as judge.
</p><p>"Righteousness from God" is roughly equivalent to "vindication", meaning that God is pronouncing that particular party to be correct/vindicated/righteous/acquitted in their dispute with the other party.
</p><p>The dispute in question in Christian theology is between those of faith (in God's promises: the covenant, the Messiah), and "the wicked", meaning everybody opposed to those of faith. Paul was positing that the people of such faith are vindicated when Messiah returns, being declared "righteous" (or in other words, vindicated for their stance), which is exactly the meaning of the Biblical term "justified" in <a href="/wiki/N._T._Wright" title="N. T. Wright">N. T. Wright</a>'s view.
</p><p>This means that we do not "receive" the righteousness of God (or as often expressed, "of Jesus") as in the classical Evangelical vernacular, nor is it "infused" as stated in the classical Roman Catholic vernacular. The "righteousness of God" remains His alone, and our "righteousness from God" means that we are found to be "of" the people of God. Paul's argument is that it has always been so, but what has changed is that the Messiah, in <a href="/wiki/Jesus_of_Nazareth" class="mw-redirect" title="Jesus of Nazareth">Jesus of Nazareth</a>, has now appeared.
</p><p>An important verse to note is 2 Cor 5:21, "For our sake he made him to be sin who knew no sin, so that in him we might become the righteousness of God." (ESV), which has traditionally been interpreted to mean that the Christian has, in some way, become righteous (by infusion or imputation), in exchange for Jesus' sinlessness. In fact, <a href="/wiki/N._T._Wright" title="N. T. Wright">N. T. Wright</a> says, Paul is speaking here <i>of the apostles</i>, and pointing out that <i>in their role as apostles</i>, their activity is effectively God's righteousness (covenant faithfulness) in action ("we are ambassadors for Christ, God making his appeal through us. We implore you on behalf of Christ, be reconciled to God" - vv 20–21). This meaning is natural when taken in context from verse 11 through 21.
</p>
