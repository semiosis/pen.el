					<div id="mw-content-text" class="mw-body-content"><div class="mw-content-ltr mw-parser-output" lang="en" dir="ltr"><div class="shortdescription nomobile noexcerpt noprint searchaux" style="display:none">Taxonomic rank directly above species</div>
<style data-mw-deduplicate="TemplateStyles:r1236090951">.mw-parser-output .hatnote{font-style:italic}.mw-parser-output div.hatnote{padding-left:1.6em;margin-bottom:0.5em}.mw-parser-output .hatnote i{font-style:normal}.mw-parser-output .hatnote+link+.hatnote{margin-top:-0.5em}@media print{body.ns-0 .mw-parser-output .hatnote{display:none!important}}</style><div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">For other uses, see <a href="/wiki/Genus_(disambiguation)" class="mw-disambig" title="Genus (disambiguation)">Genus (disambiguation)</a>.</div>
<link rel="mw-deduplicated-inline-style" href="mw-data:TemplateStyles:r1236090951"><div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">"Genera" redirects here. <p><b>Genus</b> (<span class="rt-commentedText nowrap"><span class="IPA nopopups noexcerpt" lang="en-fonipa"><a href="/wiki/Help:IPA/English" title="Help:IPA/English">/<span style="border-bottom:1px dotted"><span title="/ˈ/: primary stress follows">ˈ</span><span title="/dʒ/: &#39;j&#39; in &#39;jam&#39;">dʒ</span><span title="/iː/: &#39;ee&#39; in &#39;fleece&#39;">iː</span><span title="&#39;n&#39; in &#39;nigh&#39;">n</span><span title="/ə/: &#39;a&#39; in &#39;about&#39;">ə</span><span title="&#39;s&#39; in &#39;sigh&#39;">s</span></span>/</a></span></span> <abbr title="plural form">pl.</abbr>: <b>genera</b> <span class="rt-commentedText nowrap"><span class="IPA nopopups noexcerpt" lang="en-fonipa"><a href="/wiki/Help:IPA/English" title="Help:IPA/English">/<span style="border-bottom:1px dotted"><span title="/ˈ/: primary stress follows">ˈ</span><span title="/dʒ/: &#39;j&#39; in &#39;jam&#39;">dʒ</span><span title="/ɛ/: &#39;e&#39; in &#39;dress&#39;">ɛ</span><span title="&#39;n&#39; in &#39;nigh&#39;">n</span><span title="/ər/: &#39;er&#39; in &#39;letter&#39;">ər</span><span title="/ə/: &#39;a&#39; in &#39;about&#39;">ə</span></span>/</a></span></span>) is a <a href="/wiki/Taxonomic_rank" title="Taxonomic rank">taxonomic rank</a> above <a href="/wiki/Species" title="Species">species</a> and below <a href="/wiki/Family_(taxonomy)" class="mw-redirect" title="Family (taxonomy)">family</a> as used in the <a href="/wiki/Biological_classification" class="mw-redirect" title="Biological classification">biological classification</a> of <a href="/wiki/Extant_taxon" class="mw-redirect" title="Extant taxon">living</a> and <a href="/wiki/Fossil" title="Fossil">fossil</a> organisms as well as <a href="/wiki/Virus_classification#ICTV_classification" title="Virus classification">viruses</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-ICTV_1-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-ICTV-1"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>1<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup> In <a href="/wiki/Binomial_nomenclature" title="Binomial nomenclature">binomial nomenclature</a>, the genus name forms the first part of the binomial species name for each species within the genus.
</p>
<dl><dd>E.g. <i><a href="/wiki/Panthera_leo" class="mw-redirect" title="Panthera leo">Panthera leo</a></i> (lion) and <i><a href="/wiki/Panthera_onca" class="mw-redirect" title="Panthera onca">Panthera onca</a></i> (jaguar) are two species within the genus <i><a href="/wiki/Panthera" title="Panthera">Panthera</a></i>. <i>Panthera</i> is a genus within the family <a href="/wiki/Felidae" title="Felidae">Felidae</a>.</dd></dl>
<p>The composition of a genus is determined by <a href="/wiki/Taxonomy_(biology)" title="Taxonomy (biology)">taxonomists</a>. The standards for genus classification are not strictly codified, so different authorities often produce different classifications for genera. There are some general practices used, however,<sup id="cite_ref-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-2"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>2<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-3"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>3<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup> including the idea that a newly defined genus should fulfill these three criteria to be descriptively useful:
</p>
<ol><li><a href="/wiki/Monophyly" title="Monophyly">monophyly</a> – all descendants of an ancestral <a href="/wiki/Taxon" title="Taxon">taxon</a> are grouped together (i.e. <a href="/wiki/Phylogenetics" title="Phylogenetics">phylogenetic</a> analysis should clearly demonstrate both monophyly and validity as a separate lineage).</li>
<li>reasonable compactness – a genus should not be expanded needlessly.</li>
<li>distinctness – with respect to evolutionarily relevant criteria, i.e. <a href="/wiki/Ecology" title="Ecology">ecology</a>, <a href="/wiki/Morphology_(biology)" title="Morphology (biology)">morphology</a>, or <a href="/wiki/Biogeography" title="Biogeography">biogeography</a>; <a href="/wiki/DNA_sequences" class="mw-redirect" title="DNA sequences">DNA sequences</a> are a <i>consequence</i> rather than a <i>condition</i> of diverging evolutionary lineages except in cases where they directly inhibit <a href="/wiki/Gene_flow" title="Gene flow">gene flow</a> (e.g. <a href="/wiki/Postzygotic_barrier" class="mw-redirect" title="Postzygotic barrier">postzygotic barriers</a>).</li></ol>
<p>Moreover, genera should be composed of <a href="/wiki/Phylogenetic" class="mw-redirect" title="Phylogenetic">phylogenetic</a> units of the same kind as other (analogous) genera.<sup id="cite_ref-delamaza-benignosetal2015_4-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-delamaza-benignosetal2015-4"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>4<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup>
</p>
<meta property="mw:PageProp/toc" />
<div class="mw-heading mw-heading2"><h2 id="Etymology"><span class="anchor" id="Generic_name"></span><span class="anchor" id="Name"></span>Etymology</h2><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Genus&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Etymology"><span>edit</span></a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></div>
<p>The term "genus" comes from <a href="/wiki/Latin" title="Latin">Latin</a> <i lang="la"><a href="https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/genus#Latin" class="extiw" title="wikt:genus">genus</a></i>, a noun form <a href="/wiki/Cognate" title="Cognate">cognate</a> with <i><span title="Latin-language text"><i lang="la"><a href="https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/gigno" class="extiw" title="wikt:gigno">gignere</a></i></span></i> ('to bear; to give birth to'). The Swedish taxonomist <a href="/wiki/Carl_Linnaeus" title="Carl Linnaeus">Carl Linnaeus</a> popularized its use in his 1753 <i><a href="/wiki/Species_Plantarum" title="Species Plantarum">Species Plantarum</a></i>, but the French botanist <a href="/wiki/Joseph_Pitton_de_Tournefort" title="Joseph Pitton de Tournefort">Joseph Pitton de Tournefort</a> (1656–1708) is considered "the founder of the modern concept of genera".<sup id="cite_ref-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-5"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>5<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup>
</p>
<div class="mw-heading mw-heading2"><h2 id="Use">Use</h2><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Genus&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Use"><span>edit</span></a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></div>
<p>The scientific name (or the scientific epithet) of a genus is also called the <b>generic name</b>; in modern style guides and science, it is always capitalised. It plays a fundamental role in <a href="/wiki/Binomial_nomenclature" title="Binomial nomenclature">binomial nomenclature</a>, the system of naming <a href="/wiki/Organism" title="Organism">organisms</a>, where it is combined with the scientific name of a <a href="/wiki/Species" title="Species">species</a>: see <a href="/wiki/Botanical_name" title="Botanical name">Botanical name</a> and <a href="/wiki/Specific_name_(zoology)" title="Specific name (zoology)">Specific name (zoology)</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-6" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-6"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>6<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-7" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-7"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>7<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup>
</p>
<div class="mw-heading mw-heading3"><h3 id="Use_in_nomenclature">Use in nomenclature</h3><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Genus&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Use in nomenclature"><span>edit</span></a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></div>
<link rel="mw-deduplicated-inline-style" href="mw-data:TemplateStyles:r1236090951"><div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">Main articles: <a href="/wiki/Binomial_nomenclature" title="Binomial nomenclature">Binomial nomenclature</a>, <a href="/wiki/Taxonomy_(biology)" title="Taxonomy (biology)">Taxonomy (biology)</a>, <a href="/wiki/Author_citation_(zoology)" title="Author citation (zoology)">Author citation (zoology)</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Author_citation_(botany)" title="Author citation (botany)">Author citation (botany)</a></div>
<p>The rules for the <a href="/wiki/Scientific_name" class="mw-redirect" title="Scientific name">scientific names</a> of <a href="/wiki/Organism" title="Organism">organisms</a> are laid down in the <a href="/wiki/Nomenclature_Codes" class="mw-redirect" title="Nomenclature Codes">nomenclature codes</a>, which allow each species a single unique name that, for <a href="/wiki/Animal" title="Animal">animals</a> (including <a href="/wiki/Protist" title="Protist">protists</a>), <a href="/wiki/Plant" title="Plant">plants</a> (also including <a href="/wiki/Algae" title="Algae">algae</a> and <a href="/wiki/Fungus" title="Fungus">fungi</a>) and <a href="/wiki/Prokaryote" title="Prokaryote">prokaryotes</a> (<a href="/wiki/Bacteria" title="Bacteria">bacteria</a> and <a href="/wiki/Archaea" title="Archaea">archaea</a>), is <a href="/wiki/Latin" title="Latin">Latin</a> and binomial in form; this contrasts with <a href="/wiki/Common_name" title="Common name">common or vernacular names</a>, which are non-standardized, can be non-unique, and typically also vary by country and language of usage.
</p><p>Except for <a href="/wiki/Virus_classification" title="Virus classification">viruses</a>, the standard format for a <a href="/wiki/Species" title="Species">species</a> name comprises the generic name, indicating the genus to which the species belongs, followed by the specific epithet, which (within that genus) is unique to the species. For example, the <a href="/wiki/Gray_wolf" class="mw-redirect" title="Gray wolf">gray wolf</a>'s scientific name is <span class="nowrap"><i><span title="Latin-language text"><i lang="la">Canis lupus</i></span></i>,</span> with <i><a href="/wiki/Canis" title="Canis">Canis</a></i> (<a href="/wiki/Latin" title="Latin">Latin</a> for 'dog') being the generic name shared by the wolf's close relatives and <span title="Latin-language text"><i lang="la">lupus</i></span> (Latin for 'wolf') being the specific name particular to the wolf. A botanical example would be <i><a href="/wiki/Hibiscus_arnottianus" class="mw-redirect" title="Hibiscus arnottianus">Hibiscus arnottianus</a></i>, a particular species of the genus <i><a href="/wiki/Hibiscus" title="Hibiscus">Hibiscus</a></i> native to Hawaii. The specific name is written in lower-case and may be followed by <a href="/wiki/Subspecies" title="Subspecies">subspecies</a> names in <a href="/wiki/Zoology" title="Zoology">zoology</a> or a variety of <a href="/wiki/Infraspecific_name" title="Infraspecific name">infraspecific names</a> in <a href="/wiki/Botany" title="Botany">botany</a>.
</p><p>When the generic name is already known from context, it may be shortened to its initial letter, for example, <i>C. lupus</i> in place of <i>Canis lupus</i>. Where species are further subdivided, the generic name (or its abbreviated form) still forms the leading portion of the scientific name, for example, <span class="nowrap"><i><span title="Latin-language text"><i lang="la">Canis lupus lupus</i></span></i></span> for the <a href="/wiki/Eurasian_wolf" title="Eurasian wolf">Eurasian wolf</a> subspecies, or as a botanical example, <span class="nowrap"><i><span title="Latin-language text"><i lang="la">Hibiscus arnottianus</i></span></i> ssp. <i><span title="Latin-language text"><i lang="la">immaculatus</i></span></i></span>. Also, as visible in the above examples, the Latinised portions of the scientific names of genera and their included species (and infraspecies, where applicable) are, by convention, written in <a href="/wiki/Italic_type" title="Italic type">italics</a>.
</p><p>The scientific names of <a href="/wiki/Virus" title="Virus">virus</a> species are descriptive, not binomial in form, and may or may not incorporate an indication of their containing genus; for example, the virus species "<a href="/wiki/Salmonid_herpesvirus_1" class="mw-redirect" title="Salmonid herpesvirus 1">Salmonid herpesvirus 1</a>", "<a href="/wiki/Salmonid_herpesvirus_2" title="Salmonid herpesvirus 2">Salmonid herpesvirus 2</a>" and "<a href="/wiki/Salmonid_herpesvirus_3" title="Salmonid herpesvirus 3">Salmonid herpesvirus 3</a>" are all within the genus <i><a href="/wiki/Salmonivirus" class="mw-redirect" title="Salmonivirus">Salmonivirus</a></i>; however, the genus to which the species with the formal names "<a href="/wiki/Everglades_virus" title="Everglades virus">Everglades virus</a>" and "<a href="/wiki/Ross_River_virus" title="Ross River virus">Ross River virus</a>" are assigned is <i><a href="/wiki/Alphavirus" title="Alphavirus">Alphavirus</a></i>.
</p><p>As with scientific names at other ranks, in all groups other than viruses, names of genera may be cited with their authorities, typically in the form "author, year" in zoology, and "standard abbreviated author name" in botany. Thus in the examples above, the genus <i>Canis</i> would be cited in full as "<i>Canis</i> Linnaeus, 1758" (zoological usage), while <i>Hibiscus</i>, also first established by <a href="/wiki/Carl_Linnaeus" title="Carl Linnaeus">Linnaeus</a> but in 1753, is simply "<i>Hibiscus</i> L." (botanical usage).
</p>
<div class="mw-heading mw-heading3"><h3 id="The_type_concept">The type concept</h3><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Genus&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: The type concept"><span>edit</span></a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></div>
<link rel="mw-deduplicated-inline-style" href="mw-data:TemplateStyles:r1236090951"><div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">See also: <a href="/wiki/Type_genus" title="Type genus">Type genus</a>, <a href="/wiki/Type_species" title="Type species">Type species</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Type_specimen" class="mw-redirect" title="Type specimen">Type specimen</a></div>
<p>Each genus should have a designated <a href="/wiki/Type_(biology)" title="Type (biology)">type</a>, although in practice there is a backlog of older names without one. In zoology, this is the <a href="/wiki/Type_species" title="Type species">type species</a>, and the generic name is permanently associated with the <a href="/wiki/Type_specimen" class="mw-redirect" title="Type specimen">type specimen</a> of its type species. Should the specimen turn out to be assignable to another genus, the generic name linked to it becomes a <a href="/wiki/Junior_synonym" class="mw-redirect" title="Junior synonym">junior synonym</a> and the remaining <a href="/wiki/Taxon" title="Taxon">taxa</a> in the former genus need to be reassessed.
</p>
<div class="mw-heading mw-heading3"><h3 id="Categories_of_generic_name">Categories of generic name</h3><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Genus&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Categories of generic name"><span>edit</span></a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></div>
<p>In zoological usage, taxonomic names, including those of genera, are classified as "available" or "unavailable". Available names are those published in accordance with the <a href="/wiki/International_Code_of_Zoological_Nomenclature" title="International Code of Zoological Nomenclature">International Code of Zoological Nomenclature</a>; the earliest such name for any taxon (for example, a genus) should then be selected as the "<a href="/wiki/Valid_name_(zoology)" title="Valid name (zoology)">valid</a>" (i.e., current or accepted) name for the taxon in question.
</p><p>Consequently, there will be more available names than valid names at any point in time; which names are currently in use depending on the judgement of taxonomists in either combining taxa described under multiple names, or splitting taxa which may bring available names previously treated as synonyms back into use. "Unavailable" names in zoology comprise names that either were not published according to the provisions of the ICZN Code, e.g., incorrect original or subsequent spellings, names published only in a thesis, and generic names published after 1930 with no type species indicated.<sup id="cite_ref-Hawksworth2010_8-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Hawksworth2010-8"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>8<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup> According to "Glossary" section of the zoological Code, suppressed <i>names</i> (per published "Opinions" of the International Commission of Zoological Nomenclature) remain available but cannot be used as the valid name for a taxon; however, the names published in suppressed <i>works</i> are made unavailable via the relevant Opinion dealing with the work in question.
</p><p>In botany, similar concepts exist but with different labels. The botanical equivalent of zoology's "available name" is a <a href="/wiki/Validly_published_name" title="Validly published name">validly published name</a>. An invalidly published name is a <span title="Latin-language text"><i lang="la">nomen invalidum</i></span> or <span title="Latin-language text"><i lang="la">nom. inval.</i></span>; a rejected name is a <span title="Latin-language text"><i lang="la">nomen rejiciendum</i></span> or <span title="Latin-language text"><i lang="la">nom. rej.</i></span>; a later homonym of a validly published name is a <span title="Latin-language text"><i lang="la">nomen illegitimum</i></span> or <span title="Latin-language text"><i lang="la">nom. illeg.</i></span>; for a full list refer to the <i><a href="/wiki/International_Code_of_Nomenclature_for_algae,_fungi,_and_plants" title="International Code of Nomenclature for algae, fungi, and plants">International Code of Nomenclature for algae, fungi, and plants</a></i> and the work cited above by Hawksworth, 2010.<sup id="cite_ref-Hawksworth2010_8-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Hawksworth2010-8"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>8<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup> In place of the "valid taxon" in zoology, the nearest equivalent in botany is "<a href="/wiki/Correct_name" title="Correct name">correct name</a>" or "current name" which can, again, differ or change with alternative taxonomic treatments or new information that results in previously accepted genera being combined or split.
</p><p><a href="/wiki/International_Code_of_Nomenclature_of_Prokaryotes" title="International Code of Nomenclature of Prokaryotes">Prokaryote</a> and <a href="/wiki/International_Committee_on_Taxonomy_of_Viruses" title="International Committee on Taxonomy of Viruses">virus</a> codes of nomenclature also exist which serve as a reference for designating currently<sup class="noprint Inline-Template" style="white-space:nowrap;">&#91;<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Manual_of_Style/Dates_and_numbers#Chronological_items" title="Wikipedia:Manual of Style/Dates and numbers"><span title="The time period mentioned near this tag is ambiguous. (August 2023)">when?</span></a></i>&#93;</sup> accepted genus names as opposed to others which may be either reduced to synonymy, or, in the case of prokaryotes, relegated to a status of "names without standing in prokaryotic nomenclature".
</p><p>An <b>available</b> (zoological) or <b>validly published</b> (botanical) name that has been historically applied to a genus but is not regarded as the accepted (current/valid) name for the taxon is termed a <a href="/wiki/Synonym_(taxonomy)" title="Synonym (taxonomy)">synonym</a>; some authors also include unavailable names in lists of synonyms as well as available names, such as misspellings, names previously published without fulfilling all of the requirements of the relevant nomenclatural code, and rejected or suppressed names.
</p><p>A particular genus name may have zero to many synonyms, the latter case generally if the genus has been known for a long time and redescribed as new by a range of subsequent workers, or if a range of genera previously considered separate taxa have subsequently been consolidated into one. For example, the <a href="/wiki/World_Register_of_Marine_Species" title="World Register of Marine Species">World Register of Marine Species</a> presently lists 8 genus-level synonyms for the sperm whale genus <i><a href="/wiki/Physeter" title="Physeter">Physeter</a></i> Linnaeus, 1758,<sup id="cite_ref-9" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-9"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>9<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup> and 13 for the bivalve genus <i><a href="/wiki/Pecten_(bivalve)" title="Pecten (bivalve)">Pecten</a></i> O.F. Müller, 1776.<sup id="cite_ref-10" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-10"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>10<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup>
</p>
<div class="mw-heading mw-heading3"><h3 id="Identical_names_(homonyms)"><span id="Identical_names_.28homonyms.29"></span>Identical names (homonyms)</h3><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Genus&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Identical names (homonyms)"><span>edit</span></a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></div>
<p>Within the same kingdom, one generic name can apply to one genus only. However, many names have been assigned (usually unintentionally) to two or more different genera. For example, the <a href="/wiki/Platypus" title="Platypus">platypus</a> belongs to the genus <i>Ornithorhynchus</i> although <a href="/wiki/George_Shaw_(biologist)" title="George Shaw (biologist)">George Shaw</a> named it <i>Platypus</i> in 1799 (these two names are thus <b><i>synonyms</i>)</b>. However, the name <i>Platypus</i> had already been given to a group of <a href="/wiki/Ambrosia_beetle" title="Ambrosia beetle">ambrosia beetles</a> by <a href="/wiki/Johann_Friedrich_Wilhelm_Herbst" title="Johann Friedrich Wilhelm Herbst">Johann Friedrich Wilhelm Herbst</a> in 1793. A name that means two different things is a <i><b>homonym</b></i>. Since beetles and platypuses are both members of the kingdom Animalia, the name could not be used for both. <a href="/wiki/Johann_Friedrich_Blumenbach" title="Johann Friedrich Blumenbach">Johann Friedrich Blumenbach</a> published the replacement name <i>Ornithorhynchus</i> in 1800.
</p><p>However, a genus in one <a href="/wiki/Kingdom_(biology)" title="Kingdom (biology)">kingdom</a> is allowed to bear a scientific name that is in use as a generic name (or the name of a taxon in another rank) in a kingdom that is governed by a different nomenclature code. Names with the same form but applying to different taxa are called "homonyms". Although this is discouraged by both the <a href="/wiki/International_Code_of_Zoological_Nomenclature" title="International Code of Zoological Nomenclature">International Code of Zoological Nomenclature</a> and the <a href="/wiki/International_Code_of_Nomenclature_for_algae,_fungi,_and_plants" title="International Code of Nomenclature for algae, fungi, and plants">International Code of Nomenclature for algae, fungi, and plants</a>, there are some five thousand such names in use in more than one kingdom. For instance,
</p>
<ul><li><i><a href="/wiki/Anura_(frog)" class="mw-redirect" title="Anura (frog)">Anura</a></i> is the name of the <a href="/wiki/Order_(biology)" title="Order (biology)">order</a> of frogs but also is the name of a <a href="/wiki/Synonym_(botany)" class="mw-redirect" title="Synonym (botany)">non-current</a> genus of plants;</li>
<li><i>Aotus</i> is the generic name of both <a href="/wiki/Aotus_(plant)" title="Aotus (plant)">golden peas</a> and <a href="/wiki/Night_monkey" title="Night monkey">night monkeys</a>;</li>
<li><i>Oenanthe</i> is the generic name of both <a href="/wiki/Wheatear" title="Wheatear">wheatears</a> and <a href="/wiki/Oenanthe_(plant)" title="Oenanthe (plant)">water dropworts</a>;</li>
<li><i>Prunella</i> is the generic name of both <a href="/wiki/Accentor" title="Accentor">accentors</a> and <a href="/wiki/Self-heal" class="mw-redirect" title="Self-heal">self-heal</a>; and</li>
<li><i>Proboscidea</i> is the order of <a href="/wiki/Elephant" title="Elephant">elephants</a> and the genus of <a href="/wiki/Proboscidea_(plant)" title="Proboscidea (plant)">devil's claws</a>.</li>
<li>The name of the genus <i><a href="/wiki/Paramecia_(alga)" title="Paramecia (alga)">Paramecia</a></i> (an extinct red alga) is also the plural of the name of the genus <i><a href="/wiki/Paramecium" title="Paramecium">Paramecium</a></i> (which is in the SAR supergroup), which can also lead to confusion.</li></ul>
<p>A list of generic homonyms (with their authorities), including both available (validly published) and selected unavailable names, has been compiled by the <a href="/wiki/Interim_Register_of_Marine_and_Nonmarine_Genera" title="Interim Register of Marine and Nonmarine Genera">Interim Register of Marine and Nonmarine Genera</a> (IRMNG).<sup id="cite_ref-IRMNG_11-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-IRMNG-11"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>11<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup>
</p>
<div class="mw-heading mw-heading3"><h3 id="Use_in_higher_classifications">Use in higher classifications</h3><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Genus&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Use in higher classifications"><span>edit</span></a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></div>
<p>The <a href="/wiki/Type_genus" title="Type genus">type genus</a> forms the base for higher <a href="/wiki/Taxonomy_(biology)" title="Taxonomy (biology)">taxonomic</a> ranks, such as the family name <span title="Latin-language text"><i lang="la"><a href="/wiki/Canidae" title="Canidae">Canidae</a></i></span> ("Canids") based on <i>Canis</i>. However, this does not typically ascend more than one or two levels: the <a href="/wiki/Order_(biology)" title="Order (biology)">order</a> to which dogs and wolves belong is <span title="Latin-language text"><i lang="la"><a href="/wiki/Carnivora" title="Carnivora">Carnivora</a></i></span> ("Carnivores").
</p>
<div class="mw-heading mw-heading2"><h2 id="Numbers_of_accepted_genera">Numbers of accepted genera</h2><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Genus&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Numbers of accepted genera"><span>edit</span></a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></div>
<p>The numbers of either accepted, or all published genus names is not known precisely; Rees et al., 2020 estimate that approximately 310,000 accepted names (valid taxa) may exist, out of a total of c. 520,000 published names (including synonyms) as at end 2019, increasing at some 2,500 published generic names per year.<sup id="cite_ref-12" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-12"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>12<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup> "Official" registers of taxon names at all ranks, including genera, exist for a few groups only such as viruses<sup id="cite_ref-ICTV_1-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-ICTV-1"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>1<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup> and prokaryotes,<sup id="cite_ref-13" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-13"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>13<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup> while for others there are compendia with no "official" standing such as <i><a href="/wiki/Index_Fungorum" title="Index Fungorum">Index Fungorum</a></i> for fungi,<sup id="cite_ref-14" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-14"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>14<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup> <i>Index Nominum Algarum</i><sup id="cite_ref-15" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-15"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>15<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup> and <a href="/wiki/AlgaeBase" title="AlgaeBase">AlgaeBase</a><sup id="cite_ref-16" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-16"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>16<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup> for algae, <i>Index Nominum Genericorum</i><sup id="cite_ref-17" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-17"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>17<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup> and the <a href="/wiki/International_Plant_Names_Index" title="International Plant Names Index">International Plant Names Index</a><sup id="cite_ref-18" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-18"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>18<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup> for plants in general, and ferns through angiosperms, respectively, and <i><a href="/wiki/Nomenclator_Zoologicus" title="Nomenclator Zoologicus">Nomenclator Zoologicus</a></i><sup id="cite_ref-19" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-19"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>19<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup> and the <a href="/wiki/Index_to_Organism_Names" title="Index to Organism Names">Index to Organism Names</a> for zoological names.
</p><p>Totals for both "all names" and estimates for "accepted names" as held in the <i><a href="/wiki/Interim_Register_of_Marine_and_Nonmarine_Genera" title="Interim Register of Marine and Nonmarine Genera">Interim Register of Marine and Nonmarine Genera</a></i> (IRMNG) are broken down further in the publication by Rees et al., 2020 cited above. The accepted names estimates are as follows, broken down by kingdom:
</p>

<ul><li><a href="/wiki/Animal" title="Animal">Animalia</a>: 239,093 accepted genus names (± 55,350)</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Plant" title="Plant">Plantae</a>: 28,724 (± 7,721)</li>

<p>The cited ranges of uncertainty arise because IRMNG lists "uncertain" names (not researched therein) in addition to known "accepted" names; the values quoted are the mean of "accepted" names alone (all "uncertain" names treated as unaccepted) and "accepted + uncertain" names (all "uncertain" names treated as accepted), with the associated range of uncertainty indicating these two extremes.
</p><p>Within Animalia, the largest phylum is <a href="/wiki/Arthropod" title="Arthropod">Arthropoda</a>, with 151,697 ± 33,160 accepted genus names, of which 114,387 ± 27,654 are <a href="/wiki/Insect" title="Insect">insects</a> (class Insecta). Within Plantae, <a href="/wiki/Tracheophyta" class="mw-redirect" title="Tracheophyta">Tracheophyta</a> (vascular plants) make up the largest component, with 23,236 ± 5,379 accepted genus names, of which 20,845 ± 4,494 are <a href="/wiki/Angiosperms" class="mw-redirect" title="Angiosperms">angiosperms</a> (superclass Angiospermae).
</p><p>By comparison, the 2018 annual edition of the <a href="/wiki/Catalogue_of_Life" title="Catalogue of Life">Catalogue of Life</a> (estimated &gt;90% complete, for extant species in the main) contains currently<sup class="noprint Inline-Template" style="white-space:nowrap;">&#91;<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Manual_of_Style/Dates_and_numbers#Chronological_items" title="Wikipedia:Manual of Style/Dates and numbers"><span title="The time period mentioned near this tag is ambiguous. (August 2023)">when?</span></a></i>&#93;</sup> 175,363 "accepted" genus names for 1,744,204 living and 59,284 extinct species,<sup id="cite_ref-20" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-20"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>20<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup> also including genus names only (no species) for some groups.
</p>
<div class="mw-heading mw-heading2"><h2 id="Genus_size">Genus size</h2><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Genus&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Genus size"><span>edit</span></a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></div>
<figure class="mw-default-size" typeof="mw:File/Thumb"><a href="/wiki/File:Number_of_reptile_genera_with_a_given_number_of_species.png" class="mw-file-description"><img src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/7d/Number_of_reptile_genera_with_a_given_number_of_species.png/300px-Number_of_reptile_genera_with_a_given_number_of_species.png" decoding="async" width="300" height="211" class="mw-file-element" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/7d/Number_of_reptile_genera_with_a_given_number_of_species.png/450px-Number_of_reptile_genera_with_a_given_number_of_species.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/7d/Number_of_reptile_genera_with_a_given_number_of_species.png/600px-Number_of_reptile_genera_with_a_given_number_of_species.png 2x" data-file-width="913" data-file-height="643" /></a><figcaption>Number of reptile genera with a given number of species. Most genera have only one or a few species but a few may have hundreds. Based on data from the <a href="/wiki/Reptile_Database" title="Reptile Database">Reptile Database</a> (as of May 2015).</figcaption></figure>
<p>The number of species in genera varies considerably among taxonomic groups. For instance, among (non-avian) <a href="/wiki/Reptile" title="Reptile">reptiles</a>, which have about 1180 genera, the most (&gt;300) have only 1 species, ~360 have between 2 and 4 species, 260 have 5–10 species, ~200 have 11–50 species, and only 27 genera have more than 50 species. However, some insect genera such as the bee genera <i><a href="/wiki/Lasioglossum" title="Lasioglossum">Lasioglossum</a></i> and <i><a href="/wiki/Andrena" title="Andrena">Andrena</a></i> have over 1000 species each. The largest flowering plant genus, <i><a href="/wiki/Astragalus_(plant)" title="Astragalus (plant)">Astragalus</a></i>, contains over 3,000 species.<sup id="cite_ref-Frodin_21-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Frodin-21"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>21<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup>
</p><p>Which species are assigned to a genus is somewhat arbitrary. Although all species within a genus are supposed to be "similar", there are no objective criteria for grouping species into genera. There is much debate among zoologists whether enormous, species-rich genera should be maintained, as it is extremely difficult to come up with identification keys or even character sets that distinguish all species. Hence, many taxonomists argue in favor of breaking down large genera. For instance, the lizard genus <i><a href="/wiki/Anolis" title="Anolis">Anolis</a></i> has been suggested to be broken down into 8 or so different genera which would bring its ~400 species to smaller, more manageable subsets.<sup id="cite_ref-22" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-22"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>22<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup>
</p>
