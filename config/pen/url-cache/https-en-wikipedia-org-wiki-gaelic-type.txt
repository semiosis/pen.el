<p><b>Gaelic type</b> (sometimes called <b>Irish character</b>, <b>Irish type</b>, or <b>Gaelic script</b>) is a family of <a href="/wiki/Insular_script" title="Insular script">Insular script</a> typefaces devised for printing <a href="/wiki/Classical_Gaelic" class="mw-redirect" title="Classical Gaelic">Classical Gaelic</a>. It was widely used from the 16th century until the mid-18th century in Scotland and the mid-20th century in Ireland, but is now rarely used. Sometimes, all Gaelic typefaces are called <i>Celtic</i> or <i><a href="/wiki/Uncial" class="mw-redirect" title="Uncial">uncial</a></i> although most Gaelic types are not uncials. The "Anglo-Saxon" types of the 17th century are included in this category because both the Anglo-Saxon types and the Gaelic/Irish types derive from the insular manuscript hand.
</p><p>The terms <i>Gaelic type</i>, <i>Gaelic script</i> and <i>Irish character</i> translate the <a href="/wiki/Irish_language" title="Irish language">Irish</a> phrase <span title="Irish-language text"><i lang="ga">cló Gaelach</i></span> (<style data-mw-deduplicate="TemplateStyles:r1177148991">.mw-parser-output .IPA-label-small{font-size:85%}.mw-parser-output .references .IPA-label-small,.mw-parser-output .infobox .IPA-label-small,.mw-parser-output .navbox .IPA-label-small{font-size:100%}</style><span class="IPA-label IPA-label-small">pronounced</span> <span class="IPA nowrap" lang="ga-Latn-fonipa"><a href="/wiki/Help:IPA/Irish" title="Help:IPA/Irish">&#91;ˌkl̪ˠoː<span class="wrap"> </span>ˈɡeːl̪ˠəx&#93;</a></span>). In Ireland, the term <span title="Irish-language text"><i lang="ga">cló Gaelach</i></span> is used in opposition to the term <span title="Irish-language text"><i lang="ga">cló Rómhánach</i></span>, <a href="/wiki/Roman_type" title="Roman type">Roman type</a>.
</p><p>The <a href="/wiki/Scottish_Gaelic" title="Scottish Gaelic">Scottish Gaelic</a> term is <span title="Scottish Gaelic-language text"><i lang="gd">corra-litir</i></span> (<link rel="mw-deduplicated-inline-style" href="mw-data:TemplateStyles:r1177148991"><span class="IPA-label IPA-label-small">pronounced</span> <span class="IPA nowrap" lang="gd-Latn-fonipa"><a href="/wiki/Help:IPA/Scottish_Gaelic" title="Help:IPA/Scottish Gaelic">&#91;ˌkʰɔrˠə<span class="wrap"> </span>ˈliʰtʲɪɾʲ&#93;</a></span>). <span title="Scottish Gaelic-language text"><span lang="gd" style="font-style: normal;"><a href="/wiki/Alasdair_mac_Mhaighstir_Alasdair" title="Alasdair mac Mhaighstir Alasdair">Alasdair mac Mhaighstir Alasdair</a></span></span> (<abbr title="circa">c.</abbr><span style="white-space:nowrap;">&#8201;1698</span>–1770) was one of the last Scottish writers with the ability to write in this script,<sup id="cite_ref-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-1">&#91;1&#93;</a></sup> but his main work, <span title="Scottish Gaelic-language text"><i lang="gd">Ais-Eiridh na Sean Chánoin Albannaich</i></span>, was published in the Roman script.
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Characteristics">Characteristics</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Gaelic_type&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Characteristics"><span>edit</span></a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<figure class="mw-default-size" typeof="mw:File/Thumb"><a href="/wiki/File:Gaelic-fonts.png" class="mw-file-description"><img src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/f/fb/Gaelic-fonts.png/220px-Gaelic-fonts.png" decoding="async" width="220" height="330" class="mw-file-element" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/f/fb/Gaelic-fonts.png 1.5x" data-file-width="235" data-file-height="352" /></a><figcaption>Overview of some Gaelic typefaces</figcaption></figure>
<p>Besides the 26 letters of the Latin alphabet, Gaelic typefaces must include all vowels with <a href="/wiki/Acute_accent" title="Acute accent">acute accents</a> <span class="nowrap">&#x27e8;Áá Éé Íí Óó Úú&#x27e9;</span> as well as a set of consonants with <a href="/wiki/Dot_above" class="mw-redirect" title="Dot above">dot above</a> <span class="nowrap">&#x27e8;Ḃḃ Ċċ Ḋḋ Ḟḟ Ġġ Ṁṁ Ṗṗ Ṡṡ Ṫṫ&#x27e9;</span>, and the <a href="/wiki/Tironian_notes" title="Tironian notes">Tironian sign et <span class="nowrap">&#x27e8;⁊&#x27e9;</span></a>, used for <span title="Irish-language text"><i lang="ga">agus</i></span> 'and' in Irish.
</p><p>Gaelic typefaces also often include insular forms: <span class="nowrap">&#x27e8;ꞃ ꞅ&#x27e9;</span> of the letters <span class="nowrap">&#x27e8;r&#x27e9;</span> and <span class="nowrap">&#x27e8;s&#x27e9;</span>, and some of the typefaces contain a number of <a href="/wiki/Ligature_(typography)" class="mw-redirect" title="Ligature (typography)">ligatures</a> used in earlier Gaelic typography and deriving from the manuscript tradition. Lower-case <span class="nowrap">&#x27e8;i&#x27e9;</span> is drawn without a <a href="/wiki/Tittle" title="Tittle">dot</a> (though it is not the <a href="/wiki/Dotless_i" class="mw-redirect" title="Dotless i">Turkish dotless <span class="nowrap">&#x27e8;ı&#x27e9;</span></a>), and the letters <span class="nowrap">&#x27e8;d f g t&#x27e9;</span> have insular shapes <span class="nowrap">&#x27e8;ꝺ ꝼ ᵹ ꞇ&#x27e9;</span>.
</p><p>Many modern Gaelic typefaces include Gaelic letterforms for the letters <span class="nowrap">&#x27e8;j k q v w x y z&#x27e9;</span>, and typically provide support for at least the vowels of the other <a href="/wiki/Celtic_language" class="mw-redirect" title="Celtic language">Celtic languages</a>. They also distinguish between <span class="nowrap">&#x27e8;<a href="/wiki/%26" class="mw-redirect" title="&amp;">&amp;</a>&#x27e9;</span> and <span class="nowrap">&#x27e8;<a href="/wiki/%E2%81%8A" class="mw-redirect" title="⁊">⁊</a>&#x27e9;</span> (as did traditional typography), though some modern fonts replace the ampersand with the Tironian note ostensibly because both mean 'and'.
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Origin">Origin</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Gaelic_type&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Origin"><span>edit</span></a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<style data-mw-deduplicate="TemplateStyles:r1033289096">.mw-parser-output .hatnote{font-style:italic}.mw-parser-output div.hatnote{padding-left:1.6em;margin-bottom:0.5em}.mw-parser-output .hatnote i{font-style:normal}.mw-parser-output .hatnote+link+.hatnote{margin-top:-0.5em}</style><div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Insular_script" title="Insular script">Insular script</a></div>
<figure class="mw-default-size mw-halign-left" typeof="mw:File/Thumb"><a href="/wiki/File:Gaelic-font-Corcaigh.png" class="mw-file-description"><img src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/c/c9/Gaelic-font-Corcaigh.png/260px-Gaelic-font-Corcaigh.png" decoding="async" width="260" height="67" class="mw-file-element" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/c/c9/Gaelic-font-Corcaigh.png 1.5x" data-file-width="289" data-file-height="74" /></a><figcaption>The word <span title="Irish-language text"><i lang="ga"><a href="/wiki/Cork_(city)" title="Cork (city)">Corcaigh</a></i></span> in the Gaelic-script font of the same name.</figcaption></figure>
<p>The Irish uncial alphabet originated in medieval manuscripts as an "insular" variant of the Latin alphabet. The first Gaelic typeface was designed in 1571 for a <a href="/wiki/Catechism" title="Catechism">catechism</a> commissioned by <a href="/wiki/Elizabeth_I" title="Elizabeth I">Elizabeth I</a> to help attempt to convert the Irish Catholic population to Anglicanism.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">&#91;<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (March 2023)">citation needed</span></a></i>&#93;</sup> 
</p>
<div style="clear:both;" class=""></div>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Use">Use</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Gaelic_type&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Use"><span>edit</span></a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Typesetting in Gaelic script remained common in Ireland until the mid-20th century. Gaelic script is today used merely for decorative typesetting; for example, a number of traditional Irish newspapers still print their name in Gaelic script on the first page, and it is also popular for pub signs, greeting cards, and display advertising. <a href="/wiki/Edward_Lhuyd" title="Edward Lhuyd">Edward Lhuyd</a>'s grammar of the <a href="/wiki/Cornish_language" title="Cornish language">Cornish language</a> used Gaelic-script consonants to indicate sounds like <span class="IPA nowrap" lang="und-fonipa" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">&#91;<a href="/wiki/Voiced_dental_fricative" title="Voiced dental fricative">ð</a>&#93;</span> and <span class="IPA nowrap" lang="und-fonipa" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">&#91;<a href="/wiki/Voiceless_dental_fricative" title="Voiceless dental fricative">θ</a>&#93;</span>.
</p><p>In 1996 <span title="Irish-language text"><span lang="ga" style="font-style: normal;"><a href="/wiki/Raidi%C3%B3_Teilif%C3%ADs_%C3%89ireann" class="mw-redirect" title="Raidió Teilifís Éireann">Raidió Teilifís Éireann</a> (RTÉ)</span></span> created a new corporate logo. The logo consists of a modern take on the Gaelic type face. The R's <a href="/wiki/Counter_(typography)" title="Counter (typography)">counter</a> is large with a short tail, the T is roman script while the E is curved but does not have a counter like a lower case E, and the letters also have slight serifs to them. <a href="/wiki/TG4" title="TG4">TG4</a>'s original logo, under the brand <span title="Irish-language text"><i lang="ga">TnaG</i></span>, also used a modernization of the font, the use of the curved T and a sans-serif A in the word <span title="Irish-language text"><i lang="ga">na</i></span>. Other Irish companies that have used Gaelic script in their logos including the <a href="/wiki/Gaelic_Athletic_Association" title="Gaelic Athletic Association">GAA</a>, <span title="Irish-language text"><span lang="ga" style="font-style: normal;"><a href="/wiki/Telecom_%C3%89ireann" title="Telecom Éireann">Telecom Éireann</a></span></span> and <span title="Irish-language text"><span lang="ga" style="font-style: normal;"><a href="/wiki/An_Post" title="An Post">An Post</a></span></span>. The <span title="Irish-language text"><span lang="ga" style="font-style: normal;"><a href="/wiki/Garda_S%C3%ADoch%C3%A1na" title="Garda Síochána">Garda Síochána</a></span></span> uses Gaelic Script on its official seal.
</p><p>The GAA logo uses the script to incorporate both the English language GAA acronym and the Irish language <span title="Irish-language text"><i lang="ga">CLG</i></span> acronym (<a href="/wiki/Irish_language" title="Irish language">Irish</a>: <i lang="ga">Cumann Lúthchleas Gael</i>). The logo more strongly shows the more widely used acronym GAA but taking a closer look a C joins with an L and then to a G lying down.
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="In_Unicode">In Unicode</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Gaelic_type&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: In Unicode"><span>edit</span></a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<link rel="mw-deduplicated-inline-style" href="mw-data:TemplateStyles:r1033289096"><div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">See also: <a href="/wiki/Insular_script#Unicode" title="Insular script">Insular script §&#160;Unicode</a></div>
<p><a href="/wiki/Unicode" title="Unicode">Unicode</a> treats the Gaelic script as a font variant of the <a href="/wiki/Latin_alphabet" title="Latin alphabet">Latin alphabet</a>. A lowercase <a href="/wiki/Insular_g" class="mw-redirect" title="Insular g">insular g</a> (ᵹ) was added in version 4.1 as part of the <a href="/wiki/Phonetic_Extensions" title="Phonetic Extensions">Phonetic Extensions</a> block because of its use in Irish linguistics as a phonetic character for <span class="IPA nowrap" lang="und-fonipa" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">&#91;<a href="/wiki/Voiced_velar_fricative" title="Voiced velar fricative">ɣ</a>&#93;</span>.
</p><p>
According to <a href="/wiki/Michael_Everson" title="Michael Everson">Michael Everson</a>, in the 2006 Unicode proposal for these characters:<sup id="cite_ref-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-2">&#91;2&#93;</a></sup> <style data-mw-deduplicate="TemplateStyles:r1211633275">.mw-parser-output .templatequote{overflow:hidden;margin:1em 0;padding:0 32px}.mw-parser-output .templatequote .templatequotecite{line-height:1.5em;text-align:left;padding-left:1.6em;margin-top:0}</style></p><blockquote class="templatequote"><p>To write text in an ordinary Gaelic font, only <a href="/wiki/ASCII" title="ASCII">ASCII</a> letters should be used, the font making all the relevant substitutions; the insular letters [proposed here] are for use only by specialists who require them for particular purposes.</p></blockquote>
<p>Unicode 5.1 (2008) added a capital G (Ᵹ) and both capital and lowercase letters D, F, R, S, T, besides "turned insular G", on the basis that <a href="/wiki/Edward_Lhuyd" title="Edward Lhuyd">Edward Lhuyd</a> used these letters in his 1707 work <span title="Latin-language text"><i lang="la"><a href="/wiki/Arch%C3%A6ologia_Britannica" title="Archæologia Britannica">Archæologia Britannica</a></i></span> as a scientific orthography for <a href="/wiki/Cornish_language" title="Cornish language">Cornish</a>.

<div class="center" style="width:auto; margin-left:auto; margin-right:auto;">In each figure above, the first sentence is a <a href="/wiki/Pangram" title="Pangram">pangram</a> and reads:<br /><b><span title="Irish-language text"><span lang="ga-Latg">Chuaigh bé mhórshách le dlúthspád fíorfhinn trí hata mo dhea-phorcáin bhig</span></span></b>,<br /><b><span title="Irish-language text"><span lang="ga-Latg">Ċuaiġ bé ṁórṡáċ le dlúṫspád fíorḟinn trí hata mo ḋea-ṗorcáin ḃig</span></span></b>,  <br />meaning "A maiden of great appetite with an intensely white, dense spade went through my good little porker’s hat".<br />The second sentence (bottom line) reads:<br /><b><span title="Irish-language text"><span lang="ga-Latg">Duibhlinn/Ceanannas an cló a úsáidtear anseo</span></span></b>,<br />meaning "<span title="Irish-language text"><i lang="ga">Duibhlinn</i></span>/<span title="Irish-language text"><i lang="ga">Ceannanas</i></span> is the font used here".<br />The second sentence uses the short forms of the letters <i>r</i> and <i>s</i>; the first uses the long forms. See: <a href="/wiki/Long_s" title="Long s">Long s</a> and <a href="/wiki/R_rotunda" title="R rotunda">R rotunda</a>.</div>
