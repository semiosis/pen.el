<p>The <b>Feast of the Transfiguration</b> is celebrated by various Christian communities in honor of the <a href="/wiki/Transfiguration_of_Jesus" title="Transfiguration of Jesus">transfiguration of Jesus</a>. The origins of the feast are less than certain and may have derived from the dedication of three basilicas on <a href="/wiki/Mount_Tabor" title="Mount Tabor">Mount Tabor</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-Baggley58_1-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Baggley58-1"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>1<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup> The feast was present in various forms by the 9th century, and in the <a href="/wiki/Western_Church" class="mw-redirect" title="Western Church">Western Church</a> was made a universal feast celebrated on 6 August by <a href="/wiki/Pope_Callixtus_III" title="Pope Callixtus III">Pope Callixtus III</a> to commemorate the raising of the <a href="/wiki/Siege_of_Belgrade_(1456)" title="Siege of Belgrade (1456)">siege of Belgrade (1456)</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-2"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>2<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup>
</p><p>In the <a href="/wiki/Syriac_Orthodox" class="mw-redirect" title="Syriac Orthodox">Syriac Orthodox</a>, <a href="/wiki/Malankara_Orthodox" class="mw-redirect" title="Malankara Orthodox">Malankara Orthodox</a>, <a href="/wiki/Revised_Julian_calendar" title="Revised Julian calendar">Revised Julian calendars</a> within <a href="/wiki/Eastern_Orthodox_Church" title="Eastern Orthodox Church">Eastern Orthodoxy</a>, <a href="/wiki/Roman_Catholic" class="mw-redirect" title="Roman Catholic">Catholic</a>, <a href="/wiki/Old_Catholic" class="mw-redirect" title="Old Catholic">Old Catholic</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Anglican" class="mw-redirect" title="Anglican">Anglican</a> churches, the Feast of the Transfiguration is observed on 6 August. In the <a href="/wiki/Armenian_Apostolic_Church" title="Armenian Apostolic Church">Armenian Apostolic Church</a>, the Feast of the Transfiguration is observed on the fourteenth Sunday after Easter.<sup id="cite_ref-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-3"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>3<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup> In some Lutheran traditions preceding the reforms to the liturgy in the 1970s, 6 August was also observed as the Feast of the Transfiguration. In those Orthodox churches which continue to follow the <a href="/wiki/Julian_Calendar" class="mw-redirect" title="Julian Calendar">Julian Calendar</a>, 6 August falls on 19 August of the <a href="/wiki/Gregorian_Calendar" class="mw-redirect" title="Gregorian Calendar">Gregorian Calendar</a>. The <a href="/wiki/Transfiguration_of_Jesus" title="Transfiguration of Jesus">Transfiguration</a> is considered a major feast, numbered among the twelve <a href="/wiki/Great_Feasts" class="mw-redirect" title="Great Feasts">Great Feasts</a> in Byzantine Catholicism and Orthodoxy. In all these churches, if the feast falls on a Sunday, its liturgy is not combined with the Sunday liturgy, but completely replaces it.
</p><p>The transfiguration can also be remembered at other points in the liturgical year, sometimes in addition to the feast itself. In the ancient western lectionary, the <a href="/wiki/Ember_Days" class="mw-redirect" title="Ember Days">Ember Saturday in Lent</a> included the gospel of the Transfiguration. In the Catholic lectionary, on the second Sunday in Lent the gospel of the Transfiguration is read. In the <a href="/wiki/Revised_Common_Lectionary" title="Revised Common Lectionary">Revised Common Lectionary</a>, followed by some Lutherans, United Methodists, Anglicans, and others, the last Sunday in the <a href="/wiki/Epiphany_season" title="Epiphany season">Epiphany season</a> (that immediately preceding <a href="/wiki/Ash_Wednesday" title="Ash Wednesday">Ash Wednesday</a>) uses the gospel account, which has led some churches without established festal calendars to refer to this day as "Transfiguration Sunday". In the <a href="/wiki/Church_of_Sweden" title="Church of Sweden">Church of Sweden</a> and the <a href="/wiki/Evangelical_Lutheran_Church_of_Finland" title="Evangelical Lutheran Church of Finland">Church of Finland</a>, the story is read on the seventh Sunday after <a href="/wiki/Trinity_Sunday" title="Trinity Sunday">Trinity</a>, the eighth Sunday after Pentecost.
</p>

<div class="mw-heading mw-heading2"><h2 id="Byzantine_Catholic_and_Eastern_Orthodox_Churches">Byzantine Catholic and Eastern Orthodox Churches</h2><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Feast_of_the_Transfiguration&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Byzantine Catholic and Eastern Orthodox Churches"><span>edit</span></a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></div>
<style data-mw-deduplicate="TemplateStyles:r1236090951">.mw-parser-output .hatnote{font-style:italic}.mw-parser-output div.hatnote{padding-left:1.6em;margin-bottom:0.5em}.mw-parser-output .hatnote i{font-style:normal}.mw-parser-output .hatnote+link+.hatnote{margin-top:-0.5em}@media print{body.ns-0 .mw-parser-output .hatnote{display:none!important}}</style><div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">See also: <a href="/wiki/Savior_of_the_Apple_Feast_Day" class="mw-redirect" title="Savior of the Apple Feast Day">Savior of the Apple Feast Day</a>, <a href="/wiki/Tabor_light" class="mw-redirect" title="Tabor light">Tabor light</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Theoria" class="mw-redirect" title="Theoria">theoria</a></div>
<figure class="mw-default-size" typeof="mw:File/Thumb"><a href="/wiki/File:Fruits_Transfiguration_jp.jpg" class="mw-file-description"><img src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/2c/Fruits_Transfiguration_jp.jpg/220px-Fruits_Transfiguration_jp.jpg" decoding="async" width="220" height="165" class="mw-file-element" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/2c/Fruits_Transfiguration_jp.jpg/330px-Fruits_Transfiguration_jp.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/2c/Fruits_Transfiguration_jp.jpg/440px-Fruits_Transfiguration_jp.jpg 2x" data-file-width="700" data-file-height="525" /></a><figcaption><a href="/wiki/First_Fruits" title="First Fruits">First Fruits</a> brought to be blessed on the Feast of the Transfiguration (<a href="/wiki/Japanese_Orthodox_Church" class="mw-redirect" title="Japanese Orthodox Church">Japanese Orthodox Church</a>)</figcaption></figure>
<p>In the Byzantine Catholic and <a href="/wiki/Eastern_Orthodox_Church" title="Eastern Orthodox Church">Eastern Orthodox Churches</a>, the Transfiguration falls during the <a href="/wiki/Dormition_Fast" class="mw-redirect" title="Dormition Fast">Dormition Fast</a>, but in recognition of the feast the fast is relaxed somewhat and the consumption of fish, wine and oil is allowed on this day.
</p><p>In the Byzantine view the Transfiguration is not only a feast in honor of Jesus, but a feast of the <a href="/wiki/Holy_Trinity" class="mw-redirect" title="Holy Trinity">Holy Trinity</a>, for all three Persons of the Trinity are interpreted as being present at that moment: <a href="/wiki/God_the_Father" title="God the Father">God the Father</a> spoke from heaven; <a href="/wiki/God_the_Son" title="God the Son">God the Son</a> was the one being transfigured, and <a href="/wiki/God_the_Holy_Spirit" class="mw-redirect" title="God the Holy Spirit">God the Holy Spirit</a> was present in the form of a cloud. In this sense, the transfiguration is also considered the "Small Epiphany" (the "Great Epiphany" being the <a href="/wiki/Baptism_of_Jesus" title="Baptism of Jesus">Baptism of Jesus</a>, when the Holy Trinity appeared in a similar pattern).
</p><p>The Transfiguration is ranked as one of the <a href="/wiki/Twelve_Great_Feasts" class="mw-redirect" title="Twelve Great Feasts">Twelve Great Feasts</a> of the <a href="/wiki/Eastern_Orthodox_Church_liturgical_calendar" class="mw-redirect" title="Eastern Orthodox Church liturgical calendar">Byzantine liturgical calendar</a>, and is celebrated with an <a href="/wiki/All-Night_Vigil" class="mw-redirect" title="All-Night Vigil">All-Night Vigil</a> beginning on the eve of the Feast.
</p><p>Grapes are traditionally brought to church to be blessed after the <a href="/wiki/Divine_Liturgy" title="Divine Liturgy">Divine Liturgy</a> on the day of the Transfiguration. If grapes are not available in the area, apples or some other fruit may be brought. This begins the "Blessing of <a href="/wiki/First_Fruits" title="First Fruits">First Fruits</a>" for the year.
</p><p>The Transfiguration is the second of the "Three Feasts of the Saviour in August", the other two being the <a href="/wiki/Procession_of_the_Cross" class="mw-redirect" title="Procession of the Cross">Procession of the Cross</a> on 1 August and the <a href="/wiki/Icon" title="Icon">Icon</a> of Christ <a href="/wiki/Not_Made_by_Hand" class="mw-redirect" title="Not Made by Hand">Not Made by Hand</a> on 16 August. The Transfiguration is preceded by a one-day <a href="/wiki/Forefeast" class="mw-redirect" title="Forefeast">Forefeast</a> and is followed by an <a href="/wiki/Afterfeast" title="Afterfeast">Afterfeast</a> of eight days, ending the day before the Forefeast of the Dormition.
</p><p>In Byzantine theology, the <a href="/wiki/Tabor_Light" title="Tabor Light">Tabor Light</a> is the light revealed on Mount Tabor at the Transfiguration of Jesus, identified with the light seen by Paul on the road to Damascus.
</p>
<div class="mw-heading mw-heading2"><h2 id="Armenian_Apostolic_Church">Armenian Apostolic Church</h2><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Feast_of_the_Transfiguration&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Armenian Apostolic Church"><span>edit</span></a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></div>
<p>The <a href="/wiki/Armenian_Apostolic_Church" title="Armenian Apostolic Church">Armenian Apostolic Church</a> celebrates the feast of the transfiguration (Պայծառակերպութիւն in Armenian<sup id="cite_ref-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-4"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>4<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup>) on the fourteenth Sunday after Easter. It is one of the five major feasts in the Armenian Church calendar. It is also called <a href="/wiki/Vardavar" title="Vardavar">Vardavar</a> (Վարդավառ or rose festival), a pagan celebration that the observance of the transfiguration officially replaced but some of whose customs and traditions are still observed by Christian Armenians and Muslim <a href="/wiki/Hemshin_people" title="Hemshin people">Hemshin people</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-5"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>5<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup>
</p>
<div class="mw-heading mw-heading2"><h2 id="Coptic_Catholic_and_Orthodox_Churches">Coptic Catholic and Orthodox Churches</h2><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Feast_of_the_Transfiguration&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Coptic Catholic and Orthodox Churches"><span>edit</span></a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></div>
<p>The Coptic Orthodox Church Celebrates the feast of transfiguration on the 13th of Mesri according to the <a href="/wiki/Coptic_calendar" title="Coptic calendar">Coptic calendar</a> (i.e. 6 August <a href="/wiki/Old_Style_and_New_Style_dates" title="Old Style and New Style dates">O.S.</a> or 19 August <a href="/wiki/Old_Style_and_New_Style_dates" title="Old Style and New Style dates">N.S.</a>). The Transfiguration always falls during <a href="/wiki/Dormition_Fast" class="mw-redirect" title="Dormition Fast">St Mary's Fast</a> so it is observed as a fasting feast. The feast is considered one of the seven minor Feasts of the Lord, and is celebrated in the joyful tune.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template" style="margin-left:0.1em; white-space:nowrap;">&#91;<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Please_clarify" title="Wikipedia:Please clarify"><span title="The text near this tag may need clarification or removal of jargon. (June 2023)">clarification needed</span></a></i>&#93;</sup>
</p>

<div class="mw-heading mw-heading2"><h2 id="Roman_Catholic_Church">Roman Catholic Church</h2><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Feast_of_the_Transfiguration&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Roman Catholic Church"><span>edit</span></a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></div>
<p>In the <a href="/wiki/Roman_Catholic_Church" class="mw-redirect" title="Roman Catholic Church">Roman Catholic Church</a>, the Transfiguration was once celebrated locally in various parts of the Catholic world on different days, including 6 August, but was not universally recognized. In 1456, the <a href="/wiki/Kingdom_of_Hungary" title="Kingdom of Hungary">Kingdom of Hungary</a> repulsed an <a href="/wiki/Ottoman_Empire" title="Ottoman Empire">Ottoman</a> invasion of the Balkans by breaking the <a href="/wiki/Siege_of_Belgrade_(1456)" title="Siege of Belgrade (1456)">siege of Belgrade</a>. News of the victory arrived in Rome on 6 August.<sup id="cite_ref-6" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-6"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>6<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup> Given the importance to international politics at that time of such battles between Christian and Muslim nations, in celebration of the victory, Pope <a href="/wiki/Callixtus_III" class="mw-redirect" title="Callixtus III">Callixtus III</a> elevated the Transfiguration to a Feast day to be celebrated in the entire <a href="/wiki/Roman_rite" class="mw-redirect" title="Roman rite">Roman rite</a>.
</p><p>In 2002, <a href="/wiki/Pope_John_Paul_II" title="Pope John Paul II">Pope John Paul II</a> selected the Transfiguration as one of the five <a href="/wiki/Rosary#Luminous_Mysteries" title="Rosary">Luminous Mysteries</a> of the <a href="/wiki/Rosary" title="Rosary">Rosary</a>.
</p>
<div class="mw-heading mw-heading2"><h2 id="Old_Catholic_Church">Old Catholic Church</h2><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Feast_of_the_Transfiguration&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Old Catholic Church"><span>edit</span></a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></div>
<p>The <a href="/wiki/Old_Catholic_Church" title="Old Catholic Church">Old Catholic Church</a> celebrates the Transfiguration typically on 6 August, according to the Roman rite calendar; however, every local Old Catholic Church throughout the world has the option to celebrate this major feast on a different day. The Old Catholic theological view of the Transfiguration shares much in common with the Eastern Orthodox perspective. Old Catholics also believe that the transfiguration was a major event that revealed the divinity of Christ; that Jesus is indeed the splendor and <i>eikon</i> of the Father. The Transfiguration shows forth humanity in the splendor of its original form when it was united in the life-giving love of the Triune God. This event reveals the possibility of humanity's <a href="/wiki/Divinization_(Christian)" title="Divinization (Christian)">theosis</a>.
</p><p>If the Transfiguration falls on a Sunday, it replaces the ordinary liturgical Ordo of the season for Sacred Liturgy.
</p>
<div class="mw-heading mw-heading2"><h2 id="Anglican_Communion">Anglican Communion</h2><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Feast_of_the_Transfiguration&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Anglican Communion"><span>edit</span></a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></div>
<p>In the <a href="/wiki/Book_of_Common_Prayer" title="Book of Common Prayer">Book of Common Prayer</a> (1549 and 1552) the feast of the Transfiguration, which had a relatively low rank in the Sarum Calendar, is omitted, but was restored to the Calendar without a collect and reading being provided by royal order in 1560. This state of affairs is perpetuated in the 1662 Prayer Book, but would have been remedied had the 1928 Proposed BCP been approved by Parliament.  As it was, the Bishops of the Church of England refused to sanction those who used the 1928 Book of Common Prayer as the 6 August date came into general use. The Feast of the Transfiguration is retained in the Common Worship lectionary of the Church of England (6 August). The American <a href="/wiki/Book_of_Common_Prayer" title="Book of Common Prayer">Book of Common Prayer</a> of 1892 has been incorporated into most modern Anglican calendars (sometimes called "The Transfiguration of Our Lord").<sup id="cite_ref-7" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-7"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>7<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup>
</p>
<div class="mw-heading mw-heading2"><h2 id="Protestantism">Protestantism</h2><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Feast_of_the_Transfiguration&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Protestantism"><span>edit</span></a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></div>
<p>After the Reformation the Feast of the Transfiguration was abandoned in the Protestant parts of Germany, but continued to be observed in Sweden.
</p>
<div class="mw-heading mw-heading3"><h3 id="Lutheran_Churches_in_America">Lutheran Churches in America</h3><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Feast_of_the_Transfiguration&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Lutheran Churches in America"><span>edit</span></a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></div>
<p>The <a href="/wiki/Evangelical_Lutheran_Church_in_America" title="Evangelical Lutheran Church in America">Evangelical Lutheran Church in America</a>, <a href="/wiki/Lutheran_Church%E2%80%93Missouri_Synod" class="mw-redirect" title="Lutheran Church–Missouri Synod">Lutheran Church–Missouri Synod</a>, the <a href="/wiki/Evangelical_Lutheran_Church_in_Canada" title="Evangelical Lutheran Church in Canada">Evangelical Lutheran Church in Canada</a>, and the North American Lutheran Church (NALC) observe the Feast of the Transfiguration as the last Sunday after the <a href="/wiki/Epiphany_(Christian)" class="mw-redirect" title="Epiphany (Christian)">Epiphany</a>, which is the Sunday immediately preceding <a href="/wiki/Ash_Wednesday" title="Ash Wednesday">Ash Wednesday</a> and the season of <a href="/wiki/Lent" title="Lent">Lent</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-8" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-8"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>8<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup>
</p>
<div class="mw-heading mw-heading3"><h3 id="Reformed_Calvinist_and_Presbyterian">Reformed Calvinist and Presbyterian</h3><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Feast_of_the_Transfiguration&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: Reformed Calvinist and Presbyterian"><span>edit</span></a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></div>
<p>In the <a href="/wiki/Presbyterian_Church" class="mw-redirect" title="Presbyterian Church">Presbyterian Church</a>, the Sunday of the Transfiguration marks the last day of the Epiphany season, on the last Sunday before Ash Wednesday.  The inceptive Calvinist tradition rejected all liturgical feasts, including the Feast of the Transfiguration.  This, however, does not mean that the Transfiguration itself was ignored by the Calvinists.  Calvin's own views on the Transfiguration were far from ambivalent:
</p>
<dl><dd>"It might be asked whether it was really Moses and Elijah who were present or whether only their spectres were set before the disciples, just as often the prophets saw visions of absent things. Although there is much to be said on both sides, as they say, it seems more likely to me that they really were brought to that place."<sup id="cite_ref-9" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-9"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>9<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup></dd></dl>
<p>With time, most major feasts were restored to the Reformed ecclesiastical calendar.  The Sunday of Transfiguration is now a part of the <a href="/wiki/Revised_Common_Lectionary" title="Revised Common Lectionary">Revised Common Lectionary</a>.  Whether it is celebrated liturgically or in name only, it is left to the discretion of the clergy or Session.
</p><p><a href="/wiki/The_Book_of_Common_Worship_of_1993" class="mw-redirect" title="The Book of Common Worship of 1993">The Book of Common Worship of 1993</a> (<a href="/wiki/Presbyterian_Church_USA" class="mw-redirect" title="Presbyterian Church USA">Presbyterian Church USA</a>) contains the order of the service for Transfiguration of the Lord.  This order is either combined with the Sunday liturgy or replaces it in those congregations which orient themselves towards liturgical practices and observances.<sup id="cite_ref-10" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-10"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>10<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup>
</p>
