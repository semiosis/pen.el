<p><b>Generative Pre-trained Transformer 3</b> (<b>GPT-3</b>) is an <a href="/wiki/Autoregressive_model" title="Autoregressive model">autoregressive</a> <a href="/wiki/Language_model" title="Language model">language model</a> that uses <a href="/wiki/Deep_learning" title="Deep learning">deep learning</a> to produce human-like text.
</p><p>It is the third-generation language prediction model in the GPT-n series (and the successor to <a href="/wiki/GPT-2" title="GPT-2">GPT-2</a>) created by <a href="/wiki/OpenAI" title="OpenAI">OpenAI</a>, a San Francisco-based <a href="/wiki/Artificial_intelligence" title="Artificial intelligence">artificial intelligence</a> research laboratory.<sup id="cite_ref-CNBC_Shead_20200723_2-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-CNBC_Shead_20200723-2">&#91;2&#93;</a></sup> GPT-3's full version has a capacity of 175 billion <a href="/wiki/Parameter_(machine_learning)" class="mw-redirect" title="Parameter (machine learning)">machine learning parameters</a>. GPT-3, which was introduced in May 2020, and was in beta testing as of July 2020,<sup id="cite_ref-Medium_Bussler_20200721_3-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Medium_Bussler_20200721-3">&#91;3&#93;</a></sup> is part of a trend in <a href="/wiki/Natural_language_processing" title="Natural language processing">natural language processing</a> (NLP) systems of pre-trained language representations.<sup id="cite_ref-arXiv_Brown_20200722_1-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-arXiv_Brown_20200722-1">&#91;1&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>The quality of the text generated by GPT-3 is so high that it can be difficult to determine whether or not it was written by a human, which has both benefits and risks.<sup id="cite_ref-analyticsindiamag_Sagar_20200603_4-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-analyticsindiamag_Sagar_20200603-4">&#91;4&#93;</a></sup> Thirty-one OpenAI researchers and engineers presented the original May 28, 2020 paper introducing GPT-3. In their paper, they warned of GPT-3's potential dangers and called for research to mitigate risk.<sup id="cite_ref-arXiv_Brown_20200722_1-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-arXiv_Brown_20200722-1">&#91;1&#93;</a></sup><sup class="reference nowrap"><span title="Page / location: 34">&#58;&#8202;34&#8202;</span></sup> <a href="/wiki/David_Chalmers" title="David Chalmers">David Chalmers</a>, an Australian philosopher, described GPT-3 as "one of the most interesting and important AI systems ever produced."<sup id="cite_ref-DailyNous_Weinberg_Chalmer_20200730_5-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-DailyNous_Weinberg_Chalmer_20200730-5">&#91;5&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p><a href="/wiki/Microsoft" title="Microsoft">Microsoft</a> announced on September 22, 2020, that it had licensed "exclusive" use of GPT-3; others can still use the public API to receive output, but only Microsoft has access to GPT-3's underlying model.<sup id="cite_ref-MSgotcode_6-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-MSgotcode-6">&#91;6&#93;</a></sup>
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Background">Background</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=GPT-3&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Background">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<style data-mw-deduplicate="TemplateStyles:r1033289096">.mw-parser-output .hatnote{font-style:italic}.mw-parser-output div.hatnote{padding-left:1.6em;margin-bottom:0.5em}.mw-parser-output .hatnote i{font-style:normal}.mw-parser-output .hatnote+link+.hatnote{margin-top:-0.5em}</style><div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">Further information: <a href="/wiki/GPT-2#Background" title="GPT-2">GPT-2 §&#160;Background</a></div>
<p>According to <i><a href="/wiki/The_Economist" title="The Economist">The Economist</a></i>, improved algorithms, powerful computers, and an increase in digitized data have fueled a revolution in <a href="/wiki/Machine_learning" title="Machine learning">machine learning</a>, with new techniques in the 2010s resulting in "rapid improvements in tasks" including manipulating language.<sup id="cite_ref-theeconomist_20200611_7-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-theeconomist_20200611-7">&#91;7&#93;</a></sup> Software models are trained to learn by using thousands or millions of examples in a "structure<span class="nowrap">&#160;</span>... loosely based on the neural architecture of the brain".<sup id="cite_ref-theeconomist_20200611_7-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-theeconomist_20200611-7">&#91;7&#93;</a></sup> One architecture used in <a href="/wiki/Natural_language_processing" title="Natural language processing">natural language processing</a> (NLP) is a <a href="/wiki/Artificial_neural_network" title="Artificial neural network">neural network</a> based on a <a href="/wiki/Deep_learning" title="Deep learning">deep learning</a> model that was first introduced in 2017—the <a href="/wiki/Transformer_(machine_learning_model)" title="Transformer (machine learning model)">Transformer</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-Polosukhin_2017_8-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Polosukhin_2017-8">&#91;8&#93;</a></sup> GPT-n models are based on this Transformer-based deep learning neural network architecture. There are a number of NLP systems capable of processing, mining, organizing, connecting, contrasting, understanding and generating answers to questions.<sup id="cite_ref-thomsonreuters_nd_9-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-thomsonreuters_nd-9">&#91;9&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>On June 11, 2018, OpenAI researchers and engineers posted their original paper on <a href="/wiki/Generative_model" title="Generative model">generative models</a>—language models—artificial intelligence systems—that could be pre-trained with an enormous and diverse <a href="/wiki/Text_corpus" title="Text corpus">corpus of text</a> via <a href="/wiki/Dataset_(machine_learning)" class="mw-redirect" title="Dataset (machine learning)">datasets</a>, in a process they called <a href="/w/index.php?title=Generative_pre-training&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="Generative pre-training (page does not exist)">generative pre-training</a> (GP).<sup id="cite_ref-OpenAI_Radford_20200611_10-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-OpenAI_Radford_20200611-10">&#91;10&#93;</a></sup> The authors described how language understanding performances in natural language processing (NLP) were improved in GPT-n through a process of "generative pre-training of a language model on a diverse corpus of unlabeled text, followed by <a href="/wiki/Discriminative_model" title="Discriminative model">discriminative</a> fine-tuning on each specific task." This <a href="/wiki/Unsupervised_learning" title="Unsupervised learning">eliminated the need for human supervision</a> and for time-intensive hand-labeling.<sup id="cite_ref-OpenAI_Radford_20200611_10-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-OpenAI_Radford_20200611-10">&#91;10&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>In February 2020, Microsoft introduced its Turing Natural Language Generation (T-NLG), which was claimed to be the "largest language model ever published at 17 billion parameters."<sup id="cite_ref-Wired_Sterling_20200213_11-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Wired_Sterling_20200213-11">&#91;11&#93;</a></sup> It performed better than any other language model at a variety of tasks which included <a href="/wiki/Automatic_summarization" title="Automatic summarization">summarizing texts</a> and <a href="/wiki/Question_answering" title="Question answering">answering questions</a>, despite being smaller than IBM's Tangora language model which had over 8 trillion parameters.<sup id="cite_ref-Wired_Sterling_20200213_11-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Wired_Sterling_20200213-11">&#91;11&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Training_and_capabilities">Training and capabilities</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=GPT-3&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Training and capabilities">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>On May 28, 2020, an <a href="/wiki/ArXiv" title="ArXiv">arXiv</a> preprint by a group of 31 engineers and researchers at OpenAI described the development of GPT-3, a third-generation "state-of-the-art language model".<sup id="cite_ref-arXiv_Brown_20200722_1-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-arXiv_Brown_20200722-1">&#91;1&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-analyticsindiamag_Sagar_20200603_4-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-analyticsindiamag_Sagar_20200603-4">&#91;4&#93;</a></sup> The team increased the capacity of GPT-3 by over two orders of magnitude from that of its predecessor, GPT-2,<sup id="cite_ref-gpt2-with-quote_12-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-gpt2-with-quote-12">&#91;12&#93;</a></sup> making GPT-3 the largest non-sparse (in a sparse model, many of those parameters are set to the same constant value, so even if there are more total parameters, there is less meaningful information) language model to date.<sup id="cite_ref-arXiv_Brown_20200722_1-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-arXiv_Brown_20200722-1">&#91;1&#93;</a></sup><sup class="reference nowrap">&#58;&#8202;<span title="Page / location: 14&#10;Quotation : &quot;Since we increase the capacity by over two orders of magnitude from GPT-2 to GPT-3&quot;" class="tooltip tooltip-dashed" style="border-bottom: 1px dashed;">14</span>&#8202;</sup><sup id="cite_ref-CNBC_Shead_20200723_2-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-CNBC_Shead_20200723-2">&#91;2&#93;</a></sup> Because GPT-3 is structurally similar to its predecessors,<sup id="cite_ref-arXiv_Brown_20200722_1-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-arXiv_Brown_20200722-1">&#91;1&#93;</a></sup> its higher level of accuracy is attributed to its increased capacity and higher number of parameters.<sup id="cite_ref-ZDNet_Tiernan_20200601_13-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-ZDNet_Tiernan_20200601-13">&#91;13&#93;</a></sup> GPT-3's capacity is ten times larger than that of Microsoft's Turing NLG, the next largest NLP model.<sup id="cite_ref-analyticsindiamag_Sagar_20200603_4-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-analyticsindiamag_Sagar_20200603-4">&#91;4&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>Sixty percent of the weighted pre-training dataset for GPT-3 comes from a filtered version of <a href="/wiki/Common_Crawl" title="Common Crawl">Common Crawl</a> consisting of 410 billion <a href="/wiki/Byte_pair_encoding" title="Byte pair encoding">byte-pair-encoded</a> tokens.<sup id="cite_ref-arXiv_Brown_20200722_1-6" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-arXiv_Brown_20200722-1">&#91;1&#93;</a></sup><sup class="reference nowrap"><span title="Page / location: 9">&#58;&#8202;9&#8202;</span></sup> Other sources are 19 billion tokens from WebText2 representing 22% of the weighted total, 12 billion tokens from Books1 representing 8%, 55 billion tokens from Books2 representing 8%, and 3 billion tokens from Wikipedia representing 3%.<sup id="cite_ref-arXiv_Brown_20200722_1-7" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-arXiv_Brown_20200722-1">&#91;1&#93;</a></sup><sup class="reference nowrap"><span title="Page / location: 9">&#58;&#8202;9&#8202;</span></sup> GPT-3 was trained on hundreds of billions of words and is capable of coding in CSS, JSX, Python, among others.<sup id="cite_ref-Medium_Bussler_20200721_3-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Medium_Bussler_20200721-3">&#91;3&#93;</a></sup>
</p>

<p>Since GPT-3's training data was all-encompassing, it does not require further training for distinct language tasks.<sup id="cite_ref-Medium_Bussler_20200721_3-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Medium_Bussler_20200721-3">&#91;3&#93;</a></sup> The training data contains occasional toxic language and GPT-3 occasionally generates toxic language as a result of mimicking its training data. A study from the University of Washington found that GPT-3 produced toxic language at a toxicity level comparable to the similar natural language processing models of <a href="/wiki/GPT-2" title="GPT-2">GPT-2</a> and CTRL. GPT-3 produced less toxic language compared to its predecessor model, GPT-1, although it produced both more generations and a higher toxicity of toxic language compared to CTRL Wiki, a language model trained entirely on Wikipedia data.<sup id="cite_ref-14" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-14">&#91;14&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>On June 11, 2020, <a href="/wiki/OpenAI" title="OpenAI">OpenAI</a> announced that users could request access to its user-friendly GPT-3 API—a "machine learning toolset"—to help OpenAI "explore the strengths and limits" of this new technology.<sup id="cite_ref-OpenAI_20200611_15-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-OpenAI_20200611-15">&#91;15&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-techcrunch_20200601_16-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-techcrunch_20200601-16">&#91;16&#93;</a></sup> The invitation described how this API had a general-purpose "text in, text out" interface that can complete almost "any English language task", instead of the usual single use-case.<sup id="cite_ref-OpenAI_20200611_15-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-OpenAI_20200611-15">&#91;15&#93;</a></sup> According to one user, who had access to a private early release of the OpenAI GPT-3 API, GPT-3 was "eerily good" at writing "amazingly coherent text" with only a few simple prompts.<sup id="cite_ref-Arram_20200709_17-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Arram_20200709-17">&#91;17&#93;</a></sup> In an initial experiment 80 US subjects were asked to judge if short ~200 word articles were written by humans or GPT-3. The participants judged incorrectly 48% of the time, doing only slightly better than random guessing.<sup id="cite_ref-arXiv_Brown_20200722_1-8" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-arXiv_Brown_20200722-1">&#91;1&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>Because GPT-3 can "generate news articles which human evaluators have difficulty distinguishing from articles written by humans,"<sup id="cite_ref-analyticsindiamag_Sagar_20200603_4-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-analyticsindiamag_Sagar_20200603-4">&#91;4&#93;</a></sup> GPT-3 has the "potential to advance both the beneficial and harmful applications of language models."<sup id="cite_ref-arXiv_Brown_20200722_1-9" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-arXiv_Brown_20200722-1">&#91;1&#93;</a></sup><sup class="reference nowrap"><span title="Page / location: 34">&#58;&#8202;34&#8202;</span></sup> In their May 28, 2020 paper, the researchers described in detail the potential "harmful effects of GPT-3"<sup id="cite_ref-analyticsindiamag_Sagar_20200603_4-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-analyticsindiamag_Sagar_20200603-4">&#91;4&#93;</a></sup> which include "misinformation, <a href="/wiki/Spamming" title="Spamming">spam</a>, <a href="/wiki/Phishing" title="Phishing">phishing</a>, <a href="/wiki/Abuse_of_process" title="Abuse of process">abuse of legal and governmental processes</a>, <a href="/wiki/Academic_dishonesty" title="Academic dishonesty">fraudulent academic essay</a> writing and social engineering <a href="/wiki/Pretexting" title="Pretexting">pretexting</a>".<sup id="cite_ref-arXiv_Brown_20200722_1-10" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-arXiv_Brown_20200722-1">&#91;1&#93;</a></sup> The authors draw attention to these dangers to call for research on <a href="/wiki/Risk_management" title="Risk management">risk mitigation</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-arXiv_Brown_20200722_1-11" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-arXiv_Brown_20200722-1">&#91;1&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-18" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-18">&#91;18&#93;</a></sup><sup class="reference nowrap"><span title="Page / location: 34">&#58;&#8202;34&#8202;</span></sup>
</p><p>GPT-3 is capable of performing <a href="/wiki/Zero-shot_learning" title="Zero-shot learning">zero-shot</a>, few-shot and <a href="/wiki/One-shot_learning_(software)" class="mw-redirect" title="One-shot learning (software)">one-shot learning</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-arXiv_Brown_20200722_1-12" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-arXiv_Brown_20200722-1">&#91;1&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Reception">Reception</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=GPT-3&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Reception">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Applications">Applications</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=GPT-3&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Applications">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<ul><li>GPT-3, specifically the <a href="/wiki/OpenAI_Codex" title="OpenAI Codex">Codex model</a>, is the basis for <a href="/wiki/GitHub_Copilot" title="GitHub Copilot">GitHub Copilot</a>, a code completion and generation software that can be used in various code editors and IDEs.</li>
<li>GPT-3 is used in certain Microsoft products to translate conventional language into formal computer code.<sup id="cite_ref-19" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-19">&#91;19&#93;</a></sup></li>
<li>GPT-3 has been used by <a href="/wiki/Andrew_Mayne" title="Andrew Mayne">Andrew Mayne</a> for AI Writer,<sup id="cite_ref-20" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-20">&#91;20&#93;</a></sup> which allows people to correspond with historical figures via email.</li>
<li>GPT-3 has been used by <a href="/wiki/Jason_Rohrer" title="Jason Rohrer">Jason Rohrer</a> in a retro-themed chatbot project named "Project December", which is accessible online and allows users to converse with several AIs using GPT-3 technology.<sup id="cite_ref-21" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-21">&#91;21&#93;</a></sup></li>
<li>GPT-3 was used by <i><a href="/wiki/The_Guardian" title="The Guardian">The Guardian</a></i> to write an article about AI being harmless to human beings. It was fed some ideas and produced eight different essays, which were ultimately merged into one article.<sup id="cite_ref-22" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-22">&#91;22&#93;</a></sup></li>
<li>GPT-3 was used in <i><a href="/wiki/AI_Dungeon" title="AI Dungeon">AI Dungeon</a></i>, which generates text-based adventure games. Later it was replaced by a competing model after OpenAI changed their policy regarding generated content. <sup id="cite_ref-23" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-23">&#91;23&#93;</a></sup></li></ul>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Reviews">Reviews</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=GPT-3&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Reviews">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<ul><li>In a July 2020 review in <i><a href="/wiki/The_New_York_Times" title="The New York Times">The New York Times</a></i>, <a href="/wiki/Farhad_Manjoo" title="Farhad Manjoo">Farhad Manjoo</a> said that GPT-3's ability to generate computer code, poetry, and prose is not just "amazing", "spooky", and "humbling", but also "more than a little terrifying".<sup id="cite_ref-NYT_Farhad_20190515_24-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-NYT_Farhad_20190515-24">&#91;24&#93;</a></sup></li>
<li><i>Daily Nous</i> presented a series of articles by nine philosophers on GPT-3.<sup id="cite_ref-DailyNous_Weinberg_20200730_25-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-DailyNous_Weinberg_20200730-25">&#91;25&#93;</a></sup> Australian philosopher <a href="/wiki/David_Chalmers" title="David Chalmers">David Chalmers</a> described GPT-3 as "one of the most interesting and important AI systems ever produced".<sup id="cite_ref-DailyNous_Weinberg_Chalmer_20200730_5-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-DailyNous_Weinberg_Chalmer_20200730-5">&#91;5&#93;</a></sup></li>
<li>A review in <i><a href="/wiki/Wired_(magazine)" title="Wired (magazine)">Wired</a></i> said that GPT-3 was "provoking chills across <a href="/wiki/Silicon_Valley" title="Silicon Valley">Silicon Valley</a>".<sup id="cite_ref-Wired_Simonite_20200722_26-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Wired_Simonite_20200722-26">&#91;26&#93;</a></sup></li>
<li>The <i><a href="/wiki/National_Law_Review" class="mw-redirect" title="National Law Review">National Law Review</a></i> said that GPT-3 is an "impressive step in the larger process", with OpenAI and others finding "useful applications for all of this power" while continuing to "work toward a more <a href="/wiki/Artificial_general_intelligence" title="Artificial general intelligence">general</a> intelligence".<sup id="cite_ref-NTR_20200730_27-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-NTR_20200730-27">&#91;27&#93;</a></sup></li>
<li>An article in the <i><a href="/wiki/MIT_Technology_Review" title="MIT Technology Review">MIT Technology Review</a>,</i> cowritten by Deep Learning critic <a href="/wiki/Gary_Marcus" title="Gary Marcus">Gary Marcus</a>,<sup id="cite_ref-28" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-28">&#91;28&#93;</a></sup> stated that GPT-3's "comprehension of the world is often seriously off, which means you can never really trust what it says."<sup id="cite_ref-Marcus_Davis_2020_29-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Marcus_Davis_2020-29">&#91;29&#93;</a></sup> According to the authors, GPT-3 models relationships between words without having an understanding of the meaning behind each word.</li>
<li>Jerome Pesenti, head of the Facebook AI lab, said GPT-3 is "unsafe," pointing to the <a href="/wiki/Sexist" class="mw-redirect" title="Sexist">sexist</a>, <a href="/wiki/Racist" class="mw-redirect" title="Racist">racist</a> and other biased and negative language generated by the system when it was asked to discuss Jews, women, black people, and the <a href="/wiki/Holocaust" class="mw-redirect" title="Holocaust">Holocaust</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-30" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-30">&#91;30&#93;</a></sup></li>
<li>Nabla, a French start-up specializing in healthcare technology, tested GPT-3 as a medical <a href="/wiki/Chatbot" title="Chatbot">chatbot</a>, though OpenAI itself warned against such use. As expected, GPT-3 showed several limitations. For example, while testing GPT-3 responses about mental health issues, the AI advised a simulated patient to commit suicide.<sup id="cite_ref-31" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-31">&#91;31&#93;</a></sup></li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Noam_Chomsky" title="Noam Chomsky">Noam Chomsky</a> expressed his skepticism about GPT-3's scientific value: "It's not a language model. It works just as well for impossible languages as for actual languages. It is therefore refuted, if intended as a language model, by normal scientific criteria. [...] Perhaps it's useful for some purpose, but it seems to tell us nothing about language or cognition generally."<sup id="cite_ref-32" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-32">&#91;32&#93;</a></sup></li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Luciano_Floridi" title="Luciano Floridi">Luciano Floridi</a> and <a href="/w/index.php?title=Massimo_Chiriatti&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="Massimo Chiriatti (page does not exist)">Massimo Chiriatti</a> highlighted the risk of "cheap production of good, semantic artefacts".<sup id="cite_ref-33" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-33">&#91;33&#93;</a></sup></li></ul>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Criticism">Criticism</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=GPT-3&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Criticism">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>GPT-3's builder, <a href="/wiki/OpenAI" title="OpenAI">OpenAI</a>, was initially founded as a non-profit in 2015.<sup id="cite_ref-34" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-34">&#91;34&#93;</a></sup> In 2019, OpenAI did not publicly release GPT-3's precursor model, breaking from OpenAI's previous open-source practices, citing concerns that the model would perpetuate fake news. OpenAI eventually released a version of <a href="/wiki/GPT-2" title="GPT-2">GPT-2</a> that was 8% of the original model's size.<sup id="cite_ref-35" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-35">&#91;35&#93;</a></sup> In the same year, OpenAI restructured to be a for-profit company.<sup id="cite_ref-36" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-36">&#91;36&#93;</a></sup> In 2020, Microsoft announced the company had exclusive licensing of GPT-3 for Microsoft's products and services following a multi-billion dollar investment in OpenAI. The agreement permits OpenAI to offer a public-facing API such that users can send text to GPT-3 to receive the model's output, but only Microsoft will have access to the GPT-3's source code.<sup id="cite_ref-MSgotcode_6-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-MSgotcode-6">&#91;6&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>Large language models, such as GPT-3, have come under criticism from Google's AI ethics researchers for the environmental impact of training and storing the models, detailed in a paper co-authored by <a href="/wiki/Timnit_Gebru" title="Timnit Gebru">Timnit Gebru</a> and <a href="/wiki/Emily_M._Bender" title="Emily M. Bender">Emily M. Bender</a> in 2021.<sup id="cite_ref-37" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-37">&#91;37&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>The growing use of automated writing technologies based on GPT-3 and other language generators, has raised concerns regarding academic integrity<sup id="cite_ref-38" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-38">&#91;38&#93;</a></sup> and raised the stakes of how universities and schools will gauge what constitutes academic misconduct such as plagiarism.<sup id="cite_ref-39" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-39">&#91;39&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>GPT-3 was criticized for its <a href="/wiki/Algorithmic_bias" title="Algorithmic bias">algorithmic bias</a>; for example, it is more likely to associate <a href="/wiki/Islam" title="Islam">Islam</a> with <a href="/wiki/Islamic_terrorism" title="Islamic terrorism">terrorism</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-40" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-40">&#91;40&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>In its response to the Request for Comments on Intellectual Property Protection for Artificial Intelligence Innovation from the United States Patent and Trademark Office ("USPTO"), OpenAI acknowledges that "copyright protection arises automatically when an author creates an original work and fixes it in a tangible medium, see 17 U.S.C. § 102, the vast majority of content posted online is protected by U.S.  copyright laws.<sup id="cite_ref-41" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-41">&#91;41&#93;</a></sup> However, GPT was built with data from the Common Crawl dataset, a conglomerate of copyrighted articles, internet posts, web pages, and books scraped from 60 million domains over a period of 12 years. TechCrunch reports this data includes copyrighted material from BBC, The New York Times, Reddit, the full text of online books, and more.<sup id="cite_ref-42" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-42">&#91;42&#93;</a></sup> All GPT-based products therefore run the risk of plagiarism. In April 2021, a group of computer scientists used a tool that identifies text generated by GPT in an effort to isolate the reason for strange phrases appearing in scientific papers. Cabanac and colleagues ran a selection of abstracts from the journal Microprocessors and Microsystems through this tool and discovered "critical flaws", such as nonsensical text and plagiarized text and images.<sup id="cite_ref-43" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-43">&#91;43&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
